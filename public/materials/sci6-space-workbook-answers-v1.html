Q6 ★ Ex-Alta 1 — CS fill-in Ex-Alta 1 was launched into orbit around Earth. It collects data on the upper atmosphere — especially on space weather events. It sends information to a ground station using a radio signal.
Designed system: Ex-Alta 1 is a designed system because it was intentionally built with specific components (sensors, transmitters, processors) to perform a defined scientific function.
Q7 ★ Orbit mechanics — fill-in A satellite moves forward fast. As it falls toward Earth, Earth's surface curves away beneath it at the same rate. The result is that the satellite keeps falling in a circle.
Ball analogy: travels farther before hitting the ground. If fast enough, never hits the ground because Earth curves away beneath it at the same rate it falls.

How you know: Jupiter reflects sunlight — it does not generate its own energy through nuclear fusion. Only stars emit light. Jupiter is not hot or dense enough to be a star. Planets only reflect; they do not emit.

Q13 ★★ Pluto reclassification — science self-correcting (a) Discovery in the early 2000s of dozens of icy Kuiper Belt objects similar in size to Pluto. If Pluto remained a planet, all similar bodies would also need to be classified as planets — leading to an inconsistent, expanding list.
(b) Three conditions: 1. Orbits the Sun; 2. Large enough to pull itself into a round/spherical shape; 3. Has cleared the neighbourhood around its orbit of other debris.
Pluto does NOT meet condition: 3 (shares the Kuiper Belt with many similar objects).
(c) Science is self-correcting. When new evidence (Kuiper Belt discoveries) demanded updating the classification system, scientists revised it — not to be arbitrary, but because a consistent, evidence-based definition is more reliable than historical familiarity. Changing a classification in response to evidence is science working as intended.
Q14 ★ Ex-Alta 1 — data pipeline matching Sensor in orbit → C (satellite measures charged particles and magnetic field changes)
Data encoded → B (converted into a signal that can travel through space)
Radio signal sent → E (radio wave travels from orbit down to Earth)
Ground station receives → D (signal decoded back into readable data)
Scientists analyze → A (draw conclusions from the data)
Similarity to other remote sensing systems: Like weather satellites and ocean sensors, Ex-Alta 1 measures conditions humans cannot observe directly and transmits that data for remote analysis.
Q15 ★ FNMI sky knowledge — two uses + observation period (a) Two uses (accept any two):
1. Navigation — star positions used for direction and wayfinding across land and water
2. Seasonal timing — star patterns marked when to plant, hunt, migrate, or hold ceremonies
Also accept: tracking celestial events for calendar purposes; reading weather patterns from sky observations.
(b) The Elder's knowledge covers a longer period (many generations vs. 10 years).
Why it matters: A longer observation period can reveal patterns, cycles, and slow changes that would not appear in a short-term dataset — especially gradual or rare phenomena.
Q16 ★★ Emit vs. reflect — error analysis What is wrong: Stars and planets do NOT both "give off light." Stars emit their own light through nuclear fusion. Planets produce no light of their own — they only reflect sunlight. The student incorrectly treated both as light sources.
Correct explanation: The fundamental difference between stars and planets is the source of light. Stars generate light through nuclear fusion. Planets are visible because they reflect sunlight toward us — like a mirror, not a light bulb. Distance is not the key distinction.

Core Questions — Q17 to Q24

# Concept / Type Answer / Marking Guide
Q17 ★ Natural vs. artificial satellites — comparison table Earth's Moon: Natural · orbits Earth · formed through natural collision process ~4.5 Ga · reflects sunlight; stabilises Earth's axial tilt; drives tides · does not produce own light
Ex-Alta 1: Human-built (artificial) · orbits Earth · launched by rocket · collects space weather data · does not produce own light
Both called satellites: Both orbit a larger body (Earth) due to gravity — "satellite" means any object in orbit, natural or artificial.
Q18 ★ Comets — tail formation and direction Why tail appears: Solar heat vaporizes ice and dust from the comet's nucleus as it nears the Sun, releasing gas and dust that glow or reflect sunlight — forming the coma and tail.
Why tail points away from Sun: Solar wind — a stream of particles constantly flowing outward from the Sun — pushes the released material away from the Sun, regardless of the comet's direction of travel. The tail is pushed, not dragged.
Q19 ★★ ISS exercise — microgravity + scientific methods (a) In microgravity, there is little gravitational force pulling on muscles and bones. Without resistance, muscles weaken and bones lose density rapidly. Exercise simulates the mechanical stress that gravity normally provides continuously on Earth.
(b) Astronauts experience extreme muscle and bone loss under conditions impossible to replicate safely on Earth. Scientists use this as a natural experiment to study how the human body responds — generating data about adaptation that could not otherwise be collected ethically.
(c) Elderly people / patients with osteoporosis / people with muscle-wasting conditions — research on maintaining muscle and bone strength without gravitational load helps develop treatments for anyone who loses mobility or bone density.
Q20 ★ Exploration tools — visual tool selection Asteroid belt surface: space probe (or rover if landed) — can fly past and image the surface remotely
Star formation under different gravity: computer model — cannot be tested physically; simulation required
Atmosphere of distant unvisited planet: space probe — can analyze atmosphere remotely during flyby or orbit
Martian soil feel and composition: rover — must physically contact and analyze soil
Accept reasonable alternatives with clear justification.
Q21 ★★ Scale models — what a model can and cannot show (a) Walking 30 metres to find Earth, or realizing Neptune is a kilometre away, gives a physical, felt sense of the vast distances that no textbook diagram can convey — students experience the scale rather than just reading about it.
(b) Type of error: inconsistent scale (planets not shown at correct relative sizes). A student might incorrectly conclude all planets are roughly the same size — missing the fact that Neptune is ~4x wider than Earth and the gas giants are vastly larger than the rocky inner planets.

Q23 ★★ Ex-Alta 1 — response to "student satellites are toys" Mark for: accurately describes what Ex-Alta 1 measures (charged particles and magnetic field changes in the upper atmosphere; space weather); correctly identifies the data pipeline (sensor → encode → radio signal → ground station → analysis); gives a specific reason small satellites are valuable (lower cost; faster build/launch; fill data gaps large agencies cannot afford; demonstrate that meaningful science is accessible).
Model: Ex-Alta 1 measures charged particles and magnetic field variations to study space weather events that affect Earth's magnetic field — this is real, publishable science. It is a computational artifact with a full data pipeline from sensor to analysis. Smaller satellites are valuable precisely because they can target specific questions quickly and cheaply, and enable student researchers to contribute to an active field.
Q24 ★ Artificial satellites — fill-in Artificial satellites are placed into orbit by humans. They stay there because of gravity, not engines. GPS satellites help you find your location; weather satellites track storms; communication satellites relay phone and internet signals; scientific satellites collect data for research (like Ex-Alta 1).
Unlike artificial satellites, natural satellites like Earth's Moon formed through a natural process — not because they were designed to be there.

Stretch Zone — S1 to S4

Stretch questions are open-ended. Mark for quality of reasoning and correct application of concepts — not for matching the model answer exactly.
# Concept / Type Answer / Marking Guide
S1 ★★ Scale + solar system objects + scientific methods (a) The conclusion is wrong in almost every way: outer planets are not "a bit farther" — on the basketball scale, Earth is 30 m from the Sun; Neptune is ~1 km. The difference between inner and outer planets is enormous, not modest. A textbook diagram cannot show this because no paper could be that wide at true scale. Award marks for specific numbers and the basketball comparison.
(b) Can legitimately use: the order/sequence of planets from the Sun; their names. Cannot use: actual distances between planets; relative sizes if not drawn to scale.
(c) Accept any creative, accurate scale model description that preserves true distances (school field, town walk-out, GPS coordinates). Must include correct relative distances and explain why the demonstration helps.
S2 ★★ Pluto reclassification + science self-correcting (a) Option B. More scientifically sound because it applies a consistent, evidence-based three-condition definition. Keeping Pluto as a planet on grounds of familiarity would require adding dozens of similarly-sized Kuiper Belt objects — leading to an inconsistent, arbitrary list. A clear definition that applies uniformly is more scientifically rigorous.
(b) Any scientific revision follows the same pattern: new evidence contradicts the existing model; scientists update the model rather than ignoring the data. Parallels: atomic model revisions; germ theory replacing miasma; continental drift accepted after plate tectonics evidence. The mechanism is the same — evidence drives revision.
(c) A dwarf planet is a body that orbits the Sun and is large enough to be spherical due to its own gravity, but has not cleared the neighbourhood around its orbit of other debris. (Must contain all three elements of the IAU definition minus condition 3 being met.)
S3 ★★ FNMI sky knowledge + exploration technologies + scientific methods (a) The Elder's knowledge spans 200+ years of observation vs. 15 years — providing a multi-generational longitudinal dataset. This can reveal slow, long-term patterns in stellar positions, seasonal shifts, or environmental changes that short-term scientific records cannot yet detect.
(b) Indigenous sky knowledge contributes: long-term observational records of star positions and seasonal patterns across centuries. Modern technology contributes: precision measurements, spectroscopy, satellite imaging, archival data. Together they could answer: how star visibility or seasonal timing has shifted over the past 200 years — combining temporal depth with measurement precision.
(c) Scientific knowledge can be produced by anyone who systematically observes the natural world. Evidence includes long-term oral and recorded observations — not only instrument readings. Both Western astronomy and Indigenous sky knowledge are empirical traditions based on sustained observation of the same phenomena. What counts as evidence should not be defined by the tradition that produced it.
S4 ★★ Ex-Alta 1 + CS designed systems + emit vs. reflect — design brief Mark for all six parts being present and internally consistent:
(a) Scientific question: specific and answerable by the proposed satellite
(b) Data collected: concrete and measurable (e.g., light intensity, magnetic field strength, temperature, particle counts)
(c) Sensor/instrument: appropriate to the data (e.g., magnetometer, spectrometer, camera, radiation detector)
(d) Data pipeline: describes encoding → radio transmission → ground station → analysis
(e) Technical challenge: realistic (e.g., power supply in eclipse, data bandwidth, thermal regulation, orbit decay)
(f) Comparison to natural satellite: correctly identifies gravity as what keeps both in orbit; identifies that artificial satellite is designed for a purpose while natural satellite formed through physical process
Deduct marks if emit/reflect is confused (e.g., satellite "emits" Earth's reflected light — the satellite's sensors detect reflected sunlight, not emitted light).
