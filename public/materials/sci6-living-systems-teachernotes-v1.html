Pre-Knowledge Check — Facilitation Guide

This runs before any instruction. Students write honest first thoughts — no notes, no wrong answers.

Show

Board heading: Pre-Knowledge Check — Living Systems
Three questions visible (or distributed on paper). No additional prompts.

Say

"Before we start this unit, I want to see what you already think — not what you know for sure, just what you believe right now. Three questions. No notes. No wrong answers. Write your honest first thought and keep the paper."

"Question 2 is the important one. We'll come back to your answer at the very end of this unit. I'm not marking this — but I want you to be honest."

Cue — ★ Write

Students write responses to all three pre-K questions.

Misconception Flag — Appears in Pre-K Check

Q2: "Where do plants get their food?" — Most students will write "soil" or "water" or "sunlight." All are wrong in an important sense. Do NOT correct this now. You want to know where students start. The correction is the entire point of Section 2 — and it will land harder if students remember being wrong here.

After collecting responses (mental note or informal tally): how many said "soil"? This is your baseline for Section 2 instruction.

Section 1 — Biotic and Abiotic: What Makes Up an Ecosystem?

Lessons 1–2 · Student notes: Section 1

Beat 1.1 — What Is an Ecosystem?

Show

Board: ECOSYSTEM = living things + non-living things + interactions
Draw a simple two-column frame: "Living" | "Non-Living" — leave blank for students to help fill.

Say

"An ecosystem is every living thing in an area — and every non-living thing — plus all the ways they interact. It's not a list, it's a system."

"An ecosystem can be as big as the boreal forest — which covers most of northern Alberta — or as small as a puddle in a schoolyard. What matters is that it has both living and non-living components, interacting."

"When I say Alberta ecosystem examples — boreal forest, grassland, wetland, prairie river, mountain alpine — I want you writing those down."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill the blank: "An ecosystem is made up of all the [living] things and all the [non-living] things in an area, along with the interactions between them." Vocabulary table: Ecosystem.

Beat 1.2 — Biotic Components and the Three Roles

Show

Board: BIOTIC = living
Three-column table: Role | What they do | Examples
Rows: Producers · Consumers · Decomposers

Say

"Biotic means living. Every organism in an ecosystem plays one of three roles — producer, consumer, or decomposer. These aren't optional — every living thing fits somewhere."

"Producers make their own food using sunlight. Consumers eat producers or other consumers. Decomposers break down dead organic matter and return nutrients to the soil."

"Write 'living' in the biotic blank. Then fill the three-role table with me."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill: "Biotic means [living]." Three-role table — producers, consumers, decomposers with examples. Vocabulary terms: Biotic, Decomposer.

Misconception Flag — Decomposers Aren't Really Part of the Ecosystem

Students often think decomposers are "outside" the system — cleanup crew, not full members. Address this directly before moving on:

Say: "There's a student answer I see every year. It goes: 'Decomposers are just nature's garbage collectors.' Here's the problem. Without decomposers, dead matter piles up, nutrients stay locked in dead tissue, and producers have nothing to grow from. Remove decomposers and the ecosystem collapses within years. They're not extras — they're load-bearing."

The student notes include a misconception check callout — point students to it and have them read it.

Beat 1.3 — Abiotic Components

Show

Board: ABIOTIC = non-living
Four-row table: Sunlight · Water · Temperature · Soil/substrate — with "how it affects the ecosystem" column blank.

Say

"Abiotic means non-living — the physical conditions. These determine which organisms can survive in a given place."

"Four major ones. Sunlight — drives photosynthesis, we'll come back to that. Water — all living cells need it. Temperature — limits which organisms can survive, and sets the growing season. Soil — provides nutrients and anchoring for plants."

"For the sunlight row, the blank is 'photosynthesis.' Don't explain it yet — just write it. We'll build that word properly in Section 2."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill: "Abiotic means [non-living]." Four-row abiotic table. Sunlight row blank: [photosynthesis]. Vocabulary: Abiotic, Habitat.

Beat 1.4 — Biodiversity and Ecosystem Types

Show

Board: More varied abiotic conditions → more biodiversity
Ecosystem types table (four rows, partially completed): Boreal forest · Wetland · Grassland · Marine

Say

"Biodiversity means the variety of life in an ecosystem. An ecosystem with more varied abiotic conditions — different light levels, water depths, temperatures — supports more biodiversity because it creates more niches for different species."

"The two blanks in your notes for this: 'more biodiversity' and 'niches' — meaning different living spaces and roles."

"The ecosystem types table you fill in partly from discussion. Wetland: high moisture, standing or slow water. Grassland: hot summers, low rainfall, open. Marine: salt water, varying depth and pressure."

Cue — ★ Write

Students complete the biodiversity blanks: [more] biodiversity / more [niches]. Ecosystem types table filled with class input. Vocabulary: Biodiversity, Ecosystem.

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Biotic vs. abiotic: Biotic = living (or was recently alive) — plants, animals, fungi, bacteria. Abiotic = non-living physical conditions — sunlight, water, temperature, soil. Two examples of each acceptable.
  2. Three roles: Producers (make own food via photosynthesis), Consumers (eat producers or other consumers), Decomposers (break down dead matter, return nutrients to soil).
  3. Decomposer misconception: Decomposers are essential — not optional. Without them, nutrients stay locked in dead tissue, producers have nothing to grow from, and the ecosystem collapses. They are full members of the ecosystem, not external cleaners.
  4. Wetland vs. grassland: Different abiotic conditions (high moisture vs. low rainfall; standing water vs. open dry soil) create different niches and support different organisms even in the same region.

Section 2 — Photosynthesis: Where Does a Plant's Food Really Come From?

Lessons 3–4 · Student notes: Section 2

Misconception Flag — Plants Get Food from Soil (CRITICAL — Address Before All Else)

This is the most persistent misconception in all of biology at this level. It is not a minor error — it fundamentally misunderstands energy flow in all ecosystems. Students will believe it going in, many will resist correcting it, and some will revert under pressure.

Strategy: Begin Section 2 by returning to the Pre-K check. Ask students to read what they wrote. Then present the Van Helmont tree evidence before explaining the mechanism. Evidence first, explanation second — this is more likely to stick than definition first.

The critical distinction: Soil provides minerals (iron, nitrogen, potassium). Minerals are not food. Food = energy-containing molecules the organism uses to grow and function. Plants manufacture their food — they do not absorb it. These are fundamentally different processes.

Beat 2.1 — The Big Misconception (Evidence First)

Show

Board: A tree weighing 1,000 kg. A pot of soil. 20 years later — the soil mass has barely changed. Where did the 1,000 kg of tree come from?
Write the numbers visibly: Start soil mass → End soil mass (barely different). Start tree mass (seed) → End tree mass (1,000 kg).

Say

"Go back to your Pre-K check. Read what you wrote for question 2. Don't change it yet."

"Here's a puzzle. A tree starts as a tiny seedling. Twenty years later, it weighs 1,000 kg. I grew it in a pot of soil. At the end of 20 years, I weigh the soil. It's barely changed."

"If plants get their food — their mass, their substance — from the soil, the soil should weigh 1,000 kg less. It doesn't. So where did the tree come from?"

"The answer is the air. Specifically, from CO₂ — carbon dioxide — which the plant pulls out of the atmosphere and builds into solid matter. This is the central fact of photosynthesis."

Cue — ⊡ Think then ★ Write

Students write the answer to the tree/soil puzzle: [from the air — specifically CO₂]. Misconception callout read aloud or silently.

Beat 2.2 — The Photosynthesis Equation

Show

Board — draw the equation frame with blanks:
CO₂ + H₂O → Glucose + O₂ (write word labels above/below)
Label: "Light energy" with a downward arrow at the top.

Say

"Photosynthesis is the process plants use to make food. Three inputs. Two outputs."

"Inputs: carbon dioxide — from the air. Water — from the soil through the roots. Light energy — from the sun, absorbed by the green parts of the plant."

"Outputs: glucose — this is the food, the energy-containing molecule the plant makes for itself. Oxygen — released as a byproduct. That oxygen is what we breathe."

"In your equation blanks: CO₂ + H₂O → glucose + oxygen. In your 'in words' blanks: the plant uses light energy, carbon dioxide, and water to make glucose and oxygen."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill the equation blanks: [CO₂] + [H₂O] → [glucose] + [oxygen]. In words: "carbon dioxide + water, using light energy, produces [glucose] and [oxygen]."

Beat 2.3 — Where Each Ingredient Comes From

Show

Board: Three-row table — CO₂ | H₂O | Light energy — with "Where the plant gets it" column blank.
Add a plant diagram: leaves (CO₂ + light), roots (water), green parts labeled.

Say

"CO₂ — from the air. Absorbed through tiny pores in the leaves called stomata."

"Water — from the soil. Absorbed through the roots and carried up the stem."

"Light energy — from the sun. Absorbed by the green parts of the plant — mainly the leaves."

"Why are leaves green? There's a substance called chlorophyll inside the leaf cells. Chlorophyll captures light and uses it to drive photosynthesis. No chlorophyll — no photosynthesis. Chlorophyll happens to be green — that's why leaves are green."

"Photosynthesis happens in the green parts — mainly in the leaves."

Cue — ★ Write

Table blanks: CO₂ from [the air] · water from [the soil/roots] · light from [the sun]. Photosynthesis happens in the [green] parts — mainly [the leaves]. Vocabulary: Photosynthesis, Chlorophyll, Glucose.

Beat 2.4 — What the Plant Does with Glucose

Show

Board: Glucose → two uses: (1) energy for growth/repair (2) stored as starch
Storage locations: roots (carrot example) · seeds (grain of rice/wheat) · fruit.

Say

"The plant makes glucose — what does it do with it? Two things. First: some is used immediately to power the plant's own growth and repair. This is what makes glucose the plant's food — it contains energy."

"Second: leftover glucose is converted to starch and stored. Where? In roots — think of a carrot, or a potato. In seeds — a grain of rice or wheat is packed with stored starch. In fruit."

"Here's the connection: that stored starch is what animals eat when they eat plants. Every carbohydrate you've ever eaten traces back to photosynthesis. You run on photosynthesis."

"Blanks: stored as 'starch.' Storage examples: roots → a carrot (or potato) · seeds → a grain of wheat (or rice)."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: energy is a source of [energy] (not just a molecule). Stored as [starch]. Root example: [carrot / potato]. Seed example: [wheat / rice]. Vocabulary: Starch.

Beat 2.5 — The Three Experiments

Show

Board: Three experiment tables — one at a time. Each has: Question | Setup | What we test for / measure | Prediction | What this tells us.

Say

"Science doesn't just tell us how photosynthesis works — it tests it. Three experiments, each testing one part of the equation."

Experiment 1 — Does light matter? "One plant in full light. One in complete darkness. After a week, we test both leaves for starch using iodine. If starch is present, photosynthesis happened. If the dark plant has no starch — light is a required ingredient. The blank: we test for starch. The dark plant shows no starch."

Experiment 2 — Does photosynthesis produce oxygen? "We use an aquatic plant — Elodea — and put it under a lamp. Bubbles form on the leaves. Those bubbles are oxygen. Count them. More light → more bubbles → more photosynthesis. The blank: oxygen. Oxygen is something photosynthesis produces, not something the plant uses."

Experiment 3 — Do only green parts photosynthesize? "A leaf with green and white/yellow sections — test both for starch. Only the green parts contain starch. The green substance (chlorophyll) is required for photosynthesis to happen."

Cue — ★ Write

Experiment 1: test for [starch] · dark plant shows [no] starch · tells us [light] is required.
Experiment 2: bubbles are [oxygen] · O₂ is something photosynthesis [produces].
Experiment 3: only [green] parts contain starch · chlorophyll is [required / necessary].

Beat 2.6 — The Photosynthesis / Respiration Misconception

Show

Board: Earth's atmosphere = 21% oxygen. Where did it come from?
Write: "billions of years of photosynthesis" as the answer after pause.

Say

"There's a second misconception that comes up in photosynthesis. Some students say: plants do photosynthesis during the day but respiration at night — they use up all the oxygen they make, so it cancels out. Plants don't actually help."

"Here's the problem with that. During daylight, a healthy plant produces far more oxygen through photosynthesis than it uses through respiration. The net result is oxygen out, CO₂ in. A plant is a net oxygen producer."

"And the big proof: Earth's atmosphere is 21% oxygen. If photosynthesis and respiration cancelled perfectly, there would be zero free oxygen. That 21% came from billions of years of plants and algae producing more oxygen than they consumed."

Cue — ★ Write

Students write in their own words why photosynthesis and respiration don't cancel: accept any response that captures "net oxygen producer" / "more O₂ out than in during daylight" / "21% atmosphere as evidence."

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Photosynthesis equation in words: Carbon dioxide (from the air) + water (from the soil/roots) + light energy (from the sun) → glucose (food for the plant) + oxygen (released into the air).
  2. Soil misconception evidence: Van Helmont tree evidence — tree grows 1,000 kg, soil mass barely changes. Tree mass came from CO₂ in the air, not from the soil. Also: plants in distilled water grow without soil. Soil provides minerals, not food.
  3. Elodea bubbles: The bubbles are oxygen — produced as a byproduct of photosynthesis. The lamp drives more photosynthesis, producing more O₂ bubbles. More light = more bubbles = evidence for light as a required ingredient.
  4. Dark room prediction: The plant would run out of glucose. Without light, photosynthesis stops. No glucose is produced. The plant uses up stored starch for energy and eventually starves.
  5. Stretch: Plants produce all the oxygen in Earth's atmosphere via photosynthesis. No plants → no photosynthesis → no oxygen production → atmospheric O₂ declines. CO₂ from respiration accumulates. Answer should reference the photosynthesis equation showing O₂ as an output.

Section 3 — Plant-Animal Relationships: Interdependence

Lessons 5–6 · Student notes: Section 3

Beat 3.1 — Beyond Food: The Full Web of Dependencies

Show

Board: Two-column table — "Animals depend on plants for…" | "Plants depend on animals for…"
Leave rows blank — build it together with class input.

Say

"Most people know animals eat plants. That's one layer. But the relationship goes much deeper in both directions."

"Animals depend on plants for food — yes. But also for oxygen — produced by photosynthesis, released into the air. For habitat — shelter, nesting sites, structure. For soil stability — plant roots hold the ground."

"Plants depend on animals too. Pollination — animals carry pollen between flowers. Seed dispersal — animals move seeds to new locations by eating fruit and depositing seeds elsewhere. CO₂ — animals breathe out CO₂ which plants use in photosynthesis. Decomposition — when animals die, decomposers return nutrients to the soil for plants."

"Fill both columns. The blanks: animals → oxygen · habitat · soil stability. Plants → pollen · seeds · CO₂ · nutrients."

Cue — ★ Write

Table blanks — Animals depend on plants for: [oxygen], habitat (structure, shelter, nesting). Plants depend on animals for: pollination ([pollen] between flowers), seed dispersal ([seeds] to new locations), CO₂ ([CO₂]), decomposition ([nutrients] to soil).

Beat 3.2 — Food Chains and Food Webs

Show

Board: Grass → Grasshopper → Meadowlark → Hawk
Write the arrow rule: "Arrow = energy flows from ← to →" (arrow points toward the consumer).
Then show a partially completed food web with blanks to fill.

Say

"A food chain shows a single pathway of energy moving from producer to consumer. Each arrow means 'is eaten by' — or more precisely, 'energy flows from this organism to this one.'"

"But real ecosystems aren't chains. Most animals eat more than one thing. Most animals are eaten by more than one predator. A food web shows the full network."

"Important: the arrow always points toward the consumer — in the direction energy is flowing. Grass arrow Grasshopper means energy moves from grass to grasshopper. Not the other direction."

"Fill the arrow blank: the arrow means 'energy flows to' or 'is eaten by.'"

Cue — ✎ Draw + ★ Write

Students fill the food web diagram — two blanks for consumers. Acceptable: any Alberta-realistic herbivore (cricket, mouse, rabbit) and predator (frog/toad, coyote, weasel). Arrow blank: [is eaten by / energy flows to]. Vocabulary: Food chain, Food web, Producer, Primary consumer, Secondary consumer, Apex predator.

Beat 3.3 — Trophic Cascades: What Happens When You Remove a Part?

Show

Board: Timeline — 1920s: wolves removed from Yellowstone → 1990s: what changed → 1995: wolves reintroduced → result
Draw three-step cascade: wolves removed → elk unchecked → vegetation destroyed → rivers erode.

Say

"In the 1920s, wolves were hunted to extinction in Yellowstone National Park. The logic at the time: fewer predators = safer for hikers and livestock. What actually happened was the opposite of safe."

"Without wolves, elk populations exploded. Elk grazed vegetation along riverbanks until it was gone. Without plant roots, riverbanks eroded — rivers began to change course. Songbird populations crashed because there were no trees left for nesting."

"In 1995, wolves were reintroduced. Within years: elk changed their behaviour, avoiding riverbanks. Vegetation recovered. Rivers stabilised. Biodiversity increased."

"This is a trophic cascade — a change at one level of the food web that ripples through the whole system. The ecosystem is a system — everything is connected."

"Your blanks: elk overgrazing caused vegetation to 'disappear' or 'be stripped.' Without vegetation, rivers began to 'erode' or 'change course.' Fewer trees meant fewer nesting sites."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: elk [stripped / overgrazed] vegetation · rivers began to [erode / change course] · fewer [trees / nesting sites]. Students write in own words why this shows ecosystems are systems, not collections. Accept any response capturing: removing one organism cascades through others; nothing is isolated.

Beat 3.4 — FNMI Sacred Plants and Traditional Ecological Knowledge

Pacing Flag — This Beat Needs Space

Lesson 6 is Sacred Plants + TEK. Do not rush this. If you have a Knowledge Keeper available (in person or recorded), this is the time to use them. If not, the four-plant table and the connection prompt are the minimum. The goal is for students to understand that traditional ecological knowledge is a valid, evidence-based scientific data source — accumulated over timescales that Western science cannot match.

Show

Display images of four plants: sage, sweetgrass, cedar, tobacco.
Board: Four-column table — Plant | Ecological role | Cultural significance.

Say

"Indigenous peoples across Canada — including many First Nations here in Alberta — have maintained detailed knowledge of plant species, their ecological roles, and their cultural significance for thousands of years. This is called traditional ecological knowledge, or TEK."

"Four sacred plants. Each one is both culturally significant and ecologically important."

"Sage: nitrogen-fixing, drought-resistant, provides insect habitat. Used in ceremony and smudging — cleansing, healing."

"Sweetgrass: stabilises wetland soil, indicator species for healthy wetlands. Braided and used in ceremony — symbol of connection and kindness."

"Cedar: large habitat provider, long-lived carbon sink, rot-resistant. Used in medicine, ceremony, and building — protective."

"Tobacco: attracts pollinators, fast-growing soil pioneer. Offered in prayer and as a gift of respect."

"Here's the key point: Knowledge Keepers track the health and distribution of these plants as an indicator of ecosystem health. This knowledge has been refined over generations — often over longer timescales than any scientific data collection can reach. TEK is not folklore. It is a valid scientific data source."

Cue — ★ Write + ⊡ Think

Students complete the four-plant table. Then respond to the connection prompt: "How might traditional ecological knowledge of sacred plant health tell you something about the whole ecosystem? What would a scientist miss if they didn't consult Knowledge Keepers?" — accept any response that captures: long timescale, place-based expertise, indicators of ecosystem change that short-term data collection can't detect.

Beat 3.5 — Human Reliance on Plants

Show

Board: Beyond food and oxygen, humans use plants for:
Five bullet blanks: clothing · building materials · medicine · climate regulation · erosion control

Say

"We've established that animals depend on plants for food and oxygen. Humans are animals — so all of that applies. But we've added layers of dependency."

"Clothing — almost all textiles come from plant fibre: cotton, linen. Or from plant-fed animals: wool from sheep eating grass."

"Building materials — wood, bamboo, plant fibres."

"Medicine — aspirin originally came from willow bark. Many cancer drugs are derived from plant alkaloids."

"Climate regulation — forests absorb CO₂ and regulate regional climate."

"Erosion control — plant roots stabilise hillsides, riverbanks, agricultural land."

"The reflection question: if you eliminated every plant-based product from your home, what would be left? Almost nothing."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: [clothing / textiles] · [building materials] · [medicine] · [climate regulation] · [erosion control]. Reflection prompt written as a sentence.

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Animals depend on plants (beyond food): Any two from: oxygen (photosynthesis byproduct), habitat (shelter, nesting, structure), soil stability (root systems), clothing (plant fibre), medicine (plant compounds).
  2. Plants depend on animals: Any two from: pollination (animals carry pollen between flowers), seed dispersal (seeds moved via eating/waste), CO₂ supply (animals exhale CO₂ for photosynthesis), nutrient return via decomposition.
  3. Deer extinction — not just wolves: A student who only sees predator-prey misses the cascade. Deer eat plants — fewer deer could let some plants recover, but deer also drive seed dispersal, pollinator patterns, soil disturbance. Other consumers that depend on deer (wolves, scavengers) decline. Removing any species disrupts the whole web — ecosystems are systems.
  4. Trophic cascade: A change at one level of the food web that ripples through the whole system. Example: wolves removed → elk unchecked → vegetation stripped → river erosion → biodiversity collapse.
  5. TEK as valid data source: Traditional ecological knowledge is accumulated over generations — timescales far longer than scientific data collection. Knowledge Keepers observe indicator species (including sacred plants) and track change in ways that short-term studies cannot. It is evidence-based, place-specific, and has been tested and refined over centuries.

Section 4 — CS Connection: Conditional Logic in Ecosystems

Lesson 7 · Student notes: Section 4

CS Connection — Conditional Logic and Abstraction

This section has two distinct CS threads. Thread 1: IF/THEN conditional logic — ecosystems operate on conditions, exactly as programs do. Thread 2: a Scratch simulation is an abstraction of a real ecosystem. It keeps the essential logic (conditional relationships) and strips out the complexity (organism biology, weather variation, random events).

Connect to the particle model from Matter: both are abstractions that are useful because they are simplified, not despite it. This cross-unit connection is explicit in the student notes — point students to it.

Beat 4.1 — If/Then Logic in a Food Web

Show

Board: The conditional table from the notes — four rows:
IF sunlight = 0 THEN photosynthesis = 0
IF photosynthesis = 0 THEN glucose = 0
IF glucose = 0 THEN producer_growth = 0
IF producer_growth = 0 THEN primary_consumers = declining

Say

"Every relationship we've described in this unit follows the same logical structure: if one thing changes, then something else changes as a result. This is conditional logic — and it's the same structure used in every computer program ever written."

"Look at the chain: no sunlight → no photosynthesis. No photosynthesis → no glucose. No glucose → producers can't grow. Producers can't grow → primary consumers start declining. Each condition triggers the next."

"This is called a cascade in ecology. In programming, it's a chain of conditional statements. Same idea — different context."

Cue — ★ Write + ⊡ Think

Students write their own conditional chain starting with "IF water = low THEN…" — at least three steps. Accept any ecologically plausible chain (e.g., low water → producers can't grow → primary consumers decline → secondary consumers decline). No single right answer.

Beat 4.2 — Scratch Simulation Preview

Show

Board (or projector — Scratch interface if available): the code loop from the notes:

forever:
  if sunlight > 0:
    increase plant_population
  if plant_population > 0:
    increase herbivore_population
  if herbivore_population > 0:
    increase predator_population

Say

"In the CS activity for this unit, you'll build a simple food web simulation in Scratch. You'll use conditional statements — if/then blocks — and a forever loop to simulate what happens over time in an ecosystem."

"Before you code, you plan. What ecosystem type? What producer? What primary consumer — herbivore? What predator? What abiotic factor will you simulate — sunlight? Water? Temperature?"

"Here's the CS connection to Matter — if you've done that unit. A Scratch simulation is an abstraction. Just like the particle model kept the essential idea — particles move faster when hot — and stripped out the real physics of molecules, your simulation keeps the essential ecosystem logic and strips out real organism biology. Both are useful because they're simplified."

Cue — ★ Write

Students complete their ecosystem planning table: ecosystem type, producer, primary consumer, secondary consumer, abiotic factor to simulate. No single right answer — any ecologically plausible combination.

Unit Synthesis — Facilitation Notes

Lesson 8 · Student notes: Synthesis page

Say

"No new content today. This is a fill-from-memory session. Close your notes. Go through all three knowledge clusters on the synthesis page — biotic/abiotic, photosynthesis, plant-animal relationships. When you've finished, open your notes and check."

"Then go back to the very first page — your Pre-Knowledge Check. Read what you wrote for question 2. Write what you know now."

"If anyone wrote 'from the soil' for question 2 at the start — that's not a failure. That's what almost every person in the world believes before they learn photosynthesis. The fact that you know you were wrong and why is exactly what science learning looks like."

Misconception Flag — Pre-K Check Revisit

The revisit of question 2 is pedagogically important. Do not skip it. Students who explicitly confront the difference between what they believed and what they now know are more likely to retain the correction. Normalise starting with a misconception — that is not a failure, it is the starting point for learning.
