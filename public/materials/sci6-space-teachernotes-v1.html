Pre-Knowledge Check — Facilitation Guide

Show

Board: Pre-Knowledge Check — Space
Open-ended prompt visible or distributed. Five lines for writing.

Say

"Write everything you think you know about the solar system. What objects are in it? How big is it? How do we learn about space? What do you already know about planets, stars, moons — anything out there?"

"No marks. No correction. Be honest. We'll return to this at the end of the unit."

Cue — ★ Write

Students write for 4–5 minutes. Watch for: who says planets produce their own light? Who thinks the solar system diagram is to scale? Who thinks Pluto's reclassification was unfair? These are your three misconceptions to address directly.

Misconception Flag — Pre-K Baseline: Three Errors to Watch For
  • "Planets shine / produce their own light" — planets reflect sunlight. Address in Lesson 2.
  • "The solar system looks like the textbook diagram — planets fairly close together" — not to scale. Address in Lesson 3.
  • "Scientists changed their minds about Pluto — you can't trust science" — self-correction is a strength. Address in Lesson 8.

This unit is described as "misconception-dense relative to its length." All three errors appear almost universally and need direct address before students will release them.

Section 1 — Solar System Components and Scale

Lessons 1–3 · Student notes: Section 1

Beat 1.1 — The Solar System and the Sun

Show

Board: SOLAR SYSTEM = the Sun + everything that orbits around it (pulled by gravity)
Sun label: STAR · produces own energy via nuclear fusion · at the CENTRE.

Say

"The solar system is the Sun and everything that orbits around it — pulled in by the Sun's gravity. Every planet, moon, asteroid, comet, and chunk of rock in our solar system is there because of the Sun's gravitational pull."

"The Sun is a star. It sits at the centre of the solar system. Unlike planets, the Sun produces its own energy through a process called nuclear fusion — hydrogen atoms combine under enormous pressure and heat to release energy. That's why the Sun glows. No fusion, no light."

"Blanks: everything that [orbits] around it · the Sun is a [star] · sits at the [centre] · produces its own [energy]."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: [orbits] · a [star] · [centre] · produces its own [energy]. Vocabulary: Solar system, Star.

Beat 1.2 — The Eight Planets

Show

Board: Planet order: Mercury → Venus → Earth → Mars → Jupiter → Saturn → Uranus → Neptune
Mnemonic: My Very Excellent Mother Just Served Us Nachos
Inner vs. outer: inner = small, rocky | outer = large, gas

Say

"Eight planets in order from the Sun: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune. Mnemonic: My Very Excellent Mother Just Served Us Nachos."

"Inner planets — Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars — are small and rocky, close to the Sun."

"Outer planets — Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune — are large and made mostly of gas. Jupiter alone is more than twice the mass of all the other planets combined."

"Blanks: inner planets are [small] and rocky · outer planets are [large] and made of gas."

Cue — ★ Write

Students copy the planet order and mnemonic. Blanks: inner = [small] rocky · outer = [large] gas. Vocabulary: Planet.

Beat 1.3 — Other Solar System Objects

Show

Board: Seven-row table — Object type | What it is | Emit or reflect? | Key feature
Star · Planet · Dwarf planet · Moon · Asteroid · Comet · Meteoroid/meteor/meteorite
Leave emit/reflect blanks and key features partially blank.

Say

"Seven types of objects to know. Let's go through each."

"Star — massive ball of gas producing energy by nuclear fusion. The Sun. Emits light."

"Planet — large body orbiting the Sun, clears its orbital neighbourhood. Earth, Jupiter. Reflects light."

"Dwarf planet — orbits the Sun, large enough to be round, but does NOT clear its orbital neighbourhood. Pluto. Reflects light. Reclassified in 2006 — we'll come back to this."

"Moon — natural satellite orbiting a planet. Earth's Moon, Titan (Saturn). Reflects light. Earth has one; some planets have dozens — Saturn has over 140."

"Asteroid — rocky body, smaller than a planet. Reflects light. Most orbit in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter."

"Comet — icy body with a long elliptical orbit. Reflects light. Develops a glowing tail as it nears the Sun — ice turns to gas."

"Meteoroid — small rocky or metallic object in space. Becomes a meteor when it enters Earth's atmosphere (the streak of light). A meteorite if it lands on Earth's surface."

Cue — ★ Write

Table completed with class. Emit/reflect blanks: Star → [emits] · all others → [reflects].
Asteroid belt: between [Mars] and [Jupiter]. Comet: develops a glowing [tail] near Sun. Meteoroid → meteor in atmosphere → [meteorite] if lands. Vocabulary: Dwarf planet, Moon, Asteroid, Comet, Meteoroid/meteor/meteorite, Emit, Reflect.

Beat 1.4 — Emit vs. Reflect Light (Lesson 2)

Show

Board: Two-column heading: EMIT | REFLECT
Emit: "produce and send out light — requires nuclear fusion"
Reflect: "bounce back light from another source — no energy produced"
Draw: Sun → light hitting Venus → light bouncing to human eye

Say

"This is one of the most important ideas in this unit."

"Emit means to produce and send out light. The Sun does this. It generates its own energy through nuclear fusion. Without the Sun, there would be no light in the solar system."

"Reflect means to bounce back light that came from somewhere else. Planets, moons, and asteroids do this. They are visible because the Sun's light hits them and bounces toward our eyes."

"This is why you can see Venus at dusk. Venus is not on fire. It is not producing light. It is a rocky planet covered in clouds — those clouds are very good at reflecting sunlight back toward Earth. What you see is sunlight bouncing off cloud tops."

"Same for Mars, Jupiter, Saturn — all the planets you can see. All reflected sunlight."

"Blanks: emit = produce and send out [light] · reflect = bounce back [light] that came from somewhere else · planets are visible because they are [reflecting] sunlight."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: emit = produce and send out [light] · reflect = bounce back [light] from somewhere else · planets are [reflecting] sunlight.

Misconception Flag — Planets Produce Their Own Light

This misconception is near-universal. Students conflate "visible at night" with "produces light." The correction is conceptually simple but requires the emit/reflect distinction to be explicit.

The anchor example: Venus at dusk. It's the brightest object in the evening sky — brighter than any star. That brightness comes from how well its clouds reflect sunlight, not from any internal energy source. The planet itself is dark — we only see it because of reflected light.

Point students to the misconception callout in their notes — have them read it, then confirm understanding before moving to scale.

Beat 1.5 — Scale: Why Diagrams Lie (Lesson 3)

Show

Physical demonstration: hold up a basketball.
Board: "If the Sun = this basketball (24 cm), then Earth = a pinhead 30 metres away. Neptune = 1 km away."
Walk to the back of the room (or door of the hall) to represent Earth's position.

Say

"Here is something important: every diagram of the solar system you have ever seen is NOT to scale. I want you to write that down."

"Textbook diagrams show planets looking large and close together. The real solar system is almost entirely empty space."

"Here's what the real scale looks like. If the Sun were a basketball — hold this up — the Earth would be a pinhead roughly 30 metres away. That's outside this classroom."

"Neptune — the outermost planet — would be about 1 kilometre away. On that scale, every planet would be smaller than a grain of sand."

"Why do textbooks draw it wrong? Because if they drew it to scale, the diagram would need to be kilometres long to show all the planets. The order of the planets is correct in textbook diagrams — the distances are not."

"Scale models are tools scientists use to understand complex systems. But you must always know whether a model is to scale — because a non-scale model can give you wrong ideas about distances and sizes."

"Blanks: NOT [to scale] · almost entirely [empty] space · understand [complex] systems · cannot tell you about [distance] or [actual] size."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: NOT [to scale] · almost entirely [empty] space · understand [complex] systems · cannot tell about [distance] or [actual] size.

Misconception Flag — Textbook Solar System Diagram Shows Actual Scale

The basketball demonstration is the most effective tool for this misconception. Use it physically — don't just describe it. Walk to a position 30 metres away from where you're holding the basketball. Students need to feel the distance, not just hear about it.

If the room isn't 30 metres, use the hallway, or step outside. The point is that the Earth position is somewhere students normally think of as "far away" — not a few centimetres on a page.

Beat 1.6 — Indigenous Sky Knowledge

Pacing Flag — Indigenous Sky Knowledge Needs a Genuine Beat

This is not a one-liner. Cree, Blackfoot, and many other First Nations have rich, systematic sky observation traditions used for navigation, seasonal timing, planting, and harvesting. This is long-term scientific observation — developed over generations of careful watching and record-keeping.

The framing: Western astronomy and Indigenous sky knowledge are two knowledge systems that have observed the same sky. Each produces frameworks that are meaningful, purposeful, and grounded in sustained attention. They are not in competition — they are parallel observation traditions.

Show

Board: Indigenous Sky Knowledge — Cree, Blackfoot, and other First Nations
Uses: navigation · tracking seasons · timing planting/harvesting · understanding yearly cycles.

Say

"Before telescopes, before satellites, Indigenous peoples across what is now Canada were observing and documenting the sky with great precision. Cree, Blackfoot, and many other First Nations have names and stories for stars, constellations, and seasonal sky patterns."

"This was not casual stargazing. It was systematic and practical — used for navigation (no GPS), tracking seasons, timing planting and harvesting, and understanding the year's cycles. It was a knowledge system developed over generations of careful watching and record-keeping."

"Western astronomy and Indigenous sky knowledge are two knowledge systems that have observed the same sky. Each has produced frameworks that are meaningful, purposeful, and grounded in sustained attention to the world above."

Cue — ★ Write

Students read the Indigenous sky knowledge paragraph in their notes. Connection prompt: students write which night-sky lights they think are stars vs. planets and how they could tell the difference. Accept any reasoning that engages with the twinkling-vs-steady distinction (stars twinkle because light bends through atmosphere; planets are closer and appear steadier).

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Four objects other than Sun and planets: Any four from: moon / natural satellite · asteroid · comet · meteoroid · meteor · meteorite · dwarf planet (Pluto).
  2. Asteroid belt: A region of space containing many rocky bodies (asteroids) orbiting the Sun. Located between Mars and Jupiter.
  3. Comet tail develops near Sun: A comet is made of ice and rock. Far from the Sun, the ice is frozen — no tail. As it approaches the Sun, heat causes the ice to turn directly into gas (sublimation). Solar wind pushes this gas away from the comet, forming a visible glowing tail pointing away from the Sun.
  4. Moon emits or reflects: Reflects. The Moon has no internal energy source and does not produce its own light. It is visible because sunlight hits its surface and reflects toward Earth. This is why the Moon appears to change shape (phases) — we see different portions of the lit surface as the Moon orbits Earth.
  5. Scale error + basketball comparison: The error: the student is using the textbook diagram as a model of actual distances — but it is not to scale. It only shows order, not distance or size. Basketball comparison: if the Sun = basketball (24 cm), Earth = a pinhead 30 metres away, Neptune = 1 km away. In the textbook diagram, these would appear centimetres apart. The real solar system is almost entirely empty space.

Beat 2.1 — Exploration Technology Overview

Show

Board: Seven-row table — Tool | What it does | Example
Optical telescope · Radio telescope · Space-based telescope · Space probe · Rover · Crewed spacecraft · Computer modelling

Say

"Space is extremely far away. Most of it is impossible for humans to visit directly. Scientists use a range of technologies — each designed to answer a different kind of question."

"Optical telescope: collects visible light from distant objects. Used from Earth. Backyard telescopes to large observatories."

"Radio telescope: detects radio waves from space — can 'see' through dust and gas that blocks visible light. Arecibo, now defunct, was the largest. Used to study pulsars, distant galaxies."

"Space-based telescope: orbits Earth above the atmosphere. No atmospheric blur — sharper images. Hubble Space Telescope. James Webb — launched 2021, sees in infrared farther back in time than Hubble."

"Space probe: robotic spacecraft, no crew. Travels directly to other bodies — flies by, orbits, or lands. Voyager 1 is now beyond the solar system. New Horizons flew by Pluto in 2015."

"Rover: lands on a surface and drives across it. Takes samples, photographs, drills. Curiosity and Perseverance are currently on Mars."

"Crewed spacecraft / ISS: carries human astronauts. Allows direct human work. Apollo Moon missions. ISS — in continuous operation since 2000."

"Computer modelling: uses software to simulate conditions we cannot directly observe. Inside a star. Billions of years into the past. Tests hypotheses mathematically."

Beat 2.3 — Ex-Alta 1: Alberta's Satellite (Lesson 5)

CS Connection — Ex-Alta 1 as a Computational Artifact

Ex-Alta 1 is the primary CS connection for this unit. It is a designed system — a CubeSat built by University of Alberta students — that solves a specific problem: extending human sensing into the upper atmosphere.

The data pipeline is the key CS concept: sensor in a hard-to-reach place → data encoded → transmitted by radio signal → ground station decodes → scientists interpret. The satellite does not think. It senses, encodes, and transmits. The meaning comes from the scientists interpreting the output. This is the same logic as any remote sensing system — from weather stations to seismographs.

Blank in the CS connection callout: purpose = designed vs. natural. Natural satellites arise from physical processes; artificial satellites are engineered to solve a problem.

Show

Board: Ex-Alta 1 — CubeSat built by University of Alberta students
Data pipeline: Upper atmosphere → sensor → encoded data → radio signal → ground station → decoded data → scientists interpret.
Label each stage.

Say

"Ex-Alta 1 is a small satellite called a CubeSat — about the size of a shoebox. It was designed and built by students at the University of Alberta and launched into orbit in 2017."

"It orbits Earth and collects data on the upper atmosphere — specifically on space weather events that affect Earth's magnetic field. Solar storms can disrupt GPS, radio communication, and power grids."

"Why does this matter? Two reasons. First: satellite technology is not only for large space agencies. Students built something that serves a real scientific purpose. Second: it is a computational artifact — a human-built system designed to collect data from an environment we cannot directly access."

"Here's the data pipeline. Ex-Alta 1 has sensors that detect charged particles and magnetic field changes. That data is encoded — turned into a digital signal. Transmitted by radio to a ground station. The ground station decodes it. Scientists interpret the output."

"The satellite does not think. It senses, encodes, and transmits. The meaning comes from the scientists on the ground. This is the same logic behind every remote sensing system — weather station, seismograph, atmospheric sensor."

"Blanks: launched in [2017] · orbits [Earth] · collects data on [upper atmosphere]."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: launched in [2017] · orbits [Earth] · collects data on [upper atmosphere / space weather]. Connection prompt: students design a hypothetical space probe — question, what the probe needs to do. No single answer.

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Probe vs. rover — two differences: A probe travels to a body and may orbit, fly by, or land — but cannot move around on the surface. A rover lands on a surface and drives across it, allowing local investigation (sampling, drilling, close-up photography). Also: rovers are generally slower and limited to one body; probes can travel to multiple bodies or interstellar space.
  2. Space-based vs. ground telescopes: Earth's atmosphere blurs and blocks some wavelengths of light. A space-based telescope (like Hubble) orbits above the atmosphere, eliminating atmospheric distortion and allowing sharper images and access to wavelengths (UV, infrared) that the atmosphere absorbs.
  3. Voyager — what type of tool: Space probe.
  4. Mars soil composition — which tool: Rover. A telescope can only observe from far away — not detailed enough for soil composition. A probe could land but not investigate the surface. A rover (like Curiosity or Perseverance) can drive to locations, collect samples, and analyse soil composition directly using onboard instruments.
  5. Flaw in "just model everything": Computer models depend on existing data — they are built from the measurements and observations we already have. A model of Mars' atmosphere is only as reliable as the atmospheric data it was built with. If we never sent a probe or rover to Mars, the model would be built on assumptions, not measurements. Direct measurement provides the data that makes models reliable. Models and direct measurement are complementary — not substitutes.

"Natural and artificial satellites are different in origin but similar in physics — both stay in orbit because of the same forces."

Beat 3.2 — How Orbits Work

Show

Board: Draw the orbit logic:
Ball thrown horizontally → curves down, hits ground.
Ball thrown faster → travels farther before hitting.
Ball thrown fast enough → Earth curves away at same rate ball falls → orbit.
Caption: "Orbit = falling forward fast enough that Earth curves away beneath you."

Say

"How does a satellite stay in orbit without engines constantly running? This is one of the most surprising ideas in physics."

"A satellite moves forward so fast that as it falls toward Earth, Earth's surface curves away beneath it at the same rate. The result: it keeps falling in a circle. It never hits the ground."

"Think of it with a ball. Throw a ball horizontally — it curves down and hits the ground. Throw it faster — it travels farther before hitting. Throw it fast enough, and the curve of Earth falls away at the same rate the ball drops. The ball never hits the ground. That's an orbit."

"Satellites do not hover in place above Earth. They are always falling — they just keep missing the curve of Earth."

"Blanks: moves [forward] so fast · Earth's surface [curves] away at same rate · falling in a [circle] · do not [hover] in place · keep [missing] the curve of Earth."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: moves [forward] so fast · Earth's surface [curves] away · falling in a [circle] · do not [hover] · keep [missing] the curve of Earth.

Beat 3.3 — The ISS and Challenges of Living in Space (Lesson 7)

Show

Board: ISS stats: ~400 km above Earth · continuously occupied since 2000 · Canadarm2.
Six-row challenges table: Air · Radiation · Microgravity · Temperature · Food/water · Psychology

Say

"The ISS is a research laboratory orbiting about 400 km above Earth. It has been continuously occupied since the year 2000. Canada's contribution is Canadarm2 — a robotic arm used to move equipment and dock spacecraft."

"Humans live there for months at a time. The challenges are significant."

"No breathable air: space is a vacuum. All air must be brought from Earth or generated on board. The ISS generates oxygen by splitting water molecules."

"Radiation: Earth's magnetic field and atmosphere protect us. In space, astronauts are exposed to high radiation from the Sun and cosmic rays. Long-duration missions increase cancer risk."

"Microgravity: in orbit, everything feels weightless. Over time, muscles weaken and bones lose density because they are not working against gravity. Astronauts exercise 2+ hours per day to slow this."

"Extreme temperatures: in sunlight, surfaces reach over 120°C. In shadow, below -150°C. Spacecraft must manage these swings constantly."

"Food and water: everything must be brought from Earth or recycled. Water on the ISS is almost entirely recycled — including urine."

"Psychological: isolated, small space, far from family, no way to leave. Mental health monitoring is essential on long-duration missions."

"Blanks: brought from Earth or [generated] on board · [high] radiation · muscles [weaken] and bones lose density · [manage] temperature swings · [brought] from Earth or recycled · almost entirely [recycled] · [mental] health."

Beat 3.4 — Pluto's Reclassification: Science Is Self-Correcting (Lesson 8)

Misconception Flag — "Pluto's Reclassification Was Arbitrary / Unfair"

This is the teachable moment this unit is built around. The Pluto discussion is the clearest real-world example in the course of science being self-correcting: new evidence → updated classification. Use it every time a student says "scientists just make it up" or "they keep changing their minds."

The three conditions: (1) orbits the Sun (2) pulled itself into roughly spherical shape by gravity (3) cleared its orbital neighbourhood. Pluto fails condition 3 because the Kuiper Belt is full of similar-sized objects. Pluto is one of many, not a unique planet.

The key framing: "Changing a classification when evidence demands it is not failure. It is exactly what science is supposed to do."

Show

Board: THREE CONDITIONS TO BE A FULL PLANET:
1. Orbits the Sun ✓ (Pluto passes)
2. Pulled itself into roughly spherical shape ✓ (Pluto passes)
3. Cleared its orbital neighbourhood ✗ (Pluto fails — Kuiper Belt)
Draw: Pluto among many similar Kuiper Belt objects.

Say

"Pluto was called a planet from its discovery in 1930 until 2006. When it was reclassified, some people felt scientists had made a mistake or were being unfair to Pluto."

"This misunderstands how science works."

"Scientists reclassified Pluto because they had new data. When powerful telescopes revealed many similar-sized icy bodies in the Kuiper Belt — the region beyond Neptune — it became clear that Pluto was not a unique planet. It was one of a large group of similar objects. If Pluto was a planet, so were all of them — which would mean adding dozens of new planets."

"The old definition of 'planet' didn't fit this new evidence. So the International Astronomical Union created a clear three-condition definition and applied it consistently. Pluto meets conditions 1 and 2 — but not condition 3."

"Changing a classification when evidence demands it is not failure. It is exactly what science is supposed to do."

"This is what 'science is self-correcting' means. New data arrives. The understanding updates. That is not weakness — it is the whole point."

"Blanks: scientists had [new] data · old definition didn't [fit] new evidence · science is [supposed] to do this."

Cue — ★ Write

Pluto three conditions filled in: (1) orbit the [Sun] (2) roughly [spherical] shape (3) [cleared] orbital neighbourhood. Pluto fails condition [3]. Reclassified because: new evidence was more [consistent / accurate]. Connection prompt: students compare Pluto reclassification to a personal example of changing one's mind with new information.

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Natural + artificial satellite examples: Natural: Earth's Moon (or any planetary moon). Artificial: GPS satellite / weather satellite / ISS / Ex-Alta 1 / Hubble Space Telescope.
  2. What keeps GPS satellites in orbit: They are moving forward so fast that as they fall toward Earth, Earth's surface curves away beneath them at the same rate. The result is a continuous fall in a circle — an orbit. No engine needed to maintain orbit; an engine would only be needed to change or correct the orbit.
  3. Three ISS challenges: Any three from: no breathable air (vacuum, must generate/bring air) · high radiation (no magnetic field/atmosphere protection) · microgravity (muscles weaken, bones lose density) · extreme temperature swings (120°C to -150°C) · food and water must be brought/recycled · psychological isolation.
  4. Condition Pluto fails: Condition 3 — Pluto has not cleared its orbital neighbourhood of other debris. The Kuiper Belt contains many similar-sized icy objects in the same region of space as Pluto.
  5. Response to "scientists changed the rules": The reclassification was not arbitrary — it was driven by new evidence (discovery of many Kuiper Belt objects similar to Pluto). The old definition was vague and didn't fit the new evidence. The IAU created a clear three-condition definition and applied it consistently. Pluto doesn't meet condition 3 — and neither do many other Kuiper Belt objects. Updating a classification when evidence demands it is exactly how science is supposed to work — it is a sign of healthy science, not failure. Science that never updates its understanding despite new evidence would actually be less trustworthy.
