Section 2 — Food Chains, Webs, and Interdependence (Q11–Q20)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q11 Food chain arrangement · ordering + fill-in grass → vole → coyote → hawk (arrows point from food to eater). Producer = grass. Top predator = hawk.
Q12 Coyotes removed · cascade fill-in Vole population: increase (no predator) · Grass: decrease (eaten by more voles) · Hawks: decrease (less food — fewer voles for them)
Q13 Photosynthesis/respiration — evaluate · word bank + fix Kenji = Correct. Priya = Partially correct. Fix: Plants do respiration continuously — day and night. During the day they also do photosynthesis at the same time. They do not "switch off" photosynthesis at night — they simply cannot do it without light. Accept: plants do both processes simultaneously when light is available.
Q14 Wolves in Yellowstone — trophic cascade · chain fill-in Wolves returned → elk retreated (moved away from rivers) → riverside plants grew back → riverbanks held in place by roots → rivers became more stable. This is called a trophic cascade.
Q15 Human changes to ecosystems · table fill-in Accept any two valid pairings from the word bank. Examples: deforestation → habitat loss / species decline · pollution → species decline · overfishing → species decline · invasive species → outcompetes native species.
Q16 Invasive species — definition + example · word bank + explain An invasive species is a species introduced to an ecosystem where it did not evolve. Example: zebra mussel OR purple loosestrife. It harms the ecosystem because it has no natural predators and outcompetes native species.
Q17 Symbiosis types · table fill-in mutualism (both benefit — bees/flowers) · commensalism (one benefits, one unaffected — bird in tree) · parasitism (one benefits, one harmed — tapeworm)
Q18 Interdependence · word bank + example Interdependence means organisms depend on / affect each other in the ecosystem. They are connected. Example: wolves and elk OR bees and flowers. Accept any well-described example of two organisms depending on each other.
Q19 Nitrogen back to soil · word bank (choose 2) Any two from: decomposers break down dead organisms · dead organisms release nutrients as they decay · animal waste adds nitrogen · nitrogen-fixing bacteria convert atmospheric nitrogen · lightning fixes atmospheric nitrogen.
Q20 Abiotic changes → biotic effects · table fill-in Temperature drops below freezing → plants die OR animals migrate · River excess nutrients → algae blooms · Spring soil warms → seeds sprout. Accept any WB pairing that correctly connects the abiotic change to a logical biotic effect.

Section 3 — Applying and Evaluating (Q21–Q30)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q21 Habitat needs · word bank (choose 3) Any three from: food · water · shelter · space · suitable temperature · oxygen.
Q22 Wetland food web · draw + fill-in Drawing should include arrows from cattails (producer) to water snail and/or muskrat, then to duck and/or great blue heron. Bacteria = decomposer (off to the side). Producer = cattails. Top predator = great blue heron. Accept any web using ≥4 WB organisms with correctly directed arrows.
Q23 Tree mass from air · word bank + explain Tree mass came mostly from: carbon dioxide (from the air). How: via photosynthesis — the tree converts CO₂ + water + sunlight into glucose, which builds plant tissues. The mass is not from the soil.
Q24 Biodiversity · word bank + explain Biodiversity means the variety of species in an ecosystem. High biodiversity is important because it makes the ecosystem more stable — if one species is lost, others can fill its role. Accept: more biodiversity = more backup pathways = harder to collapse.
Q25 Goldfish released — invasive species · word bank + explain The goldfish could become an invasive species. Because: it has no predators in the lake, so it will compete for food with native species and harm the ecosystem. Accept any response using "invasive species" and explaining the lack of population control.
Q26 Drought cascade · chain fill-in Drought → grass dies → grasshoppers lose food and starve → rabbits lose food and starve (or migrate) → hawks have less prey and their population decreases.
Q27 Protect biodiversity in Alberta · word bank (choose 2) Any two from: protected areas · reduce pollution · sustainable farming · restore habitats · control invasive species · reduce deforestation.
Q28 Sweetgrass + wetland loss — TEK · word bank + explain When the wetland is drained: sweetgrass disappears (habitat loss). This also affects: Indigenous practices / cultural knowledge / medicinal plants that depend on sweetgrass. Accept: ecosystem loss and cultural loss are directly connected.
Q29 Pesticide investigation design · word bank Measure: bird population (count) · Compare: areas with pesticide vs without pesticide · Look for: changes over time — is bird population lower near farms using pesticide? Accept any response using at least two WB terms correctly.
Q30 Invasive insect cascade (aspen trees) · chain fill-in Insect → aspen trees die → woodpeckers lose nesting habitat / food and decline → moose lose food source and decline → other predators (e.g., wolves) have less prey → food web disruption / habitat loss cascades through ecosystem. Award marks for naming at least two species with logical effects.

Stretch Zone (S1–S5)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
S1 Lake food web + toxin effect · open response Food web: algae → water fleas → small fish → large fish → heron. If small fish are killed: water flea population booms (no predator to control them); large fish crash (no prey); heron decline (no fish to eat). Award marks for drawing a web with ≥4 organisms and correctly predicting at least two cascade effects.
S2 Biodiversity and resilience · open response The 5-species ecosystem is more likely to collapse. When one of 5 species is lost, 20% of the biodiversity is gone — energy pathways are limited. In the 50-species ecosystem, other species can fill the role of the lost one (redundancy). Higher biodiversity = more backup pathways = greater resilience.
S3 Why energy can't cycle · open response Energy is lost as heat at every step of a food chain. There is less energy available at each higher level (≈10% passes to the next level). Because energy is always leaking out as heat, the system cannot sustain itself without a constant new input of energy from the sun. Energy flows in one direction — it cannot be recycled.
S4 Controlled burns and biodiversity · open response Controlled burns remove accumulated dead underbrush → reduces risk of catastrophic wildfire → allows sunlight to reach the ground → promotes new plant growth → creates a mosaic of habitats (open areas + forest edges) → supports more species (more niches available) → higher biodiversity. Accept any response capturing: burn → renewal → more habitat variety → more species.
S5 Balanced aquarium design · open response Must include: producers (aquatic plants — photosynthesize, produce O₂), primary consumers (snails or small fish — eat algae/plants), decomposers (bacteria — break down waste, recycle nutrients). Needs a light source (external). Award marks for: naming each organism, stating its role, and explaining how the system stays balanced (producers → consumers → decomposers → back to producers).
