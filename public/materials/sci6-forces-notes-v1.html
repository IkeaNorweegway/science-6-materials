Misconception

"Friction isn't really a force — it just slows things down."

Friction IS a force. Like all forces, it has a direction (opposite to motion) and a size (measured in Newtons). It acts between surfaces whenever one surface moves — or tries to move — against another. You can measure friction with a spring scale. The reason it feels invisible is that it acts in the opposite direction to what we're already focused on, but it is just as real as any push or pull.

When a force acts on a material, the material may change shape — this is called  . There are two types:

Plastic deformation

The elastic limit

Every material has an elastic limit — the maximum force it can experience before elastic deformation becomes plastic deformation.

  • Below the elastic limit: the material springs   (elastic)
  • Above the elastic limit: the material stays   (plastic)

A paper clip bent slightly → springs back (below elastic limit).
A paper clip bent sharply → stays bent (  the elastic limit).

Newton's Third Law: When one object exerts a force on a second object, the second object exerts a force on the first object that is   in size and   in direction.

Shorter version: For every  , there is an equal and opposite  .

The three properties of every action-reaction pair

  1. Equal in  
  2. Opposite in  
  3. Acting on   objects

All three must be true. If the forces act on the same object — they are not an action-reaction pair.

Examples

SituationAction forceReaction force
You jump off the ground You push   on the floor Floor pushes   on you
You row a boat Paddle pushes   on the water Water pushes   on the paddle
You lean on a wall You push   on the wall Wall pushes   on you
Misconception 1

"The heavier object always pushes harder in a collision."

When a truck and a bicycle collide, the force the truck exerts on the bicycle is exactly equal to the force the bicycle exerts on the truck. Newton's Third Law does not have exceptions for size or mass. The difference in what happens to each object (the bicycle moves more) is because of the difference in mass — a small mass responds more to the same force. The forces are equal; the effects are not.

Misconception 2

"Action and reaction forces cancel out, so nothing ever moves."

Action-reaction forces cannot cancel each other because they act on different objects. Forces only cancel when they act on the same object. When you jump, the ground pushes up on you and you push down on the ground — these are two different objects. The force on you (from the ground) and the force on the ground (from you) have nothing to cancel with, because each object's motion is governed by forces acting on that object only.
