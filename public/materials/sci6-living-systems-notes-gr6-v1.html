What Is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is made up of all the   things and all the   things in an area, along with the interactions between them.

Ecosystems are not just lists of plants and animals. They are systems — every part affects every other part.

An ecosystem can be as large as a boreal forest or as small as a puddle. What matters is that it includes both living and non-living components interacting together.

Alberta ecosystem examples: boreal forest · grassland · wetland (bog, marsh, fen) · mountain alpine · prairie river

Biotic Components (Living)

Biotic means  .

Biotic components include organisms — anything that is alive or was recently alive.

Three roles in any ecosystem:

RoleWhat they doExamples
ProducersMake their own food using sunlight — the base of the food webGreen plants, algae, phytoplankton
ConsumersGet energy by eating producers or other consumersInsects, birds, deer, wolves, humans
DecomposersBreak down dead organic matter and return nutrients to the soilFungi, bacteria, earthworms
Misconception

"Decomposers are just nature's garbage collectors — they aren't really part of the ecosystem."

Decomposers are essential. Without them, dead matter would pile up, nutrients would stay locked in dead tissue, and producers would have nothing to grow from. Remove decomposers and the ecosystem collapses within years.

Connection Prompt

Think of the last time you saw something rotting — a log, a piece of fruit, leaves on the ground. That was decomposers at work. What would happen if that process stopped?

The Big Misconception — Address This First

Before we explain anything — go back to your Pre-Knowledge Check answer about where plants get their food.

Most people answer: from the soil or from water or from sunlight. All of these answers are wrong in an important way.

Misconception

"Plants get their food from the soil (by absorbing nutrients through their roots)."

Soil provides minerals — but minerals are not food. Food means energy-containing molecules the organism uses to grow and function. Plants make their own food. They do not absorb it from anywhere. The food is manufactured inside the plant, using light as the energy source. The soil is the mineral supply — not the food supply. These are completely different things.

The evidence: A tree weighing 1,000 kg grows in a pot with a small amount of soil. After 20 years, the soil has barely changed in mass. Where did the 1,000 kg of tree come from?

Answer:

(Hint: it came from the air — specifically, from CO₂)

Why are leaves green? Leaves contain a substance called chlorophyll. Chlorophyll is what captures light for photosynthesis — and it happens to be green. No chlorophyll, no photosynthesis.

What the Plant Does with Glucose

Glucose is the food the plant makes for itself. It has two uses:

  • Energy: Some glucose is used immediately to power the plant's growth and repair. Glucose is a source of  , not just a molecule.
  • Storage: Leftover glucose is converted to   and stored in roots, seeds, and fruit.
This stored starch is what animals — including humans — eat when they eat plants. Every carbohydrate you eat traces back to photosynthesis.

Misconception

"Photosynthesis and respiration are opposites — they cancel each other out, so plants don't really contribute to oxygen in the air."

During daylight, a healthy plant produces far more oxygen through photosynthesis than it uses through respiration. The net result is oxygen out and CO₂ in. A healthy plant is a net oxygen producer. Earth's atmosphere is 21% oxygen — that oxygen came from billions of years of photosynthesis. If the two processes cancelled perfectly, there would be no free oxygen at all.

The missing layer: Animals depend on plants for the oxygen they breathe. Before photosynthetic organisms began releasing oxygen ~2.5 billion years ago, Earth's atmosphere had almost no free oxygen. Every breath you take is a product of photosynthesis.

Food Chains and Food Webs

A food chain shows a single pathway of energy transfer from producer to consumer.

Example food chain — Alberta grassland:

Grass → Grasshopper → Meadowlark → Hawk

Each arrow means: " "

The arrow direction matters. It always points toward the consumer — in the direction that energy is flowing.

Why food webs are more accurate than food chains:

Real ecosystems are not simple chains. Most organisms eat more than one thing and are eaten by more than one thing. A food web shows the full network of feeding relationships.

What Happens When You Remove a Part?

Ecosystems are systems — connected webs, not independent lists. Removing one organism can cascade through the whole system.

Case study — wolves in Yellowstone:
Wolves were removed from Yellowstone National Park in the 1920s. By the 1990s:

  • Elk populations grew unchecked and   vegetation along riverbanks
  • Without vegetation, rivers began to   because roots weren't holding the soil
  • Songbird populations declined because there were fewer   for nesting
  • When wolves were reintroduced in 1995, vegetation returned, rivers stabilised, and biodiversity increased

This is called a trophic cascade — a change at one level of the food web that ripples through the whole system.

If/Then Logic in a Food Web

Ecosystems follow conditional logic — if one thing changes, then something else changes as a result. This is the same structure used in computer programming.

Ecosystem eventConditional structure
If no sunlight → photosynthesis cannot occurIF sunlight = 0 THEN photosynthesis = 0
If photosynthesis stops → glucose is not producedIF photosynthesis = 0 THEN glucose = 0
If glucose not produced → producers cannot growIF glucose = 0 THEN producer_growth = 0
If producers cannot grow → primary consumers starveIF producer_growth = 0 THEN primary_consumers = declining
