Section 1 — Particles

Everything around you is matter.

Your desk is matter. Water is matter. Air is matter.

All matter is made of tiny pieces called particles.

Particles are too small to see. But they are always there.

Section 2 — Solids, Liquids, and Gases

Matter can be a solid, a liquid, or a gas.

The difference is how particles move and how far apart they are.

Solid

  • Particles are close together.
  • Particles vibrate but do not move past each other.
  • Solids hold their shape. They do not flow.

Liquid

  • Particles are close but can slide past each other.
  • Liquids flow and take the shape of their container.
  • The amount of liquid stays the same.

Gas

  • Particles are far apart and move fast.
  • Gas fills all the space in its container.
  • Gas can be squished (compressed).

Phase changes

Matter can change from one state to another. This is called a phase change.

Heat makes particles move faster. They spread apart. Solid becomes liquid. Liquid becomes gas.

Cold makes particles slow down. They move closer. Gas becomes liquid. Liquid becomes solid.

Word bank: melting  ·  freezing  ·  evaporating  ·  condensing
What happensName
Solid → Liquid (heating)
Liquid → Solid (cooling)
Liquid → Gas (heating)
Gas → Liquid (cooling)
🔑 Word to know
Solid — holds its shape. Particles are close and vibrate in place.
Examples: ice, rock, wood.
🔑 Word to know
Liquid — flows and takes the shape of its container.
Examples: water, juice, oil.
🔑 Word to know
Gas — spreads out to fill all available space.
Examples: air, steam, oxygen.
🔑 Word to know
Phase change — when matter changes from one state to another.
Ice melting into water is a phase change.
⚠️ Watch out
When matter changes state, the mass does not change.
Ice and water are the same stuff. The same amount. Just different states.
💡 Remember
Heat = particles spread = solid → liquid → gas.
Cold = particles close = gas → liquid → solid.

Section 3 — Thermometers

A thermometer measures temperature.

Most thermometers have liquid inside a glass tube.

When it gets hotter, the liquid expands. It takes up more space. It rises up the tube.

When it gets colder, the liquid contracts. It takes up less space. It drops down the tube.

The Celsius scale

We measure temperature in degrees Celsius (°C).

Section 4 — Expanding, Shrinking, and the Strange Case of Water

When most things get hot, they get bigger. This is called expansion.

When most things get cold, they get smaller. This is called contraction.

This happens because of particles. Hot particles spread out. Cold particles move together.

Real-life examples

  • Metal bridges have gaps in them. The metal expands in summer. The gaps stop it from buckling.
  • Power lines sag in summer. They tighten in winter.
  • Jar lids are easier to open under hot water. The metal expands.

Water is different

Most things shrink when they get cold. Water does something strange.

When water freezes, it expands. It gets bigger.

This means ice takes up more space than the same amount of liquid water.

Ice is less dense than water. That is why ice floats.

The liquid in a thermometer expands when warm.
🔑 Word to know
Contraction — when something gets smaller because it was cooled.
Metal contracts in cold weather.

Dense — how much is packed into a space. More stuff = more dense.
Ice is less dense than water, so it floats.

Most things shrink when they freeze. Water does NOT.
Water expands when it freezes. That is what makes ice float. This is called water's anomaly — it is the exception, not the rule.
