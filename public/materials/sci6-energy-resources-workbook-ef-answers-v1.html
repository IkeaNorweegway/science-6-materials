Energy Resources — Easy Read Workbook Answer Key

Grade 6 Science · Easy Read version · Model answers for all 24 questions and 4 stretch questions

EF workbooks use word banks. For fill-in questions, accept any answer from the word bank that correctly fills the blank. For open response, accept any response that includes the key idea noted.

Section 1 — Types of Energy Resources (Q1–Q8)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q1 Renewable vs non-renewable · fill-in A renewable energy source can be replaced naturally in a short time. A non-renewable energy source is finite. It took millions of years to form. It will runs out if we keep using it.
Q2 Sort renewable/non-renewable · table fill-in Coal = Non-Renewable · Solar = Renewable · Natural gas = Non-Renewable · Wind = Renewable · Oil = Non-Renewable · Hydroelectric = Renewable · Biomass (wood) = Renewable · Geothermal = Renewable
Q3 Coal formation steps · fill-in list 1. dead plants die in swamps. 2. They become peat. 3. heat and pressure from rock layers act on the peat. 4. After millions of years, hard black coal forms.
Q4 Crude oil vs refined oil · table fill-in Form 1 = crude oil (thick and dark; cannot be used directly). Form 2 = refined oil (cleaner and lighter); used as gasoline · diesel · jet fuel (accept any from WB).
Q5 Five selection factors · table fill-in cost (how much?) · availability (available in this region?) · reliability (works all the time?) · environmental impact (harms the environment?) · efficiency (how much useful energy?) — matched to questions in order.
Q6 How solar panels work · fill-in Solar panels contain photovoltaic cells. sunlight hits the cells and knocks electrons free. Moving electrons create an electrical current. Solar energy is renewable and uses no fuel.
Q7 How wind turbines work · fill-in wind blows on the blades. The blades spins. This is kinetic energy. The spinning drives a generator. The generator makes electrical energy.
Q8 Hydroelectric advantage/disadvantage · word bank Advantage (any one): renewable OR reliable OR no air pollution. Disadvantage (any one): flooding habitats OR relocating communities OR high cost to build OR changes river flow.

Section 2 — Energy Comparisons and Alberta (Q9–Q16)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q9 Coal vs solar comparison · table fill-in Renewable/non-renewable: Coal = non-renewable; Solar = renewable. Releases CO₂: Coal = releases CO₂; Solar = no CO₂. Always reliable: Coal = always available; Solar = depends on sunlight.
Q10 Why switching to renewables is not simple · word bank (choose 2) Any two from: high cost · existing infrastructure (built for fossil fuels) · reliability concerns · jobs in fossil fuels · storage technology not ready · political challenges.
Q11 Nuclear power — for/against · word bank Argument for (any one): low CO₂ OR reliable OR small land use. Argument against (any one): radioactive waste OR high cost to build OR dangerous if accidents occur.
Q12 Energy efficiency — definition + examples · word bank Energy efficiency means: how much useful energy output you get relative to total energy input (what is not wasted as heat). Efficient example: LED bulb (efficient). Inefficient example: incandescent bulb (inefficient).
Q13 Burning wood — carbon-neutral? · word bank + explain Carbon-neutral means: CO₂ absorbed by trees as they grow equals CO₂ released when burned — theoretically balanced. It is not entirely correct because: regrowth takes decades — the time scale mismatch means carbon released now is not re-absorbed for many years.
Q14 Bitumen processing steps · word bank (choose 2) Any two from: mining or drilling · separating from sand · diluting · upgrading · refining · processing.
Q15 Oil spills and Indigenous communities · word bank (choose 2) Any two from: contaminated water makes drinking water unsafe · fish population declines, removing a food source · traditional foods become unavailable · treaty rights to land and water are violated · land use is disrupted · cultural practices tied to the land and water are harmed.
Q16 Family heating comparison — 5 factors · table + recommendation Accept any reasonable ratings using the five factors. Key distinctions: Natural gas = low ongoing cost / widely available in Alberta / reliable / produces CO₂. Solar = high startup cost / good in sunny areas / intermittent / low impact. Wood stove = low cost if wood available / available where forests exist / reliable / some air pollution. Recommendation: accept any supported choice. Award marks for using at least two selection factors in the justification.

Section 3 — Evaluating and Applying (Q17–Q24)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q17 Solar energy is free? · misconception + explain The student is not completely correct. The sunlight is free, but panels cost money to buy and require installation and maintenance. The total cost of solar includes equipment and setup — only the energy source (sunlight) itself is free.
Q18 Energy storage challenge · fill-in wind and solar are intermittent energy sources. We need to store energy in batteries for use when there is no wind or sun.
Q19 Indigenous biomass + sustainability · word bank + explain The connection is: both involve balance between use and regrowth. Indigenous practice of using only what is needed and allowing forests to recover reflects the same principle as modern sustainability — using renewable resources without depleting them. Traditional knowledge understood sustainable use long before the term existed.
Q20 Natural gas as "transition fuel" · word bank + explain It is called a transition fuel because: it emits less CO₂ than coal and burns cleaner, so it acts as a bridge to renewables. It is a temporary step — not a permanent solution — used while renewable energy infrastructure is being built. It is not renewable.
Q21 Computational thinking in solar design · word bank + explain This is computational thinking because: a model uses an algorithm and data (building dimensions, sun angles) to simulate and optimize panel placement — testing many possibilities instantly to find the best solution. This is faster and more accurate than doing it by hand.
Q22 Two communities — different energy choices · word bank + explain Community A (sunny southern Alberta): solar — good availability (high sunshine hours) and low environmental impact. Community B (remote northern, cloudy, forested): biomass — local resources (forests available), reliable regardless of weather. Accept any supported pairing using different needs and at least one selection factor per community.
Q23 Geothermal — why not widely used · word bank + explain Any one from: only available in certain locations (requires volcanic/geothermal activity) · high drilling cost · not in Alberta / Alberta does not have the right geology · only accessible in volcanic regions · limited access restricts where it can be used.
Q24 Off-grid cabin design · word bank + open response Accept any two sources from solar · wind · biomass. Each source must be justified using at least two of the five selection factors (cost · availability · reliability · environmental impact · efficiency). Example: solar (good availability in Alberta summer, low environmental impact) + biomass (reliable year-round, locally available wood). No single right answer — award marks for using WB sources and naming selection factors.

Stretch Zone (S1–S4)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
S1 Town switching to 100% renewables · open response Three challenges: cost of infrastructure replacement / reliability/storage gaps / job losses in coal industry. Three strategies: phased transition over time / invest in battery storage / retrain workers for renewable sector. Award marks for naming three challenges and three corresponding solutions — no single right answer.
S2 Alberta oil economy vs climate crisis debate · open response Pro-oil: Alberta's economy and many jobs depend on oil revenues; abrupt phase-out would cause significant economic hardship. Pro-action: climate change threatens Alberta's water, agriculture, and long-term economic stability; delayed action increases future costs. Accept any clearly stated position with supporting reasoning on both sides.
S3 Hydrogen fuel cells — truly "clean"? · open response Not necessarily. The fuel cell itself only emits water — it is clean at the point of use. But making hydrogen currently requires energy, often from fossil fuels ("grey hydrogen"). Only "green hydrogen" (made using renewable electricity) is truly clean. The full picture: the cleanliness depends entirely on the energy source used to produce the hydrogen.
S4 Alberta 2050 energy advisor · open response Accept any two sources justified with reasoning (e.g., solar + wind → low environmental impact, increasingly cost-competitive; or wind + geothermal for a fully renewable mix). Must acknowledge one challenge (e.g., storage, job transition, reliability). Award marks for: two sources named, selection factors applied to each, one honest challenge acknowledged.
