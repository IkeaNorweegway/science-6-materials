Living Systems — Workbook Answer Key

Grade 6 Science · Model answers and marking guidance for all 30 core questions and 5 stretch questions

Core Questions — Q1 to Q30

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q1 ★ Biotic vs. abiotic · sort Biotic: eagle, bacteria, spruce tree, grasshopper.
Abiotic: sunlight, temperature, river current, soil pH.
Q2 ★ Photosynthesis equation · fill-in table CO₂ | Carbon dioxide | From the air.
H₂O | Water | Absorbed through the roots.
Light energy | Light energy | From the sun.
Glucose | Glucose | Used/stored by the plant as food.
O₂ | Oxygen | Released into the air.
Q3 ★ Producer / consumer / decomposer · match Wolf eating deer → Consumer. Spruce tree making glucose → Producer. Fungi breaking down log → Decomposer.
Own examples: any valid — e.g., Consumer: bear, hawk; Producer: algae, grass; Decomposer: earthworms, bacteria.
Q4 ★★ Soil-food misconception · error analysis Error: Plants do not absorb "food" from soil. Food (glucose) is manufactured BY the plant through photosynthesis — it is not something that exists in the soil. Soil provides minerals (nitrogen, potassium, etc.) but these are not food.
Correct: Plants make their own food (glucose) from CO₂ (from air) + water (from soil) using light energy. Soil minerals support plant health but the plant builds its own food through photosynthesis.
Q5 ★ Abiotic factors · boreal vs. grassland Any two valid factors with explanation:
Precipitation: Boreal forests receive more rainfall and snow → supports coniferous trees that need moisture; grasslands are drier → only drought-tolerant grasses and plants can survive.
Temperature: Boreal forests have longer, colder winters → organisms adapted to cold/frost; grasslands have hotter summers and shorter cold seasons → different plant and insect communities.
Accept: soil type, sunlight/shade, wind.
Q6 ★ Photosynthesis ingredients · plant diagram CO₂ enters through stomata (tiny pores) in the leaf. O₂ exits through stomata. Water enters through the roots.
Near window: because sunlight is a required ingredient for photosynthesis. Without light energy, the plant cannot drive the chemical reaction to produce glucose — it would not be able to make food.
Q7 ★★ Decomposer role · removal cascade Dead matter begins to accumulate / pile up.
Nutrients can no longer be returned to the soil.
Producers begin to decline / struggle / die (soil depleted of minerals).
Consumers that depend on producers decline / starve.
Wetland would collapse / lose its function / become an inert pile of dead matter.
Why not separate: Decomposers are the mechanism that recycles matter through the ecosystem — they complete the nutrient cycle that producers, consumers, and the abiotic environment all depend on.
Q8 ★ Where photosynthesis happens · explain Answer: C — the green parts of the plant. Because the green parts contain chlorophyll, the substance that captures light energy.
Green substance: chlorophyll.
Yellowing leaves: chlorophyll has broken down → cannot capture light → photosynthesis cannot occur → no glucose produced.
Q9 ★ Plant-animal interdependence · beyond food Animals depend on plants for (any two): oxygen (produced by photosynthesis); habitat/shelter (nesting sites, shade, shelter in trees); medicine (plant compounds); CO₂ absorption and climate regulation; soil stability (roots prevent erosion).
Plants depend on animals for (any one): pollination (animals carry pollen between flowers); seed dispersal (seeds moved to new locations); CO₂ supply (animals release CO₂ used in photosynthesis); nutrients (decomposition returns minerals to soil).
Q10 ★ Photosynthesis diagram · fill in blanks From air: CO₂ / carbon dioxide. From roots: water (H₂O). From sun: light energy. In the: leaves / green parts. Plant's food: glucose. Released into air: oxygen (O₂).
Q11 ★ Ecosystem types · boreal vs. coastal rainforest Any two valid abiotic differences:
1. Precipitation: coastal rainforest receives far more rainfall than Alberta boreal.
2. Temperature range: coastal climate is moderated by the ocean (milder winters); Alberta boreal has colder, longer winters.
(Also accept: humidity, growing season length, wind patterns.)
Biotic difference: Coastal supports different tree species (red cedar, Douglas fir) and more amphibians / moisture-dependent species; boreal supports spruce, pine, moose, wolves — cold-adapted species.
Q12 ★ Food chain · build and extend grass → vole → coyote → hawk.
Arrow means: "is eaten by" / "energy flows to" (the next organism in the chain).
Extend: e.g., bacteria/fungi (decomposer, after hawk); OR grasshopper (before vole — grass → grasshopper → vole →…). Accept any ecologically plausible Alberta organism with correct placement.
Q13 ★★ Photosynthesis/respiration misconception · two explanations Correct: Explanation B.
Evidence: During daylight, healthy plants photosynthesize far more than they respire — net O₂ is released and net CO₂ is absorbed. The fact that Earth's atmosphere is ~21% oxygen is direct evidence that plants are net oxygen producers over geological time. Explanation A (they cancel) would predict no net oxygen in the atmosphere, which contradicts observed reality.
Q14 ★ Trophic cascade · Yellowstone wolves Wolves removed → elk population grew unchecked / exploded.
Elk ate unchecked → riverbank vegetation was stripped / overgrazed.
Without plant roots → river banks began to erode / change course.
Songbirds declined because there were fewer trees and shrubs for nesting.
Wolves reintroduced → vegetation recovered / regrew / stabilized.
Name: trophic cascade.
Q15 ★ Glucose/starch storage · explain and apply Extra glucose → converted to starch.
Plant parts: roots (carrot, potato) and seeds (wheat grain, rice).
Why valuable to animals: starch is digested back into glucose, which provides energy for the animal's cells. Animals access the plant's stored energy by eating the storage organs (roots, seeds, tubers).
Q16 ★★ Biotic/abiotic · parking lot ecosystem Biotic: any two — weeds/grass growing in cracks, birds, insects, bacteria in soil between cracks, moss.
Abiotic: any two — sunlight, rain/runoff, temperature, asphalt, concrete, air, soil in cracks.
Interaction: e.g., sunlight heats asphalt (abiotic) → raises surface temperature (abiotic) → limits which plant species can survive in cracks (biotic effect).
Valid ecosystem: Yes — an ecosystem is any area where biotic and abiotic components interact. A parking lot has both, even if human-altered.
Q17 ★ FNMI sacred plants · ecological + cultural roles Any one of four with accurate description:
Sage: grows in dry grasslands; attracts pollinators; used in smudging ceremonies, healing.
Sweetgrass: stabilizes wetland soil; used in braiding ceremonies, represents kindness.
Cedar: large habitat provider, long-lived carbon sink; used in totem poles, medicine, ceremony.
Tobacco: attracts pollinators; used in ceremony for prayer and offering to the land.
Award marks for specific ecological role AND cultural significance — both required.
Q18 ★ Experiment 1 — light required · predict and explain Prediction: Plant A (sunny window) → starch present; Plant B (dark closet) → starch absent / not detected.
Why: Plant A had light energy → drove photosynthesis → produced glucose → converted to starch. Plant B had no light → photosynthesis could not occur → no glucose → no starch.
Light's role: Light is a required energy source for photosynthesis. Without it, the reaction cannot proceed.
Q19 ★★ Animals independent of plants misconception · two errors Error 1: Without plants, there would be no energy entering the food web. All food chains begin with producers. Herbivores would have nothing to eat and quickly die; without herbivores, carnivores would also die. "Just eating each other" cannot sustain the food web — energy is lost at every trophic level and must be replenished by producers.
Error 2: Animals depend on plants for oxygen. Photosynthesis produces the O₂ in Earth's atmosphere. Without plants, atmospheric oxygen would decline and animals could not breathe.
(Accept any two of: oxygen, habitat, soil stability, climate regulation — with correct reasoning.)
Q20 ★ Experiment 3 — green parts starch · predict table Green leaf: Yes — contains chlorophyll → photosynthesis occurred → glucose produced → stored as starch.
Yellow leaf: No — chlorophyll absent/broken down → no photosynthesis → no glucose → no starch.
Conclusion: Chlorophyll is required for photosynthesis; where there is no chlorophyll, there is no photosynthesis and no food production.
Q21 ★★ Most damaging removal · justify Answer: C — decomposers.
Justification: Removing apex predators (A) causes a trophic cascade but producers and nutrient cycling continue. Removing primary consumers (B) disrupts some chains but plants still fix nutrients. Removing ALL decomposers stops the entire nutrient cycle — dead matter accumulates, minerals become locked up and unavailable, producers cannot get the nutrients they need to grow, and the entire ecosystem collapses in time. No other group can replace decomposers in breaking down dead organic matter and returning it to the soil.
Q22 ★ Wetland food web · draw and analyze Valid web must include: algae and cattail as producers; dragonfly and muskrat as primary consumers (eat algae/cattail); duck (eats algae, insects); pike eats smaller fish/muskrat; heron eats pike/duck/dragonfly; decomposer bacteria receive dead matter from all. Arrows must point from eaten → eater.
Most disruptive to remove: algae or cattail (producers at base of web). Without producers, all consumers in the web would collapse.
Q23 ★★ Tree-in-pot problem · three-part soil misconception 1. Student expected soil to get lighter because they thought soil is the plant's food — consumed as the plant grows.
2. Soil mass barely changed → the tree's mass did NOT come from the soil (soil is not the source of plant material).
3. Almost all of the 799 kg came from CO₂ in the air. Through photosynthesis, the plant absorbed CO₂, combined it with water, and used light energy to build glucose. The carbon atoms from CO₂ became the structural material of the tree — cellulose, wood, bark. Carbon from air + water = tree mass.
Q24 ★ Mutual dependencies · complete table Oxygen produced by: photosynthesis. CO₂ used in: photosynthesis.
Energy source: glucose / food. Pollination: pollination.
Shelter: habitat / nesting sites. Seed dispersal: seed dispersal.
Nutrients returned: nutrients / minerals.
Q25 ★ Experiment 2 — Elodea oxygen · predict and explain Bubbles: oxygen (O₂).
When lamp covered: bubbles slow down and stop.
Why: Light is a required ingredient for photosynthesis. Without the lamp, the Elodea cannot photosynthesize → stops producing oxygen → no more bubbles are generated.
Q26 ★ CS conditional logic · drought cascade IF water drops THEN soil moisture decreases / producers lose water supply.
IF producers cannot get water THEN grass cannot grow / photosynthesize.
IF grass cannot grow THEN voles will have less food / decline in population.
IF vole populations decline THEN coyotes will have less prey / also decline.
Type of thinking: Conditional/IF-THEN logic — the same structure as conditional statements in computer programming, where each condition triggers the next action in a chain.
Q27 ★★ Starch vs. food misconception · evaluate Partially right: "food" and "starch" aren't identical — glucose is the plant's immediate food, starch is the stored form. But starch IS food in the sense that it stores the plant's energy. The student is wrong to say they're "two different things."
Valuable to animals: Animals digest starch back into glucose, which powers all body functions. When animals eat roots, seeds, grains, or tubers, they access the plant's stored energy reserves — starch is the energy bridge from photosynthesis to animal metabolism.
Q28 ★★ FNMI knowledge + ecological inference · sweetgrass Part A ecological conclusions: The wetland's conditions have changed — possibly drainage changes, water table shift, temperature increase, or invasive species competing. Sweetgrass requires specific moisture/soil conditions; its disappearance is an indicator of abiotic change in that wetland.
Part B: The Knowledge Keeper's multi-generational record provides a much longer baseline — she can compare current absence against decades of presence and identify this as a genuine long-term change, not natural year-to-year variation. A two-year scientific study cannot distinguish trend from fluctuation.
Q29 ★ Experiment 3 — green/yellow leaf starch · predict and shade Dark/starch present: the GREEN region.
Explanation: Starch is only found where photosynthesis occurred. The green region contains chlorophyll → captured light → drove photosynthesis → produced glucose → converted to starch. Yellow region lacks chlorophyll → no photosynthesis → no starch.
Conclusion: Chlorophyll is essential for photosynthesis; it is the site where light energy is captured to drive the reaction.
Q30 ★★ Trophic cascade · invasive insect removes caterpillars Any three with accurate reasoning:
Birds that eat caterpillars → decline / food source removed.
Trees/leaves → initially less herbivory from caterpillars but more from invasive insect; long-term depends on invasive's impact.
Secondary predators (hawks, owls) → decline as insectivorous bird populations fall.
Decomposers → spike in activity if mass die-off occurs.
Long-term disruption: Invasive species often establish permanently, preventing ecosystem return to prior state. Apex predators and slow-reproducing species may not recover; some species may go locally extinct. Could also be long-term if the invasive species is eradicated quickly — ecosystem may partially recover.

Stretch Questions — S1 to S5

Stretch questions require multi-concept synthesis. Use as discussion guides or for extended assessment marking.
Q#Concept ClusterModel Answer Guide
S1 ★★ Photosynthesis + sealed system + food web (a) Plant gets mass from CO₂ in the air (sealed inside). Carbon from CO₂ is fixed into glucose and then into plant tissue via photosynthesis.
(b) Carbon in CO₂ was absorbed through photosynthesis and incorporated into plant biomass — wood, cellulose, leaves. CO₂ levels dropped because the plant was using it up.
(c) Animal CAN potentially survive if: the plant produces enough O₂ to meet the animal's respiratory needs, AND produces enough edible glucose/starch for the animal to eat. This models a simple closed-loop ecosystem. Long-term viability depends on whether plant growth and O₂ production match animal consumption rate.
S2 ★★ Experimental design · CO₂ required for photosynthesis Key design elements: Control — same plant species, same light, same water, same temperature, same time period. Change — CO₂ level (one plant in sealed chamber with CO₂ absorbed by NaOH solution; control in normal air). Measure — starch content of leaves (iodine test) after a set period.
Support hypothesis: low or no starch in CO₂-depleted plant; normal starch in control.
Disprove: equal starch in both plants (CO₂ not the limiting factor).
Award marks for clearly separating independent variable (CO₂) from controlled variables and from the dependent variable (starch).
S3 ★★ FNMI-TEK + ecosystem health + scientific methods (a) 70 years of family records vs. 2 years of scientific study — the Knowledge Keeper's record goes back 35× further, can distinguish long-term trend from short-term variation, and covers outlier years that provide context.
(b) Effect 1: loss of habitat and nesting sites for species that shelter in/under cedar. Effect 2: reduction in carbon stored in long-lived trees → less carbon sink in that wetland ecosystem.
(c) Combined study: map historical cedar locations using Knowledge Keeper oral records + current satellite/field survey to document change; correlate with water table data and temperature records to identify probable cause; interview Elders about what changed in the wetland over time. Both sources needed — Knowledge Keeper for baseline + local detail; instruments for precise measurement and causal data.
S4 ★★ CS conditional logic + photosynthesis + food web · volcanic eruption Valid cascade (any five linked IF/THEN steps): IF sunlight reduced 70% THEN photosynthesis greatly reduced in all producers → IF photosynthesis reduced THEN producers make far less glucose and O₂ → IF producers decline THEN primary consumers (herbivores) have less food and crash → IF herbivores crash THEN secondary consumers (carnivores) lose prey and crash → IF all consumer levels crash THEN decomposers face massive die-off of organisms to break down.
Recovery: Once ash clears, pioneer species/stored seeds can recolonize. Producers can regrow rapidly from seeds.
May not fully recover: Apex predators with slow reproduction may not return; invasive species may fill niches; locally extinct species cannot return without reintroduction.
S5 ★★ Synthesis — all unit concepts What is wrong with "list" idea: An ecosystem is not a list — it is a web of interdependencies where every organism's presence affects others through nutrient cycles, food webs, oxygen production, decomposition, and abiotic interactions. Removing one "item" doesn't just shorten the list; it cascades.
Must reference at least 3 examples: Yellowstone wolves (one removal = trophic cascade through the whole ecosystem); photosynthesis (plants build the oxygen atmosphere all animals breathe — the "list" is connected by chemistry); decomposers (without them, nutrient cycle stops and producers collapse); sacred plants (each plays specific ecological roles — not just culturally present but functionally essential).
One-sentence definition: An ecosystem is a community of biotic and abiotic components that are interconnected and interdependent — the removal or change of any one part cascades through the entire system.
