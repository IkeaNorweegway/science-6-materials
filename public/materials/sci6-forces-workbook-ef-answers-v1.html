Q13 Elastic vs. plastic — helmets · word bank + explain Helmet foam = plastic deformation · Rubber ball = elastic deformation. Safer for a helmet = plastic deformation. Because: Helmet foam that stays crushed absorbs the impact energy permanently — it does not spring back and hit the head again.
Q14 Bridge cable — elastic limit · word bank Cable must have elastic deformation. Force must stay below the elastic limit.
Q15 Action-reaction pairs — valid/invalid · Yes/No table Pair 1 (you push wall → wall pushes you): Yes. Pair 2 (gravity + normal force, both on you): No. Pair 3 (you push skateboard → skateboard pushes you): Yes. Pair 4 (you kick ball → ball kicks foot): Yes. Why Pair 2 is not valid: Both forces act on the same object (you). Action-reaction pairs must act on different objects.

Section 3 — Newton's Third Law and Applications (Q18–Q25)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q18 Action-reaction — jumping · fill-in Action: You push down on the floor. Reaction: The floor pushes up on you. These forces are equal and opposite.
Q19 Car-truck collision — N3L misconception · word bank + explain not correct. Because: Newton's Third Law says action and reaction forces are always equal in size. The car and truck exert the same size force on each other. They have different masses, so the effects are different — but the forces are equal.
Q20 Rowing — N3L application · word bank + explain The boat moves forward. Explanation: Paddle pushes water backward (action). Water pushes paddle forward (reaction) — Newton's Third Law. This forward push moves the boat forward.
Q21 Action-reaction cancel misconception · open response The error is: action and reaction forces act on different objects — they cannot cancel each other. Forces only cancel when they act on the same object. Because each force acts on a different object, each object can still accelerate.
Q22 Leaning on wall — action-reaction pair · word bank Action: you push into the wall. Reaction: wall pushes back on you. (Forces are equal and opposite.)
Q23 Rocket — N3L + forces · word bank Law: Newton's Third Law. Action force: gases push down. Reaction force: rocket is pushed up.
Q24 Building design — tension/compression · word bank Tension found in: cables (or suspension bridge cables). Compression found in: columns (or arch). Accept any structurally correct pairing using WB terms.
Q25 Rock climber — multi-part forces · word bank Part A: gravity (or weight). Part B: tension. Part C: net force = 0 N (or balanced — forces are equal and opposite). Part D: Hand pushes into rock (action) → rock pushes back on hand (reaction) — equal and opposite — Newton's Third Law.

Stretch Zone (S1–S4)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
S1 N3L — skateboarder and wall · open response Skateboarder pushes wall (action force on wall). Wall pushes back on skateboarder with equal and opposite force (reaction). Reaction force acts on skateboarder, accelerating her away. Two objects, two forces — N3L.
S2 Suspension bridge — tension/compression · open response Cables under tension: forces pull outward → particles pulled apart → tension is correct for cables (which are designed to stretch without breaking). Towers under compression: weight of road deck pushes down → particles pushed together → compression is correct for columns/towers. Particle-level thinking: each structure is engineered for the force it will experience.
S3 Elastic limit investigation · open response Measure: extension (length) of the rubber band. Change: force applied (add masses). Record: length at each mass. Elastic limit passed: when removing the mass does NOT return the rubber band to its original length (permanent stretch = plastic deformation begins).
S4 Indigenous technology — forces · open response Example — snowshoes: distribute weight (normal force) over large area → less pressure per cm² → avoid sinking into snow. Material must resist compression and flex without plastic deformation. Example — birchbark canoe: hull under compression from water pressure; paddles use tension in arms and N3L to propel. Accept any well-reasoned example naming specific forces and material properties.
