Misconception

"A particle is just a tiny piece of the material — like a tiny piece of steel."

This is one of the most common misunderstandings in this unit. A particle in the particle model is not a miniature version of the material. Particles are discrete units with empty space between them — that space is real, and it is what allows matter to expand and contract. If particles were stuck together with no space, nothing could ever change size with temperature. The space between particles is as important as the particles themselves.

The experimental logic of phase changes

Scientists who study phase changes control one variable at a time. To test how temperature affects the phase change of water, you would:

  • Keep the same   of water each time (the amount doesn't change the melting or boiling points)

Critical idea — Mass is conserved during a phase change

When matter changes state,   is conserved. Only the   and   of the particles change.

100 g of ice → melts → still   g of liquid water

100 g of liquid water → evaporates → still   g of water vapour (as a gas)

The mass does NOT change. Volume, however,   change during a phase change.

Misconception

"When ice melts, some of the mass disappears — that's why there's less water."

Mass is never created or destroyed during a phase change. The ice and the puddle of water weigh exactly the same. What changes is the arrangement of particles and the volume they take up — not the number of particles or their mass. If water seems to "disappear" when it evaporates, it has not vanished — it has become water vapour in the air, still carrying the same mass.

A traditional liquid thermometer works because of the same particle behaviour you just studied:

  1. The thermometer contains a liquid (often coloured alcohol) sealed in a glass tube.

  3. When temperature  , the liquid particles slow down and come closer together. The liquid   and falls down the tube.

The general rule: expansion and contraction

The particle model predicts a clear pattern for most materials:

  • When a material is  , its particles move faster and spread further apart. The material takes up more space — it  .
  • When a material is  , its particles slow down and move closer together. The material takes up less space — it  .

This applies to solids, liquids, and gases — though gases expand and contract far more dramatically than solids or liquids.

The water anomaly — when the general rule breaks

For almost every material, the solid is   than the liquid. More tightly packed particles = smaller volume for the same mass = higher density.

Water breaks this rule. This is called the water anomaly.

  • Most liquids become more   when they solidify

This means ice takes up   space than the same mass of liquid water. Ice is less dense than liquid water. This is why ice  .

Why water is different — the particle model explanation

In liquid water, particles are packed closely and moving freely. When water freezes, the particles slow down and lock into a crystal structure. This crystal structure has a   arrangement that actually pushes particles further apart — creating more space, not less.

Misconception

"Ice is denser than liquid water — solids are always denser than liquids."

For most materials, this is true — but water is a major exception. Ice is less dense than liquid water. This is why ice floats at the surface rather than sinking to the bottom. The reason is the structure water forms when it freezes: a hexagonal crystal lattice that takes up more space than randomly arranged liquid water particles. This has enormous consequences for life on Earth.

Why the water anomaly matters — ecological and practical consequences

Aquatic life in winter:
When a lake cools in winter, the ice forms on the   and floats. The liquid water beneath stays at approximately  °C — cold, but not frozen. Fish, plants, and other organisms can survive through winter in the liquid layer  .
If ice were denser than liquid water, ice would sink to the bottom. Lakes and rivers could freeze   to   in winter, and most aquatic life would not survive.

Infrastructure — ice in rock cracks:
Water that seeps into rock cracks and freezes expands as it solidifies. This expansion breaks rock apart — a process called freeze-thaw weathering.

FNMI Knowledge — seasonal ice observation in northern Alberta:
Cree, Dene, and Métis communities in northern Alberta have observed and documented seasonal freeze-thaw cycles on rivers and lakes for generations. This knowledge includes when ice is thick enough for travel, how ice changes near river currents, and how early melt or late freeze signals broader ecological shifts. This is long-term data collection that aligns with what scientists now measure using sensors and satellite data — both tracking the same anomalous behaviour of water from different frameworks.
