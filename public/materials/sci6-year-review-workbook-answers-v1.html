Grade 6 Science · Alberta Curriculum 2023 · All 6 Units · Q1–Q32 Core + S1–S6 Stretch

Core Questions — Q1 to Q15 (Session 1)

# Unit / Type Answer / Marking Guide
Q1 ★ Forces
Internal forces		 (a) Compression — the top of the beam is being squeezed together by the load pressing down.
(b) Tension — the bottom of the beam is being pulled apart as it bends.
(c) The external force (the walker's weight pressing down) causes the beam to bend. As it bends, the material inside resists deformation — producing compression at the top and tension at the bottom as internal forces.
Circulation: if students reverse the two, ask "if I squeezed clay from both sides, is the middle being pulled or pushed?"
Q2 ★ Living Systems
Biotic/abiotic + decomposers		 (a) Biotic (any two): spruce trees · wolves · insects · moss
Abiotic (any two): rocks · soil · sunlight · water
(b) Decomposers break down dead organic material, returning nutrients to the soil. They complete the nutrient cycle — without them, ecosystems would accumulate dead matter and nutrients would be locked away from living organisms.
Circulation: push beyond "clean up" — ask what specifically happens to the nutrients.
Q3 ★ Matter
Particle model — thermal expansion		 When the rail heats up, particles absorb energy and vibrate faster. As they move faster, they push slightly further apart — increasing the spacing between particles. This causes the rail's overall length and volume to increase (thermal expansion).
Key language: particles moving faster · spacing increasing · volume/length increasing.
Redirect students who say "the rail absorbs water" — keep focused on the particle model.
Q4 ★ Climate
Weather vs. climate		 (a) Weather = short-term atmospheric conditions in a specific place and time (temperature, precipitation, wind on a given day). Climate = long-term patterns of weather in a region over decades or longer.
(b) Three floods over 30 years is consistent weather variability data — but to claim the climate has changed, you need decades of data showing a consistent trend or shift in the long-term pattern. The scientist is making a claim about the long-term system, not any single event.
Q5 ★ Space
Artificial satellite + emit vs. reflect		 (a) Artificial satellite — designed and built by humans; not formed through a natural process.
(b) Ex-Alta 1 reflects sunlight. It is visible because sunlight bounces off its surface and reaches observers/tracking equipment on Earth.
(c) Produces own light: the Sun (any star) · Reflects light: any planet, moon, or asteroid (e.g., Mars, Earth's Moon)
Circulation: students who say planets "shine" — clarify that planets reflect, they do not emit.
Q6 ★ Energy Resources
Five selection factors		 Any three of the five selection factors:
Availability — is the resource accessible in this location?
Environmental impact — pollution, land use, emissions
Economic impact — installation, maintenance, ongoing cost
Societal impact — jobs, reliability, public acceptance
Responsible management — long-term sustainability; leaving options open for future generations
Q7 ★ Sci. Methods
Controlled experiment features		 Any two of:
Control group: spruce trees at normal temperature; all other conditions identical to the experimental group — isolates temperature as the variable being tested.
Variable isolation: only one variable (temperature) changes between groups; all other conditions (water, soil, light, species) held constant.
Repeated trials: multiple trees tested at each temperature so the result isn't due to chance.
Measurable outcome: growth measured consistently and quantifiably (e.g., height in cm over 6 weeks).

Q9 ★ Forces
Elasticity vs. plasticity		 (a) Elasticity (elastic deformation) — the planks flex and spring back; temporary change.
(b) Plasticity (plastic deformation) — the railing post is permanently bent; the force exceeded the elastic limit.
(c) Elasticity = temporary change; material returns to original shape when force is removed. Plasticity = permanent change; material deformed beyond its elastic limit and stays changed.
Q10 ★ Matter
States of matter — visual		 (a) Box A: solid · Box B: liquid · Box C: gas
(b) Melting (solid → liquid). Accept: fusion.
(c) During vaporization (liquid → gas), particles are gaining enough energy to overcome the attractive forces holding them close together. They break free and spread out — requiring a large energy input.
Accept "steam" colloquially but push for "gas" as the state-of-matter term.
Q11 ★ Energy Resources
Before/after processing — wind
⚠ misconception target		 (a) Before processing: wind powering a traditional windmill (grinding grain, pumping water), or sails on a boat. Accept any use of wind's mechanical energy directly without conversion.
(b) After processing: wind turbine converting wind energy into electricity — a different form of energy.
Misconception: wind is only used after processing. The before-processing use (windmill) is the key concept that separates "before/after" from "renewable/non-renewable."
Q12 ★ Forces
Newton's Third Law		 (a) 40 N — equal in magnitude to the force applied.
(b) The reaction force is: equal in magnitude · opposite in direction · acts on a different object (the railing acts on the student; the student acts on the railing).
Q13 ★ Living Systems
Plant-animal interdependence
⚠ misconception target		 Oxygen production — plants release oxygen through photosynthesis, which animals need to breathe. Accept also: shelter/habitat provision; regulation of CO₂ levels; role in the water cycle.
Do not accept: "for food" — the question explicitly asks beyond food.
Misconception: students who can only list food dependency are missing the oxygen/CO₂ cycle — a key Living Systems misconception.
Q14 ★ Climate
Natural and human causes		 (a) Any two natural causes: volcanic eruptions (CO₂ release + ash blocks sunlight) · changes in Earth's orbit (Milankovitch cycles) · variations in solar output · large meteor/asteroid impacts
(b) Any two human causes: burning fossil fuels (releasing CO₂) · deforestation (reducing CO₂ absorption) · industrial agriculture (methane) · industrial pollution
Q15 ★ Matter
Thermometers + particle model		 (a) When temperature rises, the liquid particles gain energy and move faster. Moving faster, they push further apart (expand), taking up more space — causing the liquid to rise up the thermometer tube.
(b) Celsius (°C). Reference point: 0°C = the temperature at which water freezes / ice melts.

Core Questions — Q16 to Q32 (Session 2)

# Unit / Type Answer / Marking Guide

Order of inputs is flexible — all three must be present. Both outputs must be present.
Q17 ★ Space Methods
Pluto reclassification — self-correcting science
⚠ misconception target		 (a) Science updates classifications and explanations when new evidence or better criteria emerge. The reclassification of Pluto shows that science changes when understanding improves — not that earlier scientists made careless mistakes.
(b) This is science working correctly. Scientists updated the classification of "planet" when Kuiper Belt discoveries required it. Self-correction is a strength of science — it means the field does not hold onto wrong ideas indefinitely.
Misconception: science changing = science being wrong. Push students to articulate why changing is a feature, not a flaw.
Q18 ★ Methods Climate
Traditional knowledge + scientific data		 Strong answer: The two types of evidence work together because they measure the same phenomenon from different perspectives and timescales. Temperature data provides precise, quantitative, instrument-verified measurements over 30 years. Traditional knowledge provides qualitative, observation-based documentation extending further back — capturing local, on-the-ground changes that instruments may not record. When both point to the same conclusion (warming, earlier ice break-up), the explanation is more robust — tested by independent methods and still holding.
Push for: students must say more than "they agree." Why does agreement across different knowledge systems strengthen the explanation?
Q19 ★ Forces
Newton's Third Law — visual
⚠ misconception target		 (a) Applied force (the student's weight / gravitational force acting downward through the student onto the deck). Accept: weight, gravitational force.
(b) Reaction arrow: points upward from bridge deck toward student · labelled 500 N · Normal force (or reaction force / contact force).
(c) A Newton's Third Law pair always acts on two different objects — one on the student, one on the bridge. Forces that "balance out" act on the same object. N3L pairs are always equal, opposite, and on different objects.
Misconception: action-reaction forces cancel out. They act on different objects and cannot cancel. Most high-yield misconception in Forces.
Q20 ★★ Matter
Water anomaly — ice density		 (a) In most substances, particles pack more tightly when they freeze (solid = denser than liquid). Water particles, however, arrange into a more open structure when they freeze — with more space between particles than in liquid water. This makes ice less dense than liquid water. (At Grade 6: convey that particle arrangement in ice is more spread out/open than liquid water.)
(b) If ice were denser, it would sink. Lakes would freeze from the bottom up and could freeze solid in Alberta winters — leaving no liquid water layer. The floating ice layer insulates the water below; without it, no protected zone exists for aquatic life to survive winter.
Q21 ★★ Climate
Greenhouse effect vs. ozone hole
⚠ misconception target		 (a) The error is treating the greenhouse effect and the ozone hole as the same phenomenon caused by the same gases. They are completely unrelated processes.
(b) Greenhouse effect: CO₂, methane, and water vapour absorb heat radiated from Earth's surface and redirect some back toward Earth — warming the planet. A natural process, amplified by human-added greenhouse gases.
(c) Ozone hole: thinning of the ozone layer (upper atmosphere) caused primarily by CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons). CFCs break down ozone molecules, reducing UV absorption. Not caused by the same gases as the greenhouse effect.
Q22 ★★ Energy Resources
Renewable ≠ clean
⚠ misconception target		 The claim is partially correct but misleading. "Renewable" = a resource replenished naturally and not running out in a human lifetime. "Clean" = low environmental impact in extraction and use. These are separate concepts.
Example: Large hydroelectric dams are renewable but can flood valleys, destroy ecosystems, and displace communities. Solar panel manufacturing uses toxic materials. Wind turbines require land and affect bird migration.
Accept any well-reasoned example that correctly separates the two concepts.
Q23 ★★ Living Systems
Plants get food from soil
⚠ misconception target		 (a) Correct: plants do absorb water through roots (water is a genuine photosynthesis input); fertilizer supports growth by providing minerals.
(b) Wrong: the statement implies soil and water are the plant's "food" — that the plant gets energy from soil. Soil provides minerals and water provides raw material, not energy. The plant is not consuming pre-made food.
(c) Plants make their own food through photosynthesis. Using light energy, they combine CO₂ (from air) and water (from soil) to produce glucose. Glucose is the food — the plant creates it using light energy. Water is an ingredient; soil minerals are structural supports; neither is a food source.
Q24 ★★ Forces
Newton's Third Law — heavier object misconception
⚠ misconception target		 The claim is incorrect. The railing cannot push back differently on two students who push with the same force — the reaction force equals the applied force, regardless of the pusher's mass. Both students applied 60 N; the railing pushes back with 60 N on each. Mass determines how much force a person can generate, but the Third Law pair depends on the actual force applied, not on body size.

Q26 ★★ Matter
Phase changes — particle identity misconception
⚠ misconception target		 (a) Wrong because particles do not "freeze" or "harden" or change identity during a phase change. Water molecules remain water molecules in all three states. What changes is their arrangement and speed — not what they are.
(b) Corrected: "When water in rock cracks freezes, the water particles do not change into a different kind of particle. They are still water molecules. What changes is that they slow down and arrange into a fixed pattern (solid state), held together by stronger attractive forces."
Q27 ★★ Space
Scale — solar system crowded misconception
⚠ misconception target		 Error 1 — Scale/spacing: Planets are not evenly or closely spaced. The solar system is almost entirely empty space. Distances increase dramatically moving outward. A true scale model would require microscopic dots separated by enormous distances. Why people believe this: textbook diagrams compress distances to fit a page.
Error 2 — Relative size: The Sun is vastly larger than all planets combined (>99% of total mass). Planets vary enormously — Jupiter is 11× Earth's diameter; Mercury is less than half. Why: diagrams show all objects visible for readability, not to scale.
Q28 ★★ Forces
Internal forces — bridge structures		 (a) Tension — the rope is being pulled/stretched from both ends by the weight it supports. The material is under a pulling force.
(b) Compression — the vertical post is being squeezed from above by the weight pressing down. The material is under a pushing force.
(c) Torsion — the diagonal brace resists twisting; forces act in a rotational direction. Accept shear if students argue the brace resists sliding forces; acknowledge and clarify torsion as the more precise term for resisting twist.
Q29 ★★ Living Systems
Scientific methods — photosynthesis experiment		 (a) Does photosynthesis require light? / Can plants make food without light?
(b) Two groups of identical spruce seedlings in identical conditions (same soil, water, temperature). Control group: normal light. Experimental group: complete darkness (covered, no light). All other variables identical. Run for a consistent period (e.g., 2 weeks).
(c) Dark seedlings: no starch produced (iodine test shows no blue-black colour change; light group shows colour change). May also show yellowing as chlorophyll breaks down without light.
Push for iodine test as observable outcome — not just "they die" (plants draw on stored glucose first; death is not the immediate observable result).
Q30 ★★ Energy Resources
Selection factors — wind vs. natural gas		 (a) Any two valid advantages: wind is renewable — will not run out unlike natural gas · reduced greenhouse gas emissions from heating · local energy production reduces dependence on market-priced fuel · once installed, fuel cost is essentially zero (wind is free).
(b) Any valid limitation: wind is intermittent — needs energy storage or backup when wind is calm · high upfront capital cost for turbines and grid upgrades · Alberta wind patterns may not be consistent at all locations · natural gas infrastructure already exists; transition requires significant new infrastructure.
Q31 ★★ Matter
Thermal expansion — expansion joints		 (a) Steel and concrete expand when heated and contract when cooled. In Alberta, the seasonal range is extreme (–30°C to +35°C). Expansion joints allow material to expand in summer without pressing against adjacent sections. Without the gap, expanding sections push against each other, building enormous compressive forces.
(b) Without joints: sections expand and press together. Compressive forces build until the material buckles, curves sideways, or cracks. Particle model: particles move faster and push further apart — but if expansion is blocked, they press against adjacent sections with increasing force, causing structural failure (buckling = compression-driven internal force failure).
Q32 ★★ Methods Climate
Self-correcting science		 Self-correcting science: Scientists use new evidence to update or revise explanations. When better data, instruments, or methods reveal an earlier explanation was incomplete or wrong, science changes its position. This is not failure — it is how knowledge improves.
Example from the year: Pluto's reclassification — scientists updated the definition of "planet" when Kuiper Belt discoveries demanded it. The evidence required revision; the scientific community agreed on a better classification. The change was made because evidence demanded it, not arbitrarily.
Also accept: particle model refinement; climate model improvements with better data; evolving understanding of photosynthesis. Marks for: naming the concept (self-correcting) + giving a specific example with explanation.

Stretch Zone — S1 to S6

Stretch questions are open-ended and cross-unit. Mark for quality of reasoning, accurate use of unit concepts, and cross-unit connections — not for matching the model answer exactly.
# Units / Type Answer / Marking Guide
S1 ★★ Matter + Forces
Thermal expansion → internal forces		 (a) Heat causes steel particles to gain energy, vibrate faster, and push further apart — increasing the overall length of the rail (thermal expansion, particle model).
(b) Compression — the expanding sections press against each other (pushed together). The internal force at the contact point is compressive.
(c) Without expansion gaps, compressive force builds until the rail buckles sideways (thermal buckling). The material exceeds its compressive limit and deforms permanently outward to relieve the force. This is a compression failure.
Cross-unit connection: particle model explains why expansion happens; internal forces concept explains what structural failure results.
S2 ★★ Living Systems + Climate
Boreal forest + carbon cycle feedback		 (a) Trees absorb CO₂ during photosynthesis and incorporate the carbon into biomass (wood, leaves). A healthy boreal forest continuously removes large amounts of CO₂ from the atmosphere — acting as a carbon sink.
(b) Feedback loop: wildfire releases stored carbon as CO₂ → more CO₂ in atmosphere → stronger greenhouse effect → warmer temperatures → drier conditions → more fires. The forest that was removing carbon now adds it, and there are fewer trees to absorb CO₂ going forward.
(c) The boreal forest is one of Earth's largest carbon sinks. Its loss affects global CO₂ concentrations — not just Alberta's climate. This connects Alberta's forest health to global climate stability.
S3 ★★ Energy Resources + Methods
TEK + evidence-based energy decisions		 (a) Collect wind speed and direction data at multiple points over at least one full year (to capture seasonal variation). Compare against the town's energy demand profile. Standardized measurement tools, consistent recording intervals, and independent collection of demand data. Testing "viability" requires cost analysis separately from availability data — keep claims separate.
(b) Traditional knowledge could reveal seasonal and long-term wind pattern shifts that a one-year instrument study would miss entirely. If wind patterns have shifted over a generation, a one-year study might conclude the valley is viable when it is historically unreliable. Indigenous observation provides long-term trend data that improves research accuracy.
S4 ★★ Forces + Living Systems
Internal forces in a living tree		 (a) Windward side (facing wind): tension — being stretched as wind pushes the crown away. Leeward side: compression — being squeezed as trunk bends. The outer surface on the windward side may also experience shear as wood layers slide relative to each other.
(b) Elasticity — living wood returns to original shape when wind stops. This is elastic deformation; the wood is below its elastic limit.
(c) Plasticity — the dead branch has exceeded its elastic limit; the deformation is permanent. Dead wood is typically less flexible (more brittle) because it has lost the moisture and cellular structure that gives living wood its elasticity.
Cross-unit connection: Both bridge (Forces) and tree (Living Systems context) demonstrate the same internal force principles — elasticity and plasticity operate in biological materials, not only engineered ones.
S5 ★★ Matter + Living Systems + Methods
Scientific models as abstractions		 (a) Abstraction — a simplified version of a complex system that retains only the most relevant features.
(b) Simplified models are useful because they make essential relationships understandable without overwhelming detail. The particle model correctly predicts key behaviours (expansion, phase changes) without requiring quantum mechanics. The photosynthesis model correctly identifies inputs, outputs, and energy flow without requiring biochemistry. Simplification allows learners to build accurate, usable understanding of core concepts.
(c) A simplified model becomes a problem when its omissions lead to incorrect predictions in contexts that need the missing detail. Example: the Grade 6 particle model treats all particles as identical spheres — this works for thermal expansion but produces incorrect predictions about chemical reactions, where particle type matters enormously.
S6 ★★ All Tier 1 Units
Cross-unit synthesis — climate effects		 Sample answer — students may identify different valid connections:
Matter: Concept: thermal expansion and water density anomaly. Observable change: permafrost thaw in northern Alberta — warming causes ice in soil to melt, particle spacing changes (solid → liquid), destabilizing ground and damaging infrastructure.
Living Systems: Concept: photosynthesis/carbon absorption. Observable change: boreal forest fires and reduced cover. Higher temperatures and drought weaken forest health; fires release stored carbon. The forest's CO₂ absorption role is reduced, accelerating the greenhouse effect.
Forces: Concept: thermal expansion → compressive internal forces. Observable change: road and rail buckling in summer heat events. Extreme temperatures cause expansion in paved roads and railways; without adequate expansion joints, compressive forces build until the material buckles. Heat waves in Alberta are increasing in frequency and intensity.
Mark for: names a specific unit concept · identifies an observable Alberta change · explains the link accurately. Penalize only if the unit concept is missing or the connection is factually wrong.
