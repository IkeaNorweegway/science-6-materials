Pre-Knowledge Check

~8 minutes — before any instruction

Show
Board: "Write everything you think you know about matter and particles. What is matter made of? What happens to matter when it heats up or cools down? What is the difference between a solid, a liquid, and a gas?"
Say
  • "Before we start — write everything that's already in your head. No marks. No correction today."
  • "There are no right answers here. I want to see what you already think."
  • "You'll come back to this at the end of the unit and see how your thinking changed."
Cue
★ Write: "5 minutes — write everything. Go." Circulate. Watch for: students who say particles are tiny pieces of the material (the core misconception of the unit). Do not correct during the pre-check.

Section 1: The Particle Model

Learning target: I can explain what the particle model says, describe how particles are arranged and moving, and predict what happens to particles when matter is heated or cooled.
Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1 — What is matter?
Show
Board: MATTER = has mass + takes up space
Say
  • "Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space."
  • "The chair you sit on. The air you breathe. The water you drink. All matter."
  • "What is NOT matter? Light. Sound. Heat. These are forms of energy — they don't have mass in the same sense."
Cue
★ Write: "Matter is anything that has [mass] and takes up [space]."
Beat 2 — The particle model: four key ideas
Show
Board: Write the four ideas as a numbered list — leave room to fill in the missing words with students. 
1. All matter is made up of tiny _____ (too small to see)
2. There are _____ between particles
3. Particles are always _____
4. The _____ of movement changes with temperature
Say
  • "The particle model is how scientists explain matter. Four ideas — they work together."
  • "Idea 1: All matter is made of tiny particles — too small to see with the naked eye."
  • "Idea 2: There are spaces between particles — they are NOT packed together with zero space between them. The space is real and it matters."
  • "Idea 3: Particles are always moving. Even in a solid. They never fully stop."
  • "Idea 4: The speed of that movement changes with temperature. Hot = faster. Cold = slower."
Cue
★ Write: "1. [particles] 2. [spaces] 3. [moving] 4. [speed]" — fill in the four key ideas.
Flag — Core Misconception of the Unit
"A particle is just a tiny piece of the material — like a tiny piece of steel." This is wrong and it will block everything else. Address it now. SAY: "A particle of steel is not a tiny steel shaving. Particles are discrete units with real empty space between them. That space is what allows matter to expand, contract, and change phase. If particles were packed solid with no space, none of that could happen." Return to this misconception at every phase change discussion.
Beat 3 — Temperature = particle speed
Show
Board: Two columns — COLD (slow, close together) vs. HOT (fast, spread apart). Draw simple particle diagrams with spacing differences.
Say
  • "Temperature is not a measure of heat stored. Temperature measures how fast particles are moving on average."
  • "Hot thermometer = fast particles. Cold thermometer = slow particles."
  • "When matter gets hotter, particles move faster and spread further apart."
  • "When matter gets colder, particles slow down and come closer together."
Cue
★ Write: "Temperature is a measure of how [fast] particles are moving on average." Then: "When heated → particles move [faster] and spread [further apart]. When cooled → particles move [slower] and come [closer together]."
Beat 4 — Particle diagrams from memory
Show
Board: Two blank diagram boxes. Label A and B. Students must label them hot/cold and faster/slower based on particle spacing shown.
Cue
⊡ Think: "Look at Diagram A and Diagram B in your notes. Which is hotter? How do you know? Write your labels before I tell you." Wait 60 seconds. Then confirm: "Tightly packed, close together = cold, slow. Spread out = hot, fast."
Beat 5 — Summary table
Cue
★ Write: Complete the summary table in notes: "All matter is made of [particles] / Particles have [space] between them / Particles are always [moving] / Heating makes particles move [faster] and spread [further apart] / Cooling makes particles move [slower] and come [closer together]."
Flag — CS Connection
The particle model is an abstraction — it keeps particle spacing and movement speed, and removes actual molecular structure, chemical bonding, quantum behaviour. A map abstracts a city. A weather app abstracts the atmosphere into temperature, pressure, humidity. The particle model does the same for matter. Allow 60 seconds for students to write one example of an abstraction they use in everyday life.
Section 1 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Two of: (a) all matter is made of particles, (b) there are spaces between particles, (c) particles are always moving, (d) speed changes with temperature
  2. Temperature measures how fast particles are moving on average (not heat stored)
  3. The space between particles increases — particles spread further apart as they move faster
  4. Particles absorb energy, move faster, and spread further apart — the rod gets slightly longer (expansion)
  5. (Stretch) The particle model makes predictions that can be tested. Expansion when heated, contraction when cooled, phase changes at specific temperatures — all are observable evidence consistent with the model. "Can't see it" does not mean "can't test it." Science tests models by their predictions, not by direct observation of every component.

Section 2: States of Matter and Phase Changes

Learning target: I can describe the particle arrangement and movement in solids, liquids, and gases, name the six phase changes, and explain why mass is conserved during a phase change.
Timing: ~22 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1 — The three states: particle comparison
Show
Board: Three columns — SOLID / LIQUID / GAS. For each: draw particle arrangement, write "particle spacing" and "particle movement" below each diagram.
Say
  • "The particle model explains the three states of matter. Same idea — different spacing, different speed."
  • "Solid: particles packed tightly in a regular arrangement. They vibrate but don't move past each other. Shape is fixed."
  • "Liquid: particles are close but NOT in a fixed arrangement. They can slide past each other — they flow. Shape is NOT fixed."
  • "Gas: particles are far apart, moving fast and randomly. Fills all available space."
Cue
★ Write notes blanks: Solid — [tightly] / [vibrate] / [fixed]. Liquid — [close] / [past] / [not fixed]. Gas — [far apart] / [fast and random] / [not fixed].
Flag — Pacing
Students will draw particle diagrams too quickly or too randomly. Walk to a few desks and check before moving on. A correct solid diagram is a regular grid of closely-spaced circles. A correct gas diagram has widely-spaced circles scattered across the space. Liquid is somewhere between — randomly arranged but close.
Beat 2 — Phase changes: six names
Show
Board: Draw the phase change diagram with arrows between SOLID, LIQUID, GAS. Label sublimation and deposition arrows. Leave the other four labels blank for students to fill in.
Say
  • "A phase change happens when matter moves from one state to another."
  • "Temperature changes — particle speed changes — arrangement changes."
  • "Six phase changes. Four you need to name from memory. Two I'll tell you."
  • "Solid → Liquid: melting. Liquid → Solid: freezing. Liquid → Gas: evaporation. Gas → Liquid: condensation."
  • "Sublimation (solid → gas directly) and deposition (gas → solid directly) — these are given."
Cue
★ Write: Fill in the phase change table — [melting / freezing / evaporation / condensation]. Then circle heating changes and box cooling changes on the diagram.
Beat 3 — Conservation of mass
Show
Board: 100 g ice → melts → ___ g water → evaporates → ___ g water vapour
Say
  • "When matter changes state, mass is conserved. Only the arrangement and speed of particles change."
  • "100 g of ice melts. How many grams of water? 100. Exactly. No exceptions."
  • "That water evaporates. How many grams of vapour? Still 100."
  • "The particles are the same particles — just moving differently and arranged differently."
Cue
★ Write: "When matter changes state, [mass] is conserved. Only the [arrangement] and [speed] of particles change." Fill in: 100 g → melts → [100] g → evaporates → [100] g. "Volume, however, [does] change."
Flag — Misconception: "When ice melts, some mass disappears."
Mass is NEVER created or destroyed during a phase change. The ice and the puddle weigh exactly the same. What changes is the arrangement and the volume — not the number of particles or their mass. If water seems to "disappear" when it evaporates, it has become water vapour in the air — still carrying the same mass. Use the sealed tin thought experiment: seal a pot and boil — then open it. Mass of the system is unchanged.
Section 2 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Solid: particles tightly packed in regular arrangement, vibrate in place; Gas: particles widely spaced, fast-moving and random. (Must use "spacing" and "movement.")
  2. (a) Freezing (b) Evaporation
  3. 75 g. Mass is conserved during a phase change — melting changes particle arrangement, not the number of particles or their total mass.
  4. Gas particles have so much energy and space between them that they move fast and randomly, filling all available space. Solid particles are in a fixed arrangement with little kinetic energy — they cannot move past each other.
  5. (Stretch) Steam is water vapour — it still has mass. To prove the student wrong: seal a container before boiling. Measure total mass before and after. The mass of the system is unchanged — the water became vapour and was trapped. The vapour "floating away" is still matter — just in a different state.

Section 3: Thermometers and Temperature

Learning target: I can explain how a thermometer works using the particle model, read the Celsius scale, and identify key reference points and safety practices.
Timing: ~18 minutes direct instruction
Bring: thermometers, beakers of hot and cold water.

Beat 1 — Celsius scale reference points
Show
Board: Number line or thermometer diagram. Mark: 0°C (water freezes), 100°C (water boils), 37°C (body temperature), ~20°C (room temperature), −20°C (cold Alberta day).
Say
  • "The Celsius scale is calibrated to water. Two reference points — both based on what water does at specific temperatures."
  • "0°C: water changes from liquid to solid — it freezes. This is reproducible in any lab in the world."
  • "100°C: water changes from liquid to gas — it boils. Also reproducible."
  • "These reference points were chosen because they are reliable — they always happen at the same temperature under standard conditions."
Cue
★ Write: "Water [freezes] at 0°C / Water [boils] at 100°C." Fill in the reference points table in notes. These two are the scale's anchors.
Beat 2 — How a thermometer works
Show
Board: Two thermometer diagrams — COLD (low liquid level) and HOT (high liquid level). Label: "particle speed" and "liquid volume" for each.
Say
  • "A liquid thermometer works because of the particle model — specifically, thermal expansion."
  • "When the thermometer gets hotter: liquid particles move faster and spread further apart. The liquid expands — it rises up the tube."
  • "When the thermometer gets colder: liquid particles slow down and come closer together. The liquid contracts — it falls down the tube."
  • "The scale on the glass shows what temperature the liquid level matches."
Cue
★ Write: "When temperature [increases] → liquid particles move faster → liquid [expands] and rises. When temperature [decreases] → liquid particles slow → liquid [contracts] and falls." Fill in: "The thermometer works because the [volume] of the liquid changes predictably with temperature."
Beat 3 — Safety practices (rapid)
Say
  • "Five safety rules — I say them, you write them. These are the ones that appear on every lab sheet."
  • Never squeeze — hold gently, can crack under pressure.
  • Never stir with it — it is not a stirring rod.
  • If it breaks — do not touch the glass or liquid; notify the teacher.
  • Read at eye level — not from above or below. Parallax error.
  • Wait for the reading to stabilise before recording.
Cue
★ Write: Fill in the five safety blanks in notes. Allow 2 minutes.
Flag — Parallax
Demonstrate parallax with a ruler and a pencil held off the surface. Students who read from above see a higher reading than the actual level. This is a real source of lab error — and a question that regularly appears on assessments. A quick demo is more effective than a verbal explanation.
Section 3 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. 0°C (water freezes / liquid → solid) and 100°C (water boils / liquid → gas)
  2. When temperature increases, the liquid particles move faster and spread further apart (thermal expansion). The liquid takes up more volume and rises in the tube. The reading on the scale shows the height of the liquid level.
  3. Parallax error — looking down at an angle makes the liquid level appear higher than it actually is.
  4. The thermometer is not a stirring rod — using it to stir can crack the glass or break the seal, releasing the liquid inside.
  5. (Stretch) Calibrated to water means the scale was built using water's behaviour as the anchors — 0°C and 100°C are defined by what water does. This is useful for science because water is a common subject of experiments. It is useful for everyday life because human body temperature (37°C), room temperature (~20°C), and freezing outdoor conditions (0°C) all fall in a meaningful range on the same scale.

Section 4: Expansion, Contraction, and the Water Anomaly

Learning target: I can explain why most materials expand when heated, describe real-world applications, explain why water is an exception, and explain why ice floats.
Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction
Bring: ice cubes in a clear beaker, photo of bridge expansion joint.

Beat 1 — The general rule
Show
Board: GENERAL RULE: Heated → expand. Cooled → contract. Draw particle diagrams side by side: close-packed (cold) vs. spread-apart (hot).
Say
  • "The particle model makes a clear prediction: heat a material → particles move faster → spread further apart → material takes up more space. It expands."
  • "Cool a material → particles slow down → come closer together → material takes up less space. It contracts."
  • "This applies to solids, liquids, and gases — though gases expand far more dramatically."
Cue
★ Write: "When [heated] → particles move faster → material [expands]. When [cooled] → particles slow → material [contracts]."
Beat 2 — Engineering applications
Show
Board: Draw bridge deck cross-section with gaps. Label: expansion joints. Summer: gap gets [smaller]. Winter: gap gets [larger].
Say
  • "Engineers must account for expansion and contraction. A material that is fixed rigidly in place and cannot expand will bend, crack, or buckle."
  • "Expansion joints in bridges — those small gaps between sections — allow the concrete and steel to expand in summer and contract in winter without destroying each other."
  • "Power lines hang lower in summer — the wires expand and sag. Tighter in winter — they contract. Line engineers calculate this when setting tower heights."
  • "Railway tracks — traditional tracks have small gaps. If no gaps, a hot day would buckle the rails."
Cue
★ Write: "Without expansion joints, the force of expansion would [buckle / damage / break] the bridge." Fill in power lines and railway track blanks.
Beat 3 — The water anomaly: the experiment
Show
Hold up an ice cube in a clear beaker of water. Watch it float.
Say
  • "Here's a question. For almost every material, the solid is denser than the liquid — solid sinks in its liquid. Makes sense from the particle model: packed tightly = more mass per volume = higher density."
  • "What does water do? Watch." [Drop ice cube in water.]
  • "Ice floats. This means ice is less dense than liquid water. This is the water anomaly — water breaks the general rule."
  • "If the particle model predicts solid is denser, and water contradicts this prediction — what does that mean?"
  • Pause 3–4 seconds. "It means we need to update the model. We add information about water's crystal structure."
Flag — Misconception: "Ice is denser than liquid water — solids are always denser."
For most materials, this is true — but water is a major exception. Ice is less dense because of the crystal structure it forms when freezing: a hexagonal lattice that pushes particles further apart, not closer together. This is counterintuitive. Let students see the ice float before explaining. Observation before explanation is more effective for counterintuitive content.
Beat 4 — Why water is different: crystal structure
Show
Board: Two particle diagrams — LIQUID WATER (random, close) vs. ICE (hexagonal, more space between particles). Label: "Density: higher" vs. "Density: lower (ice floats)".
Say
  • "In liquid water, particles are randomly arranged and close together."
  • "When water freezes, particles slow down and lock into a hexagonal crystal structure."
  • "That crystal structure pushes the particles further apart — creating more space, not less."
  • "More space for the same number of particles = lower density. Ice is less dense than liquid water."
  • "So ice floats."
Cue
★ Write: "When water freezes, particles lock into a [hexagonal crystal] structure that actually pushes them [further apart] — creating more space." Ice is [less] dense than liquid water. This is why ice [floats].
Beat 5 — Why the anomaly matters
Say
  • "Why does this matter? Aquatic life in winter."
  • "When a lake cools in winter, ice forms at the surface and floats. The liquid water underneath stays at approximately 4°C — cold, but not frozen."
  • "Fish, plants, and other organisms survive in the liquid layer below the ice."
  • "If ice sank — if it were denser — lakes and rivers would freeze solid from the bottom up. Most aquatic life would not survive Alberta winters."
  • "FNMI communities in northern Alberta — Cree, Dene, Métis — have observed and documented these seasonal freeze-thaw cycles for generations. They track when ice is thick enough for travel, how it changes near currents, what early melt signals about the season. This is systematic, long-term observation — aligned with scientific data collection."
Cue
★ Write: Ice forms on the [surface] and floats. The water below stays at approximately [4°C]. If ice sank, lakes would freeze [solid] and most aquatic life would not survive.
Section 4 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. When a material is heated, its particles move faster and spread further apart — the material expands (takes up more space).
  2. Expansion joints are small gaps between sections of bridge deck. They allow the bridge material to expand in heat and contract in cold without the pressure cracking or buckling the structure.
  3. Ice is less dense than liquid water — it has lower density, so it floats.
  4. Ice is less dense than liquid water, so it floats on the surface. The liquid water below stays unfrozen at approximately 4°C. Fish can survive in the liquid layer beneath the ice during winter.
  5. (Stretch) No — this is not a correct critique. The water anomaly does not disprove the particle model. The particle model says particles spread apart when heated and come closer when cooled. Water mostly follows this. The crystal structure that forms when water freezes is an additional piece of information that explains why the solid is less dense — the model is extended, not broken. Science works exactly this way: a surprising observation prompts an update to the model. The ice-floats observation led scientists to discover the hexagonal crystal structure. That's the model working correctly.

Unit Synthesis

~12 minutes — notes closed, then compare to pre-knowledge check

Show
Board: Four section headers — blank lines beside each.
Say
  • "Notes closed. Four sections. One sentence each — the single most important idea from memory."
  • Allow 5–7 minutes. Circulate. Watch for: vague sentences ("matter is made of stuff") vs. content sentences ("the particle model says particles always move and their speed changes with temperature").
Cue
↩ Check: After writing from memory, open notes and check. "Return to your Pre-Knowledge Check. Read what you wrote before the unit started. What did you get right? What were you wrong about? What surprised you?" Allow 3–5 minutes quietly.
