Section 1 — The Solar System (Q1–Q8)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q1 Eight planets in order · fill-in list 1. Mercury · 2. Venus · 3. Earth · 4. Mars · 5. Jupiter · 6. Saturn · 7. Uranus · 8. Neptune
Q2 Emits or reflects light · table fill-in Star = emits light · Planet = reflects light · Moon (natural satellite) = reflects light · Asteroid = reflects light · Comet = reflects light · Dwarf planet = reflects light. Only the Sun (a star) produces its own light; all other solar system objects reflect sunlight.
Q3 Distance units in space · fill-in 1 AU = average distance from Earth to the Sun = about 150 million km. A light-year = about 9.46 trillion km. We use these units because space is too big to measure in kilometres.
Q4 Planet vs dwarf planet · table fill-in Row 1: planet — has cleared its orbit — example: Earth OR Mars. Row 2: dwarf planet — has not cleared its orbit — example: Pluto.
Q5 Pluto reclassification — why right · word bank + explain Right decision because: scientists found new criteria and applied evidence. Pluto did not clear its orbit — it shares the Kuiper Belt with many similar objects. When new evidence emerges, classification changed — that is a strength of science, not a weakness. Accept any response citing at least one factual reason.
Q6 What is a satellite? Natural + artificial examples · fill-in A satellite is any object that orbits another object. Natural: 1. Earth's Moon · 2. Phobos (Mars's moon). Artificial: 1. ISS · 2. Hubble Space Telescope OR GPS satellite. Accept any two natural and two artificial examples from the WB.
Q7 Three things satellites do · word bank (choose 3) Any three from: GPS navigation · weather forecasting · internet service · military surveillance · environmental monitoring · communication.
Q8 Scale of the solar system · fill-in The solar system is enormous. It spans billions of kilometres. Even light takes 8 minutes to travel from the Sun to Earth.

Q10 Three astronaut challenges · word bank (choose 3) Any three from: microgravity · radiation · isolation · muscle loss · bone density loss · communication delays · limited food and water.
Q11 Ex-Alta 1 — why meaningful · word bank + explain It is meaningful because: students made a real contribution to space science — they not just learning — doing. Accept any response capturing that it was a real scientific contribution, not a simulation.
Q12 Space probe vs telescope · table fill-in Telescope: where = stays on Earth (or in Earth orbit); how = observes from far away. Space probe: where = travels to other planets; how = collects data up close.
Q13 Challenges observing distant stars · word bank + explain One challenge (any one): stars are very faint at great distances · light years away so the light is old · atmospheric distortion blurs images · light pollution hides faint stars · light takes time to arrive so we see old light. It matters because: we are seeing the past — the star might not look the same (or exist) now; the light we receive is very old light.
Q14 Why send rovers instead of astronauts · word bank (choose 2) Any two from: safer (no human lives at risk) · cheaper (no life support, no return trip) · no life support needed · can stay longer · rovers are not harmed by radiation · 7-month journey is risky for humans · no food or water needed.
Q15 Two spinoff technologies · word bank (choose 2) Any two from: memory foam · scratch-resistant lenses · water filters · cordless tools · freeze-dried food · camera phones · GPS.
Q16 Looking far = looking back in time · word bank + explain Looking far away means looking back in time because: light takes time to travel. Light from objects billions of years away has been traveling for billions of years. When we observe it, we are seeing old light — what existed long ago. We are literally looking into the past. At the speed of light, there is no way to see an object's present state if it is very distant.

Section 3 — Applying and Evaluating (Q17–Q24)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q17 Indigenous sky knowledge — two connections · word bank Any two from: both track star positions and patterns over time · both used for navigation · both observe seasons and annual cycles through the sky · both study constellations / patterns in the night sky · traditional ecological knowledge includes detailed astronomical observation that overlaps with modern astronomy.
Q18 Moon glows? · misconception + word bank The student is not correct. What we actually see: the Moon reflects sunlight — it does not produce its own light. The side of the Moon facing the Sun is illuminated. We see the lit side that faces us.
Q19 Moon phases causes · fill-in Moon phases happen because the Moon orbits Earth. As it orbits, we see different amounts of its sunlit side. When we see none of the lit side: new moon. When we see the full lit side: full moon.
Q20 Inner vs outer planets · table fill-in Material: inner = rocky; outer = gas giants. Size: inner = small; outer = large. Distance from Sun: inner = close to sun; outer = far from sun.
Q21 Life on Mars — conditions needed · word bank + circle Any three conditions: liquid water · energy source · carbon-based molecules · suitable temperature · protection from radiation · atmosphere. Circle: Partly. Explanation: Mars has some evidence of past water, a thin atmosphere, and some carbon-based molecules, but no confirmed liquid water today and high radiation levels — so conditions are partial, not fully present.
Q22 Space exploration — for/against · word bank Argument for (any one): spinoff technology benefits everyday life · scientific discovery advances knowledge · inspiration for future scientists · long-term resource potential · national security. Argument against (any one): costly · money for health or poverty on Earth is more urgent.
Q23 Computational thinking in solar system models · word bank + explain This is computational thinking because: a model uses an algorithm (mathematical rules for gravity and velocity) and data (current positions, masses) to simulate and predict future positions. Abstraction simplifies the real solar system to what is needed for the calculation. The model represents orbital mechanics mathematically.
Q24 Europa mission design · table fill-in Any three from the WB with a stated purpose: spacecraft (transport to Europa) · ice drill (penetrate ice shell to reach ocean) · water sampler (collect and analyse water for signs of life) · communication system (send data back to Earth) · power source (run instruments far from Sun) · radiation shielding (protect electronics in Jupiter's intense radiation belt) · cameras/sensors (observe and measure environment). Award marks for any three technologies with correct purposes.

Stretch Zone (S1–S4)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
S1 Scale model calculation · open response Scale factor: Sun model (22 cm) ÷ real Sun (1.4 million km) ≈ 1.57 × 10⁻⁸ cm per km. Earth diameter (12,700 km) × scale factor ≈ 0.02 cm ≈ a small pea (about 2 mm). Earth distance from Sun (150 million km) × scale factor ≈ 235 cm ≈ about 2.4 m from the basketball Sun. Award marks for setting up the proportion correctly and showing a reasonable estimate, even if arithmetic varies slightly.
S2 Voyager 1 distance in AU · calculation + interpretation 23 billion km ÷ 150 million km per AU ≈ 153 AU. This tells us: the space beyond our solar system is vastly larger than the solar system itself (Neptune is ~30 AU). Even after 45+ years of travel, Voyager has barely reached the nearest stars — the nearest star is ~268,000 AU away. Award marks for correct division and a meaningful interpretation of the scale.
S3 40-light-year exoplanet — communication challenge · open response Challenges: signals take 40 years one way (80 years for a reply) — no real-time communication possible. Travel time at current speeds would be tens of thousands of years. We cannot know what conditions are like there now — we see 40-year-old information. Any mission would last far longer than a human lifetime. Accept any response capturing: the communication delay, the travel time, and the impossibility of real-time interaction.
S4 Indigenous star knowledge + astronomy collaboration · open response Indigenous astronomers contribute: long-term observations (centuries of records) of star patterns, seasonal cycles, and specific objects — filling gaps in written scientific records. Modern astronomers contribute: instrumentation, mathematical modelling, and global data networks. Benefits for both: Indigenous knowledge adds historical depth and local specificity; modern science provides tools to verify and extend that knowledge. Mutual respect leads to a more complete picture of the sky. Award marks for addressing benefits to both groups.
