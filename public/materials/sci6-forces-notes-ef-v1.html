Section 1 — What Is a Force?

A force is a push or a pull.

Forces make things move, stop, or change shape.

You cannot always see a force. But you can see what it does.

Two things every force has

  • Size (magnitude) — how big the push or pull is. We measure this in Newtons (N).
  • Direction — which way the force is pushing or pulling.

Force diagrams

We use arrows to show forces. A longer arrow means a bigger force. The arrow points in the direction of the force.

→→→→ (big force, pushing right) → (small force, pushing right) ← (force pushing left)

Section 2 — Forces from Outside

External forces come from outside an object. They act ON the object.

There are three types you need to know.

1. Applied force

This is a direct push or pull from a person or another object.

Example: You push a box across the floor. That is an applied force.

2. Friction

Friction acts between two surfaces that are touching.

Friction always acts in the opposite direction to motion. It slows things down.

Example: A book sliding across a desk slows down because of friction.

3. Elastic force

Elastic force pulls something back to its original shape.

Example: A stretched elastic band pulls back when you let go.

Section 3 — Forces Inside Things

Internal forces happen inside an object. They are caused by external forces acting on the object.

When you push or pull something, forces build up inside the material. There are four types.

The four internal forces

ForceWhat it meansExample
Tension A pulling apart force. The material is being stretched. A rope being pulled from both ends.
Compression A pushing together force. The material is being squished. The legs of a chair holding your weight.
Shear Two parts being pushed in opposite directions, side by side. Scissors cutting paper.
Torsion A twisting force. Wringing out a wet cloth.

Section 4 — Elastic and Plastic Materials

When a force acts on something, it may change shape. This is called deformation.

There are two types of deformation.

Elastic deformation

The material bounces back to its original shape when the force is removed.

Example: A stretched elastic band bounces back when you let go.

Plastic deformation

The material stays in its new shape. It does not go back.

Example: A bent paper clip stays bent.

Section 5 — Newton's Third Law

Newton's Third Law says:

When you push on something, it pushes back on you — with the same size force, in the opposite direction.

These two forces are called an action-reaction pair.

Examples

  • You push on a wall. The wall pushes back on you.
  • A rocket pushes gas down. The gas pushes the rocket up.
  • You push off a skateboard. The skateboard pushes you forward.
You push wall → ← Wall pushes you back [YOU] [WALL] Same size force. Opposite directions. But they act on DIFFERENT objects.
Word bank: equal  ·  opposite  ·  different  ·  same

Action and reaction forces are in size.

They act in directions.

They act on objects — NOT on the same one.

🔑 Word to know
Newton's Third Law — for every action force, there is a reaction force that is equal in size and opposite in direction.
🔑 Word to know
Action-reaction pair — the two forces that always come together. Push and push-back.
⚠️ Watch out
Action and reaction forces do NOT cancel each other out.
They act on different objects. Forces can only cancel when they act on the same object.
⚠️ Watch out
A bigger or heavier object does NOT push harder.
The forces are always equal — no matter the size.
💡 Remember
Push → pushes back. Same size. Opposite direction. Always on different objects.
