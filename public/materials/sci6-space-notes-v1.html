Pluto — why was it reclassified?

For most of the 20th century, Pluto was called the 9th planet. In 2006, the International Astronomical Union (IAU) officially reclassified it as a  .

Emit vs. reflect light

  means to produce and send out light. The Sun does this — it generates its own energy.

  means to bounce back light that came from somewhere else. Planets, moons, and asteroids do this — they are visible because the Sun's light hits them and bounces toward our eyes.

This is why you can see planets in the night sky even though they are not on fire — they are   sunlight, not producing it.

Scale — why diagrams lie

Every diagram of the solar system you have ever seen is   to scale. Textbook diagrams make the planets look large and close together. The real solar system is almost entirely   space.

If the Sun were a basketball (about 24 cm across), the Earth would be a pinhead roughly 30 metres away. Neptune — the outermost planet — would be about 1 kilometre away.

On that same scale, the planets would each be smaller than a grain of sand.

Scientific Methods — Scale models

A scale model shows accurate proportions. A diagram that is NOT to scale can still show order and relationships — but it cannot tell you about   or   size. Always ask: "Is this model to scale? What can I — and can I NOT — learn from it?"

Misconception — "The textbook diagram shows the solar system is fairly compact"

Textbook diagrams are NOT to scale. They compress enormous distances to fit on a page. In reality, the solar system is almost entirely empty space. If the planets were shown at their true distances from the Sun, most of the diagram would be blank. The order of the planets is correct in these diagrams — the distances are not.

Misconception — "Planets are visible at night because they produce their own light"

Planets do NOT produce their own light. They reflect light from the Sun. The difference matters: a star is massive enough for nuclear fusion to occur — the process that generates light and heat. Planets are not. When you see Venus, Mars, or Jupiter in the night sky, you are seeing reflected sunlight bouncing off their surfaces or atmospheres — not light they are generating themselves.

Indigenous Sky Knowledge

Indigenous peoples across what is now Canada have long, rich traditions of sky observation. Cree, Blackfoot, and many other First Nations have names and stories for stars, constellations, and seasonal sky patterns. This knowledge was not casual — it was systematic and practical: used for navigation, tracking seasons, timing planting and harvesting, and understanding the year's cycles. This is a form of long-term scientific observation. It developed over generations of careful watching and record-keeping. Western astronomy and Indigenous sky knowledge are two knowledge systems that have observed the same sky — each producing frameworks that are meaningful, purposeful, and grounded in sustained attention to the world above.

Ex-Alta 1 — Alberta's satellite

Ex-Alta 1 is a small satellite called a CubeSat. It was designed and built by students at the University of Alberta. It was launched into orbit in  .

Ex-Alta 1 orbits   and collects data on the upper atmosphere — specifically on space weather events that affect Earth's magnetic field.

This matters because: (1) it shows that satellite technology is not only for large space agencies — students with the right knowledge and tools can build technology that serves a real scientific purpose; (2) it is an example of a computational artifact — a human-built system designed to collect data from an environment we cannot directly access, transmit it back to Earth, and contribute to scientific knowledge.

Connection to Computer Science — Ex-Alta 1 as a designed system

What problem does Ex-Alta 1 solve? It extends human sensing into the upper atmosphere — a region we cannot send people. What data does it collect? Measurements of charged particles and magnetic field changes linked to solar storms. How does it transmit data back? By radio signal — data is encoded, sent to a ground station, decoded, and analyzed.

This is the same logic behind every remote sensing system: a sensor in a hard-to-reach place, connected to a human-readable output through a data pipeline. The satellite does not think — it is designed to sense, encode, and transmit. The meaning comes from the scientists interpreting the data on the ground.

How does a satellite stay in orbit?

Satellites do not need engines running constantly to stay in orbit. A satellite moves   so fast that as it falls toward Earth, Earth's surface   away beneath it at the same rate. The result is that it keeps falling in a  .

Think of it this way: if you threw a ball perfectly horizontally, it would curve down and hit the ground. Throw it faster — it travels farther before hitting. Throw it fast enough, and the curve of Earth falls away at the same rate the ball drops. The ball never hits the ground. That is an orbit.

Satellites do not   in place above Earth. They are always falling — they just keep   the curve of Earth.

Science is self-correcting — the case of Pluto

Pluto was called a planet from its discovery in 1930 until 2006. Scientists reclassified Pluto because they had   data. When powerful telescopes revealed many similar-sized icy bodies in the Kuiper Belt, it became clear that Pluto was not a unique planet. The old definition of "planet" did not   this new evidence. So scientists updated the definition.

Changing a classification when evidence demands it is not failure. It is exactly what science is   to do.

Scientific Methods — self-correction

Science makes progress by being willing to update. A scientist who refuses to change their view when new evidence arrives is not doing science well. A scientist who updates their understanding when evidence demands it is. The reclassification of Pluto is a model of science working correctly: new data → better classification system → more accurate understanding of the solar system.

Misconception — "Scientists changed the rules to be unfair to Pluto"

The reclassification of Pluto was not arbitrary. By the early 2000s, astronomers had discovered dozens of similarly sized icy objects beyond Neptune. If Pluto was a planet, so were all of them — which would have meant adding many new planets to the list. Instead, the International Astronomical Union created a clear three-condition definition and applied it consistently. Pluto does not meet condition 3 (clearing its orbital neighbourhood). Updating a classification when new evidence changes the picture is a sign of healthy science — not unfairness to a rock.
