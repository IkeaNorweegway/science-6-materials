Teaching Stance for This Unit

Energy Resources is the most values-laden unit in Grade 6 Science. Students arrive with strong opinions about fossil fuels and renewable energy. Your job is to ground those opinions in evidence. When a student says "we should just use renewables," ask: "Which selection factors support that? Which ones create challenges?" When a student says "oil is bad," ask: "What do the five factors tell us?"

There is no correct answer in the Alberta town scenario — there are only trade-offs with evidence. Model this: "Based on the evidence in these five factors, I lean toward X — but I recognize these trade-offs." Do not take a position on which resource is best. Your position is: "What do the factors tell you?"

Pre-Knowledge Check — Facilitation Guide

Show

Board: Pre-Knowledge Check — Energy Resources
Open-ended prompt on board or paper. Five lines for writing.

Say

"Write everything you think you know about energy resources. What is an energy resource? Where do we get our energy from? What is the difference between renewable and non-renewable? What energy resources does Alberta use?"

"No marks. No correction. Be honest. We'll return to this at the end of the unit."

Cue — ★ Write

Students write for 4–5 minutes. Watch for: who says "renewable = clean"? Who knows anything about Alberta oil sands or wind energy? Who can distinguish renewable from non-renewable accurately?

Misconception Flag — Pre-K Baseline: Watch For
  • "Renewable = clean / no environmental impact" — solar panels use toxic materials; hydro floods ecosystems; wind affects birds. This is the unit's primary misconception.
  • "Before processing = renewable, after = non-renewable" — completely independent categories. Address in Section 2.
  • "We should just switch to all renewables immediately" — five-factor analysis is your tool for grounding this. Don't argue against it, apply the framework.

Section 1 — What Are Energy Resources?

Lessons 1–2 · Student notes: Section 1

Beat 1.1 — Definitions

Show

Board: ENERGY RESOURCE = anything we use to do work, produce heat, or generate electricity.
Two categories: RENEWABLE | NON-RENEWABLE
Under each: leave blank for student input, then complete together.

Say

"An energy resource is anything we use to do work, produce heat, or generate electricity. Everything that powers your life — your house, your school, your phone — runs on energy resources."

"Two categories. Renewable resources are replenished naturally — replaced by natural processes fast enough that we don't run out of them. Wind keeps blowing. Sunlight keeps arriving. Moving water keeps moving."

"Non-renewable resources were formed over millions of years. Once we use them, they cannot be replaced in a human lifetime. Coal, oil, natural gas — compressed ancient organic matter."

"Blanks: energy resource = do [work] · produce [heat] · generate [electricity]. Renewable: replenished [naturally]. Non-renewable: formed over [millions] of years."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: do [work] · produce [heat] · generate [electricity] · replenished [naturally] · [millions] of years. Vocabulary table: Energy resource, Renewable resource, Non-renewable resource.

Beat 1.2 — Renewable vs. Non-Renewable Examples

Show

Board: Two-column table — Renewable | Non-Renewable
Renewable: Wind · Solar · Hydro · Geothermal · Biomass
Non-renewable: Coal · Oil (petroleum) · Natural gas · Uranium

Say

"Five renewable resources — wind, solar, hydro, geothermal, biomass. Four non-renewable — coal, oil, natural gas, uranium."

"Note uranium — it powers nuclear plants. Uranium is non-renewable: there is a limited supply in the ground. Nuclear energy from uranium is sometimes presented as a 'clean' alternative to fossil fuels because it produces almost no CO₂. That's true — but uranium itself is finite."

"Daily uses table: students fill from class input. Heating a home → natural gas, wood. Cooking → natural gas, electricity. Transportation → oil (gasoline/diesel). Lighting → electricity (from various sources). Manufacturing → electricity, natural gas."

Cue — ★ Write

Students copy the renewable/non-renewable table. Daily uses table completed with class input. Vocabulary: Fossil fuel, Uranium.

Beat 1.3 — Alberta Context

Show

Board or map: Alberta energy profile
• Oil sands: northern Alberta — one of world's largest oil deposits
• Wind farms: southern Alberta — consistent high winds
• Coal phase-out: 2018 announcement → 2023 phase-out complete
• Growing solar and wind sectors

Say

"Alberta has historically relied on oil and natural gas — fossil fuels found in large amounts beneath the province. The oil sands in northern Alberta are one of the world's largest oil deposits."

"Alberta is also growing its solar and wind energy sectors. Southern Alberta has some of the best wind resources in Canada — large wind farms operate there where winds are high and consistent."

"In 2018, Alberta began a coal phase-out — a plan to phase out coal-fired electricity generation. By 2023, coal is no longer used to generate electricity in Alberta."

"Blanks: Alberta relied on [oil] and [natural gas] · growing [solar] and [wind] sectors · coal [phase-out] · by [2023] coal gone."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: relied on [oil] and [natural gas] · growing [solar] and [wind] · coal [phase-out] · by [2023]. Connection prompt written.

Beat 1.4 — Misconception: Renewable ≠ Clean

Show

Board: RENEWABLE ≠ CLEAN
Three examples of renewable resource environmental impacts:
• Hydro dams → flood river ecosystems
• Wind turbines → affect bird migration, land use
• Solar panels → manufacturing uses toxic materials

Say

"Here is the misconception I want to address directly. Renewable means the resource is replenished naturally and will not run out. It does NOT mean the resource is environmentally harmless."

"Large hydroelectric dams flood entire river ecosystems — the Three Gorges Dam in China flooded 1,000 villages and displaced over a million people. Wind turbines require land and affect bird and bat migration — in Alberta, wind farms affect prairie bird populations. Solar panel manufacturing uses toxic materials including cadmium and lead."

"Renewable and clean are separate ideas. A resource can be renewable without being clean. A resource can be relatively clean without being renewable — nuclear energy produces almost no CO₂ but uses non-renewable uranium."

"Blank in the misconception callout: renewable = replenished [naturally]."

Cue — ★ Write

Students read the misconception callout. Blank: replenished [naturally].

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Energy resource definition: Anything we use to do work, produce heat, or generate electricity.
  2. Two renewable + two non-renewable: Renewable — any two from: wind, solar, hydro, geothermal, biomass. Non-renewable — any two from: coal, oil, natural gas, uranium.
  3. What makes a resource renewable: It is replenished by natural processes fast enough that we don't run out of it. Natural processes replace it on a timescale humans can rely on.
  4. Replace coal in Alberta: Wind (already dominant in southern Alberta) or solar are both valid. Hydro possible but geography-dependent.
  5. Solar = renewable but not zero impact: Correct: solar is renewable — sunlight will not run out. Incorrect: solar has environmental impacts — manufacturing solar panels uses toxic materials (cadmium, lead), panels create e-waste at end of life, and large solar farms require significant land use. Renewable and clean are separate classifications.

Section 2 — Before and After Processing

Lessons 3–4 · Student notes: Section 2

Beat 2.1 — The Core Distinction

Show

Board: Two headings: BEFORE PROCESSING | AFTER PROCESSING
Before: "used directly in natural form — no conversion needed"
After: "converted into another form before we can use the energy"
Then: "These categories are INDEPENDENT from renewable/non-renewable."

Say

"Some energy resources can be used directly in their natural form — before any processing or conversion. A sail catches wind directly. A solar window lets sunlight in directly."

"Some must be converted into another form first — after processing — before we can use the energy. Crude oil must be refined into gasoline before it becomes useful fuel."

"Critical point: this does not map onto renewable vs. non-renewable. A resource can be renewable AND used before processing. A resource can be renewable AND used after processing. The two categories are completely independent."

"Blanks: directly [in natural form] / before [any processing] · converted [into another form] / after processing · the two categories are [independent]."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: used directly [in natural form] · must be [converted] · the two categories are [independent]. Vocabulary: Before processing, After processing, Conversion.

Beat 2.2 — Wind: The Key Example (Used Both Ways)

Show

Board: Side-by-side images or sketches — traditional windmill (grinding grain) vs. modern wind turbine (generating electricity).
Table: Wind | Before processing | After processing

Say

"The most important example in this section: wind is used both before and after processing."

"Before processing: a traditional windmill uses wind directly to turn a millstone — grinding grain — or pump water. The wind's kinetic energy directly does mechanical work. No conversion to a different form of energy."

"After processing: a modern wind turbine converts wind into electricity — a different form of energy. The turbine adds a conversion step: kinetic energy → electrical energy."

"Same resource. Same source. Two completely different ways of using it."

"Blanks: windmill uses wind [directly] to turn a millstone · wind [turbine] converts wind into electricity."

Cue — ★ Write

Wind table blanks: before = uses wind [directly] · after = wind [turbine] converts into electricity.

Beat 2.3 — Multiple Before/After Examples

Show

Board: Five-row table — Resource | Before | After:
Wood · Solar · Crude oil · Moving water · Biomass (grain)

Say

"Five examples — complete the table with me."

"Wood: before = burning logs directly to heat a room. After = converting wood to charcoal, which burns hotter and cleaner."

"Solar: before = a south-facing window lets sunlight warm a room directly. After = a photovoltaic (PV) cell converts sunlight into electricity."

"Crude oil: no before processing use — crude oil in its natural form is not useful as fuel. Only after: refined into gasoline, diesel, and other fuels."

"Moving water: before = a water wheel uses moving water directly to turn a millstone. After = a hydroelectric dam generates electricity."

"Biomass (grain): before = eaten directly as food, body extracts energy. After = fermented and distilled into ethanol fuel."

Cue — ★ Write

Table blanks: Wood after → [charcoal] · Solar before → [directly] · Solar after → [electricity] · Crude oil after → [gasoline] · Water before → [directly] · Water after → [hydroelectric] dam · Biomass after → [ethanol] fuel.

Misconception Flag — Before Processing = Renewable / After Processing = Non-Renewable

This is the most common error in this section. Students assume a pattern that doesn't exist. The misconception callout in the student notes makes this explicit — use it.

Key line: "The before/after categories and the renewable/non-renewable categories answer completely different questions."

Diagnostic question to use: "Is crude oil used before or after processing? Is crude oil renewable or non-renewable? Notice — those are two separate questions with two separate answers. The answers don't predict each other."

Blank in misconception callout: "Wind ([renewable]) can be used before (windmill) or after (turbine) processing."

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Before vs. after processing: Before = energy resource used directly in its natural form, no conversion. After = resource must first be converted into a different form of energy before use.
  2. Wind — before and after: Before = traditional windmill uses wind directly to grind grain or pump water. After = modern wind turbine converts wind's kinetic energy into electricity (a different form of energy).
  3. Crude oil — before or after: After processing only. Crude oil in its natural form is not useful as fuel. It must be refined into gasoline, diesel, and other products before it can be used for energy.
  4. Wood and solar from memory: Wood — before: burning logs directly to heat a room. After: converting to charcoal (burns hotter/cleaner). Solar — before: south-facing window lets sunlight warm a room directly. After: photovoltaic (PV) cell converts sunlight into electricity.
  5. Why "renewable = before processing" is wrong: Counter-example 1: Wind is renewable AND used after processing (wind turbine converts wind to electricity). Counter-example 2: Solar is renewable AND used after processing (PV cells convert sunlight to electricity). The before/after and renewable/non-renewable categories are independent — knowing one tells you nothing about the other.

Section 3 — Selecting Energy Resources

Lessons 5–7 · Student notes: Section 3

Beat 3.1 — Introducing the Five Factors

Show

Board: FIVE SELECTION FACTORS:
1. Availability
2. Societal impact
3. Economic impact
4. Environmental impact
5. Responsible management

Say

"Choosing which energy resource to use is not simple. Scientists, engineers, governments, and communities all weigh these factors when making energy decisions."

"Five factors. You need to know all five, what each one covers, and how to apply them. These are the tools you'll use in the Alberta town decision."

"Blank: weigh these [factors] when making energy decisions."

Cue — ★ Write

Students write the five factor names and begin vocabulary table.

Beat 3.2 — Factor 1: Availability

Show

Board: AVAILABILITY: Is enough of this resource present, where and when it is needed?
Three examples: wind (consistent region → use wind) · solar (low winter sun → challenges year-round) · hydro (needs a river).

Say

"Availability: is the resource present in sufficient quantity at the location? And is it available when you need it?"

"A region with consistent wind can use wind power effectively — southern Alberta qualifies. A region with little sunlight in winter may not get enough energy from solar panels year-round — many northern communities face this challenge. A community without a nearby river cannot use hydroelectric power."

"Availability is about geography and season. Blanks: sufficient [quantity] at the location · consistent [wind] · [solar] panels · nearby [river] · geography and [season]."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: sufficient [quantity] · consistent [wind] · [solar] panels · nearby [river] · geography and [season]. Vocabulary: Availability.

Beat 3.3 — Factor 2: Societal Impact

Show

Board: SOCIETAL IMPACT: How does this resource affect people and communities?
Four sub-factors: Jobs · Health effects · Public acceptance · Reliability

Say

"Societal impact covers how using a resource affects people. Four sub-factors."

"Jobs — does extracting or generating this energy create employment? The oil sands employ hundreds of thousands of Albertans."

"Health effects — does the resource benefit or harm nearby communities? Coal power plants produce particulates linked to respiratory illness. Wind farms may cause noise issues for nearby residents."

"Public acceptance — do people support or oppose this energy project in their community? A wind farm near a residential neighborhood vs. remote prairie — very different public reactions."

"Reliability — can this energy source deliver power consistently? Natural gas delivers consistent power 24/7. Wind and solar are intermittent — they only work when the wind blows or sun shines."

"Indigenous land stewardship: First Nations and Métis communities understand resources as held in trust for future generations — not simply extracted for present-day benefit. Many communities affected by oil sands development are now leaders in renewable energy development on their lands."

"Blanks: create [employment/jobs] · [health] effects · public [acceptance] · [reliability] · resources held in trust for [future] generations · leaders in [renewable] energy development."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: [employment/jobs] · [health] effects · public [acceptance] · [reliability] · held in trust for [future] generations · leaders in [renewable] energy. Vocabulary: Societal impact.

Beat 3.4 — Factor 3: Economic Impact

Show

Board: ECONOMIC IMPACT: What does it cost — to build, to maintain, to operate?
Three cost types: Install (capital cost) · Maintain (ongoing upkeep) · Operate (day-to-day running)
Key principle: look at the TOTAL cost over the full life of the project.

Say

"Economic impact covers costs — but not just upfront costs. Three types."

"Install cost — building a wind farm, installing solar panels, drilling for natural gas. This is often called capital cost."

"Maintain cost — ongoing upkeep and repairs. A wind turbine needs regular maintenance. A natural gas pipeline needs inspection and corrosion control."

"Operate cost — day-to-day running costs. Running a wind turbine costs almost nothing once built — the wind is free. Running a natural gas plant requires buying natural gas continuously."

"Key principle: look at the total cost over the full life of the project. A resource that is cheap to install may be expensive to operate. Solar has high install cost — but very low operating cost. Compare the full lifetime."

"Blanks: cost to [install] · [maintain] · [operate] · look at [total/lifetime] cost."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: cost to [install] · [maintain] · [operate] · [total / lifetime] cost. Vocabulary: Economic impact.

Beat 3.5 — Factor 4: Environmental Impact

Show

Board: ENVIRONMENTAL IMPACT: How does using this resource affect the environment?
Four sub-factors: Air quality · Land use · Ecosystem disruption · Water effects

Say

"Environmental impact: how does using this resource affect the environment? Four sub-factors."

"Air quality — releasing greenhouse gases or particulates into the air. Burning coal releases both CO₂ and particulates. Natural gas releases CO₂ but fewer particulates. Wind and solar generate electricity with no direct air emissions."

"Land use — how much land is disturbed or taken up? Oil sands extraction disturbs massive areas of boreal forest. Wind farms require large land areas but can be shared with farming."

"Ecosystem disruption — does extraction or infrastructure harm wildlife or ecosystems? Hydroelectric dams block fish migration. Wind turbines affect bird and bat populations."

"Water effects — does extraction affect rivers, groundwater, or lakes? Oil sands processing uses enormous amounts of water and produces toxic tailings ponds."

"Blanks: [air quality] / releasing GHGs · [land use] / how much disturbed · [ecosystem] disruption · water [effects / quality]."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: [air quality] · [land use] · [ecosystem] disruption · water [effects / quality]. Vocabulary: Environmental impact.

CS Connection — Technology Always Has Unintended Consequences

The CS connection in Factor 4 is about computational artifacts and unintended consequences. Smart grids, electric vehicles, and renewable infrastructure are designed systems that solve emissions problems but create new trade-offs:

  • Mining rare earth metals for batteries and solar panels disturbs ecosystems.
  • Rapid expansion of electric vehicles creates e-waste at end of battery life.
  • Connecting more renewable sources to the electrical grid can cause instability when supply and demand mismatch.

The CS principle: technology design is not neutral. Every solution creates new problems to monitor. This applies to any designed system — including software.

Blank in notes: connecting more renewable sources can cause [grid] instability.

Beat 3.6 — Factor 5: Responsible Management

Show

Board: RESPONSIBLE MANAGEMENT: How do we use the resource wisely over the long term?
Four sub-components: Conservation · Long-term supply · Monitoring · Future generations

Say

"Responsible management: how do we use the resource wisely over the long term? Four components."

"Conservation — using less energy overall, reducing waste. If we need less energy, every other decision becomes easier."

"Long-term supply — will this resource still be available and affordable in 50 years? Natural gas: limited supply, prices will rise. Wind: available indefinitely."

"Ongoing monitoring — using data to track the impacts of energy use and adjust as needed. This requires data collection and evidence-based decisions."

"Future generations — not locking in decisions that can't be reversed. Choosing a 50-year fossil fuel commitment forecloses options."

"This factor connects to Indigenous land stewardship: the land, water, and resources we use today are held in trust. What we take now affects what is available to those who come after us."

"This factor also requires scientific methods: data collection, evidence-based decisions, willingness to update when evidence changes."

"Blanks: [conservation] · long-term [supply] · [ongoing] monitoring · [future] generations · [data] collection · willingness to [update/revise] decisions."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: [conservation] · long-term [supply] · [ongoing] monitoring · [future] generations · [data] collection · willingness to [update / revise]. Vocabulary: Responsible management.

Beat 3.7 — Alberta Town Decision (Lesson 7)

Pacing Flag — Alberta Town Decision Needs Full Lesson

The Alberta town decision is the unit's summative application task. Give it a full lesson. Use it as a small group task — 3–4 students per group, each group working through all five factors before making a recommendation. Then whole-class debrief: what did different groups decide? What trade-offs did they see differently?

Critical facilitation move: when a group says "we chose wind because it's better," push: "Better by which factor? And what about the factors where it creates challenges?" Force them to stay in the evidence. There is no correct answer — there are only more or less evidenced arguments.

Show

Board or projected: The Alberta town decision table — three options (Natural gas / Wind / Solar) evaluated across all five factors.
Allow student notes to be open for this task.

Say

"A small Alberta town must choose how to generate electricity. Three options: natural gas, wind, solar. Your group will evaluate all three across all five factors — then make a recommendation with at least two reasons grounded in the factors."

"No single option is perfect. Every resource creates trade-offs. Your job is not to find the 'right' answer — your job is to make the most evidenced argument for your recommendation."

"When you write your recommendation, you must acknowledge at least one trade-off — something your chosen option does poorly. That's what distinguishes an evidence-based recommendation from an opinion."

Cue — ⊡ Think (group) then ★ Write (individual)

Groups discuss and analyse all five factors for all three options. Students write individual recommendations: which resource, at least two factor-based reasons, and at least one acknowledged trade-off.

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Five factors: Availability · Societal impact · Economic impact · Environmental impact · Responsible management.
  2. Wind farm in low-wind valley: Availability is the problem. The resource is not present in sufficient quantity at the location — low wind speeds mean a wind farm cannot generate enough electricity to be useful. No amount of money spent on turbines will compensate for inadequate wind.
  3. Economic vs. environmental impact: Economic = costs (install, maintain, operate, full lifetime cost). Environmental = effects on the natural environment (air quality, land use, ecosystem disruption, water effects). One is about money; one is about nature — they are different questions that sometimes point in different directions.
  4. Responsible management + Indigenous stewardship: Both treat resources as held in trust for future generations rather than as commodities to extract for present-day benefit. Both require thinking about long-term consequences, monitoring impacts, and not locking in irreversible decisions. Indigenous land stewardship principles — accumulated over generations — are aligned with what responsible management requires of any decision-maker.
  5. Why cheapest isn't enough: Economic impact (cheapest upfront) is only one of five factors. Environmental impact: a cheap resource may cause expensive environmental damage later (oil spills, pollution cleanup). Societal impact: cheap fossil fuels may create health costs and community opposition. Responsible management: cheap now may not be affordable or available in 50 years as fossil fuel supplies decline and prices rise. A complete decision requires all five factors.
