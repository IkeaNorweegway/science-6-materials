Energy Resources — Workbook Answer Key

Grade 6 Science · Alberta Curriculum 2023 · Q1–Q24 Core + S1–S4 Stretch

Core Questions — Q1 to Q15

# Concept / Type Answer / Marking Guide
Q1 ★ Sorting — renewable vs. non-renewable Renewable: wind · sunlight · moving water · geothermal heat · biomass (wood)
Non-renewable: coal · oil · natural gas
Key criterion: renewable = replaced naturally within a human lifetime.
Q2 ★ Before/after processing — wind Before processing: windmill · turns millstone directly · no conversion
After processing: wind turbine · converts wind into electricity
In own words: Before processing = energy used directly in its natural form; after processing = energy is first converted into a different form before use.
Q3 ★ Selection factors — availability Better location: Location A (southern Alberta plains)
Selection factor used: Availability
Why: A wind farm needs consistent, strong wind to generate electricity reliably. 28 km/h year-round produces predictable energy; 6 km/h variable does not.
Q4 ★★ Error analysis — renewable ≠ clean (misconception) Correct: Solar energy is renewable (the Sun continuously provides energy; it is replenished naturally).
Incorrect: "No negative effects / totally clean" — solar panel manufacturing uses toxic materials and energy; renewable describes the replenishment rate, not environmental impact.
Correct explanation: Solar is renewable because sunlight is continuously available. However, manufacturing solar panels may involve toxic materials and has environmental costs. Renewable and clean are not the same classification.
Q5 ★ Energy resources in Alberta daily life Heating a home: natural gas (most common in Alberta); also coal
Generating electricity: coal · natural gas · wind · solar · hydro · uranium
Fuelling a car/bus: oil (refined to gasoline or diesel)
Lighting: any electricity-generating resource
Replace coal for electricity: wind · solar · hydro · uranium (accept any one)
Q6 ★ Before/after processing — multiple examples Wood: burn logs directly → convert to charcoal
Solar energy: electricity
Crude oil: gasoline (or diesel)
Moving water: turns millstone directly → hydroelectric dam
Q7 ★★ Selection factors — five-factor comparison table Availability: Strong winds in southern Alberta year-round · limited in winter
Societal: new renewable industry jobs; some noise · minimal disruption; no noise/air pollution
Economic: rising long-term fuel costs · very low operating cost · very low operating cost
Environmental: CO₂ and methane · bird and bat mortality · toxic materials
Responsible management: supply will eventually run out · renewable if maintained · renewable if panels recycled/managed
Recommendation: accept any well-reasoned choice with two factor-based justifications.

2. renewable · quickly

Q9 ★★ Two explanations — before/after ≠ renewable/non-renewable Correct explanation: B
How you know: Wind is renewable AND used both ways — before processing in a traditional windmill, after processing in a wind turbine. Processing and renewable status are independent classification systems. Explanation A incorrectly claims processing determines renewability.
Q10 ★ Selection factors — economic calculation Natural gas: $40M + (20 × $8M) = $40M + $160M = $200M
Wind: $120M + (20 × $0.5M) = $120M + $10M = $130M
Cheaper over 20 years: wind
Selection factor: Economic impact
Q11 ★ Visual classification — renewable or non-renewable Oil pumped from beneath ground: Non-renewable — formed over millions of years; cannot be replaced in a human lifetime
Wind turbine: Renewable — wind is continuously replenished by nature
Coal burned in plant: Non-renewable — fossil fuel; not replaced in a human lifetime
Sunlight on solar panel: Renewable — continuously provided by the Sun
Natural gas from rock: Non-renewable — fossil fuel
River water through dam: Renewable — water cycle continuously replenishes it
Q12 ★★ FNMI stewardship — responsible management Part A: Factor: Responsible management. Holding resources "in trust for future generations" means not depleting them faster than they regenerate, tracking impacts, and leaving future generations with options — exactly what responsible management requires.
Part B: A First Nations community may prioritize the land's long-term health, spiritual relationship, and cultural continuity — not just economic returns. Their relationship to the land extends over many generations and includes obligations beyond financial benefit. Accept any specific, reasoned response.
Q13 ★ Before/after processing — sorting Before processing: windmill grinding grain using wind directly · wood fire heating a living room · water wheel turning a millstone using river flow
After processing: solar panel converting sunlight into electricity · crude oil refined into gasoline · fermented corn converted into ethanol
Q14 ★ Selection factors — environmental impact table Natural gas: releases CO₂ and methane · needs pipelines and a facility/plant · air quality affects ecosystem/animals near plant
Wind farm: produces no air emissions · needs a large area of open land · turbines can harm birds and bats
Selection factor: Environmental impact
Q15 ★★ Scientific methods — evidence-based energy decisions Part A: Data that might matter: cost per kilowatt-hour; CO₂ output per unit of energy; reliability/output in a given region. Accept any specific, relevant example.
Part B: Two factors beyond data: Societal impact (community values, jobs, health priorities) and Responsible management (long-term stewardship values, intergenerational equity). Also accept Environmental impact.
Part C: No single resource is "best" for all contexts. The right choice depends on what is available locally, what the community can afford, what values they hold, and what environmental trade-offs they accept. Evidence informs but does not determine the decision.

Core Questions — Q16 to Q24

# Concept / Type Answer / Marking Guide
Q16 ★ Before/after processing — Device A vs. B Device A: before processing
Device B: after processing
Key difference: Device A uses wind's mechanical energy directly to do work (turn a millstone). Device B converts wind's energy into electricity (a different form of energy) before it is used.
Q17 ★★ Selection factors — responsible management Part A: Factor: Responsible management (also accept Environmental impact). New evidence of serious pollution and GHG emissions demands revisiting the decision — keeping options open includes being willing to change course when evidence demands it.
Part B: Factor 1: Environmental impact — coal's health and climate harms create costs not reflected in the sunk cost of the plant. Factor 2: Societal impact — ongoing air quality harm to nearby residents outweighs the convenience of keeping a paid-for plant running. Accept any two well-applied factors.
Q18 ★ Renewable/non-renewable — Alberta context 1. Non-renewable
2. Fossil fuels formed over millions of years from ancient organisms; they cannot be replaced in a human lifetime even though they originated from living things.
3. Southern Alberta has consistently high wind speeds year-round (e.g., 25–30+ km/h on the plains) — strong, reliable Availability.
4. Yes; different resources can serve different purposes and regions simultaneously.
Q19 ★ Selection factors — remote community five-factor table Availability: must be shipped/trucked in · limited in winter
Societal: keeps some local trucking/transport jobs · reduces dependence on fuel deliveries
Economic: high cost per litre; rises with fuel prices · high installation/capital cost; low operating cost
Environmental: releases CO₂/emissions when burned · no emissions during operation
Responsible management: non-renewable; long-term supply at risk · reduces long-term dependence/risk
Recommendation: accept solar with at least one factor-based reason.
Q20 ★★ Error analysis — before/after ≠ renewable/non-renewable (diagram) Error 1: Wind turbine labeled as "Non-Renewable" — wrong. Wind turbines use wind, which is renewable.
Correction: Wind turbine belongs in the "Renewable" column.
Error 2: Solar panel labeled as "Non-Renewable" — wrong. Solar panels use sunlight, which is renewable.
Correction: Solar panel belongs in the "Renewable" column. The before/after processing distinction does not determine renewable status.
Q21 ★ Selection factors — societal impact situations Wind farm noise near town: Societal impact — affects quality of life for nearby residents
Coal plant closes; 300 workers lose jobs: Societal impact — affects employment and community economy
New gas plant; air quality worsens: Environmental impact (accept Societal if framed as community health) — air pollution affects ecosystem and human health
Q22 ★★ CS thread — technology trade-offs (EVs, smart grids) Part A: Intended benefit: EVs reduce greenhouse gas emissions by eliminating combustion / reduce dependence on fossil fuels for transportation.
Part B: Unintended consequence: Mining rare earth metals for EV batteries disturbs ecosystems / EVs create e-waste at end of battery life / grid instability from variable renewable supply.
Part C: No. A trade-off means weighing benefits against costs — not rejecting the technology. EVs still provide net benefit, but understanding unintended consequences allows for better design, regulation, and waste management. Every designed technology creates new problems even as it solves existing ones.
Q23 ★ Before/after processing — wood/charcoal 1. Wood is heated without full combustion, driving off moisture and volatile compounds — this is a chemical/thermal processing step (pyrolysis). It changes the form of the energy resource.
2. No. Processing does not change whether a resource is renewable. Renewability depends on replenishment rate, not the form the resource takes.
3. Renewable. To stay renewable, trees must be harvested no faster than they can regrow (sustainable forestry practices).
Q24 ★★ Selection factors — synthesis/refutation ("cost only") Accept any three well-applied factors. Model answers:
Environmental impact: A cheap resource (e.g., coal) may produce serious pollution — creating health and ecosystem costs not reflected in the purchase price.
Societal impact: A resource might be cheap but destroy jobs in an existing industry or harm community health.
Responsible management: A cheap non-renewable resource will eventually run out, leaving future generations without options.
Closing sentence: A complete energy decision weighs all five selection factors — availability, societal impact, economic impact, environmental impact, and responsible management — using evidence and community values to balance trade-offs.

Stretch Zone — S1 to S4

Stretch questions are open-ended. Mark for quality of reasoning and correct application of concepts — not for matching the model answer exactly.
# Concept / Type Answer / Marking Guide
S1 ★★ FNMI stewardship + responsible management + multi-factor (a) "We do not own the land; we hold it for our grandchildren" → Responsible management: the factor explicitly includes leaving options open for future generations, not depleting resources beyond replacement, and being willing to revisit decisions when evidence changes. The philosophy is a direct match.
(b) At least two other factors: Environmental impact — community health is tied to land health, and environmental harm affects the community directly; Societal impact — cultural continuity and employment are inseparable from land stewardship decisions; Availability — renewable projects ensure long-term access. Accept any two with clear explanation.
(c) Both are valid forms of knowledge. Long-term land observation is empirical evidence accumulated across generations — it is not less valid than instrument measurements; it provides temporal depth that short-term scientific records cannot. Neither is superior; they are complementary. Award marks for: explicitly acknowledging both as valid, explaining what each contributes, avoiding a hierarchy.
S2 ★★ Before/after processing + renewable/non-renewable + technology trade-offs (a) After processing — sunlight is converted into hydrogen fuel (a different form of energy) before being used for heating or appliances.
(b) Sunlight is renewable. Converting it to hydrogen does not change that — the source is continuously provided by the Sun. Renewability depends on the original source, not the conversion step.
(c) Intended benefit: Energy available at night or in winter → Environmental or Economic factor. Unintended consequence: Hydrogen storage infrastructure is expensive and has safety risks; production efficiency may be low → Economic or Environmental factor. Accept any coherent pair.
(d) Recommendation: yes, with reasoned application of at least three factors. Model: Availability — sun available in summer, hydrogen stored for winter; Environmental — no emissions during use; Economic — high upfront cost but eliminates diesel fuel cost; Responsible management — renewable, reduces long-term dependence on shipped diesel.
S3 ★★ All five selection factors + evidence-based decision-making Mark for: names a specific renewable resource(s); correctly applies all five factor names with relevant explanation; identifies at least one real trade-off (e.g., high upfront cost for wind; intermittency for solar; land use); describes a plausible evidence-based monitoring approach (e.g., track electricity output, measure CO₂ emissions annually, survey community satisfaction, compare costs year-over-year).
All five factor names must appear. Deduct if any factor is missing or misapplied.
S4 ★★ Full synthesis — identify and correct three errors Error 1: "Renewable = clean / no negative effects" → Wrong. Solar manufacturing uses toxic materials; wind turbines affect bird habitat. Renewable describes replenishment rate, not environmental impact.
Error 2: "Before processing = renewable; after processing = non-renewable" → Wrong. These are independent classification systems. Wind is renewable AND used both ways.
Error 3: "No trade-offs with renewables / switch everything right away" → Wrong. Every energy source has trade-offs (land use, wildlife, manufacturing, intermittency, upfront cost). Rapid transition also has societal and economic costs.
Closing sentence: Must reference evidence, values, and/or the five selection factors. Award full marks for: correctly identifying all three errors; using specific examples; addressing trade-offs; coherent closing sentence.
