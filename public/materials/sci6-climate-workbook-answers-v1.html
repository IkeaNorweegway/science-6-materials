Climate — Workbook Answer Key

Grade 6 Science · Model answers and marking guidance for all 30 core questions and 5 stretch questions

Core Questions — Q1 to Q30

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q1 ★ Weather vs. climate · sort Weather (short-term): It snowed in Edmonton on May 3; There was a thunderstorm in Calgary last Tuesday; The Bow River flooded in June 2013.
Climate (long-term): Alberta's average winter temperature has risen 2°C over 50 years; July 2023 was the hottest month on record globally.
Note: July 2023 record — some teachers may debate this; accept either column if student provides clear reasoning about timescale.
Q2 ★ Greenhouse effect · label diagram Arrow 1 (↓↓ from sun): sunlight (incoming).
Arrow 2 (↑↑ from surface): infrared heat (rising from surface).
Arrow 3 (↑↑ ↓↓ in atmosphere): heat re-emitted back toward Earth.
(Accept "heat escaping to space" for the upward ↑↑ portion of Arrow 3.)
Without greenhouse gases: Circle B — much colder. The natural greenhouse effect keeps Earth ~33°C warmer than it would otherwise be (~−18°C without it vs. ~+15°C with it).
Q3 ★ Evidence quality · weather ≠ climate Circle: No — this is not good evidence for a climate claim.
One cold March is a single weather event covering a few days or weeks. Climate is defined by the average pattern over at least 30 years of data. A single cold event does not establish a trend and cannot support or disprove a claim about a multi-decade pattern. The timescale of the evidence does not match the timescale of the claim.
Q4 ★ Human causes · fossil fuels word bank millions of years · carbon was stored away · released as CO₂ very quickly — in decades · This is faster than any natural process can remove it.
Q5 ★ Geographic factors · latitude and sunlight Diagram A (equator): same sunlight hits a small area → energy is more concentrated.
Diagram B (polar): same sunlight hits a large(r) area → energy is more spread out / diluted.
Warmer region: equator. Why: more concentrated sunlight per unit area = more energy absorbed = higher temperature.
Q6 ★★ Evidence quality misconception · error analysis Problem: A single hottest year is one data point — still just a weather record. One year, even a record year, cannot by itself confirm a 30-year climate trend. One data point could be an outlier; climate requires a pattern sustained over decades.
What would confirm a trend: Temperature data spanning at least 30 years showing a consistent upward direction; multiple independent measuring tools (weather stations, satellites, ocean buoys, ice cores) converging on the same pattern; ruling out natural causes (volcanic activity, solar cycles) as explanations for the full trend.
Q7 ★ Effects in Alberta · glaciers and rivers Short term (early summer): rivers will have more water (from faster glacier melt).
Long term (mid-to-late summer): rivers will have less water (glaciers are smaller, less ice to melt).
Communities and farms face water shortages / reduced irrigation supply.
Why glaciers matter: Glaciers act as frozen reservoirs — they store winter snow and release meltwater through summer, sustaining river flow even during dry periods when there is little rain. Without them, rivers depend only on direct rainfall and snowpack, which is lower in summer.
Q8 ★ Greenhouse gases · match and explain Water vapour → Evaporation from oceans and lakes. CO₂ → Volcanic eruptions and respiration. Methane → Wetlands and decomposition.
Human CO₂ source: burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas); deforestation; any valid industrial process.
Why more CO₂ = more warming: CO₂ molecules absorb infrared heat rising from Earth's surface and re-emit some of it back down. More CO₂ = more heat trapped = higher surface temperatures.
Q9 ★★ Natural vs. human causes misconception · two explanations Correct: Explanation B.
Specific evidence (any one): Ice core data shows current CO₂ levels (~420 ppm) are higher than at any point in the last 800,000 years of natural cycles. The rate of warming since industrialization is 10× faster than natural climate shifts coming out of ice ages. Natural cause models (solar cycles, orbital changes, volcanic activity) cannot replicate the observed warming pattern without including human GHG emissions. Explanation A is a misconception that confuses natural climate variability (real) with the current rate and cause of change (human-driven).
Q10 ★ TEK as valid evidence · Bow River ice break-up Circle: Yes — valid scientific data source.
Reason 1: Systematic long-term observation — the Elder's record spans her lifetime (decades), providing a baseline far longer than most instrument records. Long-term, consistent observation of the same phenomenon is a valid scientific method.
Reason 2: The observation is precise and quantifiable ("about two weeks earlier") — not vague. It captures a measurable change in a documented phenomenon, which makes it comparable to instrument data and useful for detecting trends.
Q11 ★ Ice-albedo feedback loop · complete diagram Ice melts → darker surface (open water/land) is exposed → darker surface absorbs more heat than ice → more heat causes more ice to melt → cycle continues.
Albedo: the proportion of sunlight that a surface reflects back into space.
Higher albedo: fresh snow.
Why it matters: As Arctic ice melts, more dark ocean is exposed; the ocean absorbs more heat than ice did; this extra heat drives more melting in a self-reinforcing cycle — warming accelerates.
Q12 ★ Effects in Alberta · wildfire season word bank Forests dry out drier earlier in the season. Less snowpack → forests enter fire season with less moisture. Higher temperatures dry vegetation more quickly. Fire season starts earlier in spring and ends later in fall → making it longer each year.
Specific wildfire example: any Alberta wildfire mentioned in notes; the 2016 Fort McMurray wildfire (Horse River fire) is the most significant and commonly cited.
Q13 ★★ Greenhouse ≠ ozone hole misconception · two errors Error 1: The greenhouse effect and the ozone hole are NOT the same problem — they involve different gases and different atmospheric layers. Correction: The greenhouse effect involves CO₂, methane, and water vapour in the lower atmosphere (troposphere) trapping heat. The ozone hole involves CFCs destroying ozone in the upper atmosphere (stratosphere), allowing more UV radiation through — a different problem.
Error 2: Fixing one does NOT fix the other. Correction: Reducing CFC emissions (which helped the ozone layer recover) did not reduce CO₂ levels. The two problems require different solutions and addressing one has no significant effect on the other.
Q14 ★ Ice cores · proxy data Each layer traps: air bubbles (containing the atmosphere of that time), dust, volcanic ash, pollen, and water molecules from the snowfall of that year.
Measure from air bubbles: CO₂ concentration, methane, other greenhouse gas levels in the atmosphere at the time the layer formed.
Proxy data: Ice cores do not directly record temperature or CO₂ — they are an indirect record (a proxy). Scientists infer past conditions from what is preserved in the ice, rather than measuring directly with instruments at the time.
Q15 ★ FNMI impacts · ice roads What ice roads are: Roads built on frozen lakes and rivers in winter, allowing vehicles to travel to remote communities not connected by permanent roads. Communities depend on them to transport food, fuel, building materials, and medical supplies at lower cost.
Effects (two): 1. Ice freezes later in autumn and thaws earlier in spring → fewer weeks when the ice road is safe. 2. Ice is thinner / less reliable → roads may be unusable even when frozen, due to safety concerns.
More than inconvenience: For communities without permanent roads, ice roads are the only affordable way to bring in bulk supplies. Without them, communities must rely on expensive air transport — food and fuel costs rise dramatically.
Q16 ★★ Weather vs. climate · analyze data table (a) Lowest: 2016 (18.5°C). Highest: 2023 (23.6°C).
(b) No — 10 years of data is not sufficient to confirm a climate trend (climate requires at least 30 years). Even if the pattern looks clear, a 10-year span could represent natural variation.
(c) The overall pattern suggests a warming trend — later years tend to have higher temperatures than earlier years, and the highest temperatures are all in the more recent years. This is consistent with climate change but not yet proof of a trend from this data alone.
Q17 ★ Human causes · deforestation table Trees burned: CO₂ stored in trees is released → atmospheric CO₂ increases.
No trees remain: future CO₂ cannot be absorbed/removed by photosynthesis → atmospheric CO₂ stays elevated / higher.
Double problem: Deforestation releases stored carbon immediately (problem 1), AND removes the trees that would have absorbed future CO₂ through photosynthesis for decades (problem 2). It both adds CO₂ and removes the mechanism that removes it.
Q18 ★ Climate models · CS abstraction connection Abstraction: A simplified representation of a complex system that keeps only the essential features needed to answer a specific question, leaving out unnecessary detail.
Climate model keeps key variables; leaves out minor / trivial details.
Like a force diagram: A force diagram keeps only force arrows (direction and size); leaves out object colour, texture, material. A climate model keeps CO₂, temperature, ice cover; leaves out individual trees, waves, buildings. Both are abstractions that preserve what matters for the question being asked.
Multiple scenarios: To understand which future outcomes are possible under different decisions — "what if we cut emissions?" vs. "what if we don't?" Scientists use models to inform policy by comparing outcomes, not just predict one future.
Q19 ★★ "Climate change is future" misconception · three effects Any three documented Alberta effects with approximate timing:
1. Wildfire season: measurably longer since 1970s; 2016 Fort McMurray fire ($9B damage).
2. Glaciers shrinking: Athabasca Glacier has retreated 1.5 km since 1844; pace accelerating since 1980s.
3. Average temperatures: Alberta has warmed ~1.5°C since 1950 (documented).
4. Ice roads: open weeks fewer per year since 1990s.
5. Agricultural shifts: growing zones moving northward; new pests/diseases already arriving.
One sentence: Climate change is not a future prediction — it is already changing Alberta's wildfires, glaciers, temperatures, and communities right now.
Q20 ★ Geographic factors · proximity to water Factor: proximity to the ocean (water body).
How it works: Water heats and cools much more slowly than land. A coastal city is adjacent to a massive body of water → temperatures are moderated — cooler summers and milder winters. Edmonton is far inland from the ocean → no moderating effect → much colder winters and much hotter summers.
Alberta is described as a continental climate; this means it has an extreme temperature range.
Q21 ★★ TEK as scientific evidence · four-part comparison (a) Longer: Source B (Elder records). By 55 years (80 vs. 25).
(b) Source B — Elder records document specific plant species and where they grew at specific locations. Satellites provide broad vegetation coverage but cannot identify specific plant species at local scale or track subtle changes in a particular spot the way generations of observation in a specific territory can.
(c) Scientific error: dismissing systematic long-term observation as "stories" fails to recognize that TEK is built on careful, consistent, multi-generational observation of the same phenomena — meeting the key criteria of scientific evidence (systematic, long-term, verifiable by comparing accounts). Calling it "stories" reflects a cultural bias, not a scientific argument.
(d) Source A provides precise, instrument-verified spatial data. Source B provides a longer baseline and fine-grained local detail. Together they produce a more complete picture: the longer time series allows trend detection that satellite data cannot yet provide, while satellite data verifies and spatially extends what Elder records document locally.
Q22 ★ Natural vs. human causes · sort Natural causes: Changes in Earth's orbit; large volcanic eruption; variation in Sun's output; meteor impact throwing dust into atmosphere.
Human causes: Burning coal in power plants; driving gasoline-powered cars; cutting and burning forests; cattle producing methane.
Which column matches current period: Human causes. Natural causes operate on timescales of thousands to tens of thousands of years and cannot explain the rapid warming observed since industrialization (~1850). The rate, timing, and isotopic signature of current CO₂ increase all match fossil fuel burning, not natural processes.
Q23 ★ Signal vs. noise · trend graph Each dot = weather (one year's measurement is a short-term event).
Trend line: should slope upward (dots in later years generally higher).
What trend line tells us: the overall direction of change over 20 years is upward (warming), even though individual years vary above and below the trend. Short-term fluctuations are "noise"; the trend line reveals the underlying "signal."
Q24 ★★ FNMI harvesting impacts · moose and traditional knowledge (a) Moose behaviour is changing because climate change is shifting the timing of seasons — plants grow at different times, insects emerge differently, snowpack changes — so the cues that moose used to time their migration are no longer occurring at the same time. The ecosystem the moose moves through has changed.
(b) More than inconvenience: for many northern communities, moose hunting is essential for food security, not just cultural tradition. It is also a primary way traditional knowledge and relationships with the land are transmitted between generations. When moose arrive unpredictably, families cannot reliably count on the food, and the accumulated knowledge of where and when to hunt — built over generations — becomes unreliable or unusable.
(c) The Elder's observation spans decades vs. three years for the scientist. Three years of data cannot distinguish between a real shift in moose behaviour and year-to-year natural variation. The Elder's multi-decade record provides the baseline needed to confirm that a genuine change has occurred.
Q25 ★ Albedo feedback loop · fill-in explanation Before melt: surface covered in white / bright ice. Ice has high albedo — reflects ~80% of sunlight.
After melt: surface becomes open dark water. Ocean has low albedo — absorbs ~94% of sunlight.
When ice melts → more energy absorbed by surface → more warming → melts more ice.
Name: ice-albedo feedback loop (positive feedback loop).
Q26 ★★ Carbon cycle + fossil fuels · three-part (a) Carbon in coal and oil originally came from CO₂ in the atmosphere. Ancient plants and marine organisms absorbed CO₂ through photosynthesis → built carbon into their tissues → died and were buried → compressed over millions of years into fossil fuels. The carbon in coal started as atmospheric CO₂ that plants fixed into glucose.
(b) The rate matters because natural carbon sinks (oceans, forests) can only absorb CO₂ at a certain rate. When we release millions of years' worth of stored carbon in a few decades, the rate of release far exceeds the rate of natural removal → CO₂ accumulates in the atmosphere faster than it can be drawn down. Even if the total amount were the same over a longer time, the rapid release overwhelms natural buffers.
(c) Trees absorb CO₂ through photosynthesis — they are carbon sinks. When forests are destroyed, the trees can no longer absorb CO₂. Less forest = less photosynthesis = less CO₂ removed from the atmosphere, leaving human emissions less offset.
Q27 ★ Measuring climate · tree rings proxy data Warmest/wettest years: 1, 3, 6, 9, 10 (wide rings). Coldest/driest years: 5, 8 (narrow rings).
Proxy data: Tree rings do not directly record temperature or rainfall — they are an indirect record. Scientists infer growing conditions from ring width, which is a physical trace of past climate left in the wood.
How far back: Thousands of years — especially when overlapping records from multiple trees are combined (dendrochronology).
Q28 ★★ Agriculture + "climate change is future" misconception Part A — partly true: Longer frost-free growing seasons do allow some crops to be grown further north and over a longer period. This is a real effect some farmers have already noticed.
Part B — two things left out: 1. Greater heat and drought stress: warmer summers also mean more extreme heat events and drier conditions that damage crops, reduce yields, and require more irrigation precisely when water is scarcer (glaciers declining). 2. New pests, diseases, and competition: species ranges are shifting northward — bringing insects, pathogens, and invasive weeds that Alberta farmers have not had to manage before. The overall picture is mixed, not straightforwardly positive.
Part C: Incomplete — it acknowledges only a potential benefit while ignoring the documented costs and risks that accompany it.
Q29 ★ TEK + Bow River ice break-up · three-part Why further back matters: A longer baseline allows scientists to determine whether the observed 2-week shift is outside the range of normal variation or just a short-term fluctuation. With only 40 years of instrument data, it is harder to rule out natural decadal cycles. Elder records extending further back add confidence that the change is a genuine trend.
Two independent sources agreeing: When two independent, methodologically different sources reach the same finding, it greatly strengthens the conclusion — ruling out that the result is an artifact of one particular measurement method. Agreement across independent sources is a key marker of robust scientific evidence.
Qualitative or quantitative: Both — the Elder observation includes qualitative detail (what the ice looked like, where it broke first, what other changes accompanied it) AND a quantitative estimate (~two weeks earlier), making it a richer data source than purely quantitative instrument records.
Q30 ★★ Human causes · methane (a) Any two human sources: cattle digestion (enteric fermentation); rice paddies; natural gas leaks during extraction/transport; landfills; wastewater treatment.
(b) Student is wrong / partially wrong: methane matters significantly. While CO₂ is the most abundant long-lived GHG from human activities, methane traps ~80× more heat per molecule over 20 years — so even smaller quantities have large warming effects. Methane is responsible for approximately 30% of human-caused warming to date. The student is right that CO₂ receives the most attention, but wrong to conclude methane "doesn't matter much."
(c) CO₂ is produced in far larger quantities than methane and stays in the atmosphere for centuries to millennia (vs. ~12 years for methane). The sheer volume of CO₂ emitted makes its cumulative effect the dominant long-term driver of warming, even though methane is more potent per molecule.

Stretch Questions — S1 to S5

Stretch questions require multi-concept synthesis. Use as discussion guides or for extended assessment marking.
Q#Concept ClusterModel Answer Guide
S1 ★★ Weather vs. climate + evidence quality + signal vs. noise Core error: Three years of cold January data is weather evidence being used to make a climate claim. A trend requires at least 30 years of data; three data points cannot establish a trend.
Trend: A trend is the consistent direction of change over a long period, visible when short-term fluctuations are averaged out. You identify a trend by plotting data over many years and looking at the overall direction — not by selecting a few recent data points.
Example: A long-term warming trend can still contain cold years, cold decades, and even cold winters — because trend ≠ every data point going up. A river's long-term flow can be increasing even if some individual years have low flow.
Evidence needed to determine warming has stopped: 30+ years of temperature data showing no upward trend across multiple independent measuring sources (weather stations, ocean buoys, satellite); natural cause explanations that account for the full pattern; peer-reviewed scientific consensus shifting — none of which currently exists.
S2 ★★ Albedo + greenhouse effect + feedback loops Feedback loop 1 (albedo): Arctic warming → ice and snow melt → darker ocean/land surface exposed → lower albedo → more sunlight absorbed rather than reflected → more warming → more ice melts → cycle reinforces itself. More warming causes more warming.
Feedback loop 2 (any valid second): e.g., Permafrost-methane: Warming thaws permafrost → frozen organic material decays → releases methane → methane traps more heat → more warming → more permafrost thaws. OR Glacier-river: Glaciers melt → darker land exposed + reduced river flow → less cooling effect from meltwater → regional warming amplified.
Why feedback loops concern scientists: They mean the relationship between emissions and warming is not linear — once certain thresholds are passed, warming can accelerate beyond what emissions alone would predict, making future conditions harder to model and potentially irreversible.
S3 ★★ TEK + scientific methods + evidence quality · collaboration (a) Research question must require both sources: e.g., "Has the timing of fish spawning in this lake shifted over the past 70 years, and can we identify the abiotic conditions (water temperature, ice cover timing) responsible for the change?" This requires 70-year baseline (TEK) + precise water temperature and ice data (instruments).
(b) Scientist's 25 years of satellite data alone: not long enough to determine whether observed changes represent a trend or natural variation; lacks fine-grained local species-level detail; cannot capture pre-satellite baseline.
(c) Elder's 70 years of oral records alone: does not include precisely measured water temperatures or ice thickness; difficult to independently verify exact dates without instrumental corroboration; lacks spatial coverage beyond the specific territory.
(d) Multiple lines of evidence: when independent sources using different methods reach the same conclusion, the risk that the finding is an artifact of one method is greatly reduced. The conclusion becomes more robust and more defensible.
S4 ★★ CS climate model + greenhouse effect + causes (a) Misunderstanding: a model is not a "guess" — it is a mathematical simulation built on known physical laws (how gases absorb radiation, how oceans circulate heat, how ice reflects sunlight). Scientists use models to test hypotheses, not to guess. Models are validated against historical observations; if the model accurately reproduces the past, it has more credibility for the future. The student is confusing uncertainty in long-range projections with the model having no grounding.
(b) This tells us that human GHG emissions are the dominant cause of current warming. Natural cause models fail to reproduce the observed record — they can explain natural variation up to ~1850 but leave a gap after that. Only when human emissions are added does the model match reality. This is strong evidence that the "missing factor" is human influence.
(c) Running multiple scenarios reflects the scientific principle of testing multiple hypotheses / exploring a range of possibilities under different assumptions. It is more useful than one scenario because: the future is not determined — human decisions can change emission trajectories; showing a range of outcomes allows policymakers to understand the consequences of different choices; it prevents overconfidence in any single prediction.
S5 ★★ Synthesis — all unit concepts Why temperature alone is incomplete: Temperature change triggers cascading effects across weather patterns, ecosystems, infrastructure, water supply, food security, and cultural practices — none of which can be captured by temperature alone.
Must include at least 4 of: Greenhouse effect (mechanism, not just temperature); geographic factors (different regions warm differently — latitude/albedo affect who is hit hardest); feedback loops (warming accelerates beyond what emissions predict); wildfire season (longer, more severe — not just "hotter"); glaciers and rivers (water supply downstream of glaciers affected for decades); agricultural shifts (new pests, drought, heat stress — mixed effects); FNMI ice roads (community access and food security); FNMI traditional harvesting (moose timing, sacred plants, TEK becoming less reliable); TEK and measuring climate (multiple evidence types needed); climate models (show a range of futures under different choices).
Closing sentence: Climate science is studying the interconnected effects of a changing energy balance on every system on Earth — from ocean chemistry and glacier retreat to Indigenous food sovereignty and future generations' water supply.
