Grade 6 Science — Forces

Teacher Notes Package · SHOW / SAY / CUE format

How to Use This Document

Each teaching beat has three parts:

LabelWhat it contains
SHOWWhat is visible to students — draw on the board or project. One thing per beat.
SAYKey phrases and questions. Not a script — prompts for fluent delivery. Answers to blanks shown in [brackets].
CUEThe exact signal for students to write. Four types: ★ Write / ✎ Draw / ⊡ Think / ↩ Check

Flags appear in coloured blocks:

Flag — Misconception
Red alert. Students will get this wrong without a direct address.
Flag — Pacing
Warning for where lessons tend to lose momentum.
Flag — CS Connection
Embedded cross-curricular moment — worth lingering on.

The screen and the voice carry different things. What is SHOW stays visual. What is SAY carries the reasoning. Never put your SAY on the screen.

Pre-Knowledge Check

~10 minutes — before any instruction

Beat 1 — Opening

Show
Board: "Write everything you think you know about forces." (That's it — no further prompts.)
Say
  • "Before we start the unit, I want to know what's already in your head."
  • "This is not a test. There are no marks. I'm not going to correct it today."
  • "Write honestly. You will come back to this page at the end of the unit."
  • "What is a force? Where does it come from? What does it do?"
Cue
★ Write: "Open your notes to page 1. You have 5 minutes — write everything you know. Go."
Flag — Pacing
Do not read student responses aloud or comment on them. The pre-knowledge check loses its value if students calibrate to what you reward. Circulate quietly. You're looking for: (a) students who already know the push/pull definition, (b) students who think forces only exist when something moves.

Section 1: What Is a Force?

Learning target: I can explain what a force is, describe its direction and size, and represent a force using a force arrow.
Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup

Show
Board: Five vocabulary terms in a column — Force / Newton (N) / Magnitude / Direction / Force diagram
Say
  • "Before we go into the notes — these five terms are on your vocab table."
  • "Don't fill them in yet. We'll build them as we go."
  • "By the end of this section you'll have your own definition and example for each one."
Cue
⊡ Think: "Read the five terms. Which one do you think you already know? Keep it in your head."

Beat 2 — What is a force?

Show
Board: FORCE = push or pull. Draw a simple arrow pushing a box. Draw a simple arrow pulling a box.
Say
  • "A force is a push or a pull acting on an object."
  • "That's the whole definition. Push. Pull. Acting on an object."
  • "Kick a ball — that's a push. Pull a door open — that's a pull."
  • Pause 3 seconds.
Cue
★ Write: "A force is a [push] or a [pull] acting on an object."
Flag — Pacing
Students often want to add "energy" or "movement" to the definition. That's coming later. Hold the line: push or pull, that's it for now.

Beat 3 — Magnitude

Show
Board: MAGNITUDE = size / strength (measured in Newtons). Write: N large.
Say
  • "Forces have two properties — magnitude is the first."
  • "Magnitude means the size or strength of the force."
  • "Unit: Newtons. Named after Isaac Newton. Symbol: capital N."
  • "A gentle push might be 2 N. A hard shove might be 50 N. Picking up a textbook is about 10 N."
Cue
★ Write: "Magnitude = [size] or [strength] of the force, measured in [Newtons (N)]"

Beat 4 — Direction

Show
Board: Two arrows — same length, pointing opposite directions. Label: 10 N → and ← 10 N. Same magnitude. Different direction.
Say
  • "Second property — direction."
  • "These two forces are both 10 N. Are they the same force?"
  • Pause 3–4 seconds.
  • "No. One pushes right. One pushes left. Direction changes everything."
  • "Both properties matter — always."
Cue
★ Write: "Direction = the [way] the force is acting"

Beat 5 — Force arrows

Show
Board: Draw a single arrow. Label: Direction = which way the arrow points. Then draw a short arrow and a long arrow side by side. Label: Length = magnitude.
Say
  • "We represent forces as arrows. The arrow gives us both properties at once."
  • "Which way the arrow points → direction."
  • "How long the arrow is → magnitude. Longer arrow = bigger force."
  • "This is a convention — an agreed-upon language. All scientists use the same system."
Cue
★ Write: "The [way] the force points shows the direction. The [length] of the arrow represents the magnitude."
Flag — Pacing
Students sometimes confuse arrow length with actual distance or movement. Clarify: length here is a proportional representation, not a measurement of how far the object moves.

Beat 6 — Force diagrams and abstraction

Show
Board: Draw a box with shape, colour, texture. Then erase everything except the outline and draw force arrows. Label: Force diagram — forces only.
Say
  • "A force diagram strips away everything except the forces."
  • "Not the colour. Not the material. Not whether it's on wheels."
  • "Just: what forces are acting, and which direction."
  • "This is called abstraction — keeping only what you need, removing everything else."
Cue
★ Write: "A force diagram shows only the [forces] acting on an object, removing all unnecessary detail."
Flag — CS Connection
Pause here. "This is exactly what happens in programming and design. When you write code to model a problem, you keep only the properties that matter. A force diagram is an abstraction. A variable in code is an abstraction. Same thinking." Allow 30–60 seconds for students to record this in the connection note.

Beat 7 — Worked example: reading a force diagram

Show
Board: Box in the centre, 20 N → on the left side, ← 8 N on the right side. Make the 20 N arrow visibly longer. Label both forces.
Say
  • "Two forces on this box. Look at the diagram."
  • "The 20 N arrow points right. The 8 N arrow points left."
  • Pause 3 seconds.
  • "They're working against each other. How do we find the net force?"
  • Pause 3–4 seconds.
  • "Subtract. Both on the same line, opposite directions: 20 minus 8 equals 12."
  • "Net force: 12 N to the right. The box would move right."
Cue
★ Write: "20 N pushes [right], 8 N pushes [left]. Net force = [12] N to the right."
Flag — Misconception
Students frequently say the net force is 28 N (they add instead of subtract). Pre-empt this: "Some people add them. Why would that be wrong?" Wait for a response. Key idea: forces in opposite directions partially cancel each other.

Beat 8 — Vocabulary table fill-in

Say
  • "Now go back to your vocabulary table."
  • "Fill in each definition in your own words — not the textbook definition."
  • "Your definition should be something you could explain to a Grade 4 student."
  • "Add one example you thought of yourself."
Cue
★ Write: "Complete your vocab table now — own words, own examples." Allow 3–5 minutes.

Beat 9 — Questions Now Answerable

Teacher: Q1, Q6, Q11, Q22

Cue
⊡ Think: "Scan the workbook. Find 4 questions you think you could now answer. Put a tick beside each one — do not answer yet." Allow 60–90 seconds. Then as a class: have students share which questions they found. For each one — ask why it connects to this section.
Section 1 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide

Before releasing: SAY "Notes closed. Everything closed."

  1. Magnitude (size/strength) and direction
  2. Newtons (N)
  3. The arrow represents a larger force — greater magnitude
  4. Right arrow twice as long as left arrow, both labeled
  5. (Stretch) Incorrect — forces can act on an object without causing movement. If the box isn't moving, the forces are balanced, not absent.

Q5 is the stretch — prompt: "The box isn't moving. What does that tell you about the forces?" Don't give the answer before the retrieval check ends.

Section 2: External Forces

Learning target: I can identify applied force, friction, and elastic force, describe their direction, and explain how external forces affect objects.
Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction
Bring: spring scale, elastic band.

Beat 1 — External forces intro

Show
Board: EXTERNAL FORCE = acts from outside the object
Say
  • "External — where does that prefix come from? External means outside."
  • "External forces come from outside the object — a person pushing, the floor pressing up, the air."
  • "The object is being acted on. The force source is outside it."
Cue
★ Write: "External forces act on an object [from outside] — from a person, another object, or the environment."

Beat 2 — Applied force

Show
Board: APPLIED FORCE → direction: wherever you push or pull. Draw: stick figure pushing a box.
Say
  • "Applied force — you apply it. Direct contact."
  • "A person pushes a cart. An engine drives a car forward. You kick a ball."
  • "Direction: wherever you push or pull. The force arrow follows the push."
Cue
★ Write: "Applied force direction: [wherever] you push or pull"

Beat 3 — Friction (with demonstration)

Show
Board: FRICTION → opposes motion → direction: OPPOSITE to motion. Draw: box moving right, friction arrow pointing left.
Say
  • "Friction acts between two surfaces in contact."
  • Hold up spring scale. Drag an object across the desk. "Watch the scale — that number is the friction force."
  • "Friction always opposes motion. If the box moves right, friction points left. Always."
  • "Friction never acts in the direction of motion."
Cue
★ Write: "Friction [opposes] motion. Direction: always [opposite] to the direction of motion."
Flag — Misconception: "Friction isn't really a force — it just slows things down."
Address directly before moving on. SAY: "Here's something students often get wrong — they say friction isn't really a force. Is it? What makes something a force?" Pause. Then: "Friction has a direction — opposite to motion. Friction has a size — measurable in Newtons, right here on this spring scale. It acts between surfaces. It IS a force." Point to the force diagram arrow. Do not skip this. Students who leave with the misconception intact will miss all friction questions on the unit test.

Beat 4 — Elastic force (with demonstration)

Show
Board: ELASTIC FORCE → stretched or compressed → direction: back to original shape. Hold up elastic band, stretch it, release.
Say
  • "Elastic force comes from stretching or compressing an elastic material."
  • "Stretch this elastic band — what happens when I let go? It returns to its original shape."
  • "The force that pulls it back is the elastic force."
  • "Direction: always back toward the original shape."
Cue
★ Write: "Elastic force — material is [stretched] or [compressed]. Acts to [return] the material to its original shape."

Beat 5 — Four-force diagram

Show
Board: Box on a floor. Four arrows: up (Normal), down (Gravity/weight), right (Applied), left (Friction). Label each directly beside its arrow.
Say
  • "Here's a box on a floor being pushed to the right."
  • Point up: "Normal force — floor pushing up. Always perpendicular to the surface."
  • Point down: "Gravity — weight pulling down."
  • Point right: "Applied force — the push."
  • Point left: "Friction — opposing motion."
  • "Notice: labels are beside each arrow, not in a legend on the side — split-attention rule."
Cue
✎ Draw: "Draw this force diagram in your notes. Include all four labels directly beside each arrow."
Flag — Pacing
Normal force is introduced here but not defined as a vocabulary term — it appears in the diagram only. Don't go deep on it. "The floor pushes up just as hard as you push down — we'll explore this more in Newton's Third Law" is enough.

Beat 6 — Bridge to internal forces

Say
  • "All these forces are pushing and pulling from the outside."
  • "What do you think is happening inside the material of that box when those forces act on it?"
  • "That's Section 3. Hold that thought."
Cue
★ Write: "External forces cause forces to develop [inside / within] the material — called internal forces."
Section 2 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Applied force, friction, elastic force
  2. Opposite to the direction of motion (leftward if the puck moves right)
  3. Elastic force
  4. Applied force pushes right; friction acts left (opposite to direction of motion)
  5. (Stretch) Friction must be 15 N acting to the left — equal in magnitude to the applied force, opposite in direction. Net force is zero, which is why the box doesn't move. This previews Newton's Third Law logic.

Section 3: Internal Forces

Learning target: I can identify and distinguish tension, compression, shear, and torsion, and explain how external forces produce internal forces in a material.
Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction
Bring: foam block or sponge.

Beat 1 — Internal forces intro

Show
Board: INTERNAL FORCE = acts inside the material. Draw external arrows pushing on a box → question mark inside.
Say
  • "Section 2 was about forces acting on the outside of an object."
  • "Section 3 is about what happens inside the material when those outside forces act."
  • "Push on a foam block. The outside force is your hands. The inside force is what the foam does in response."
Cue
★ Write: "Internal forces act [within / inside] a material — the result of external forces acting from outside."

Beat 2 — Tension

Show
Board: Block labeled MATERIAL, arrows pulling left and right simultaneously. Label: TENSION — pulling / stretching force.
Say
  • "Tension — the material is being pulled apart from both ends."
  • "Two external forces pulling outward. Inside, the particles are being pulled away from each other."
  • "Rope in a tug of war. Suspension bridge cable. A stretched elastic band."
Cue
★ Write: "Tension = [pulling / stretching] internal force. Particles are being pulled [apart]."

Beat 3 — Compression

Show
Board: Block, arrows pushing in from both ends. Label: COMPRESSION — pushing / squeezing force.
Say
  • "Compression — opposite of tension. Forces pushing inward from both ends."
  • "Particles are being pushed together."
  • "Chair leg when you sit. Spring being squeezed. Concrete pillar holding up a floor."
  • "Your weight compresses the chair legs right now."
Cue
★ Write: "Compression = [pushing / squeezing] internal force. Particles are being pushed [together]."

Beat 4 — Shear (with demonstration)

Show
Board: Two layers, top layer arrow pointing right, bottom layer arrow pointing left. Label: SHEAR — sliding layers.
Say
  • "Shear — layers of the material slide past each other in opposite directions."
  • Demonstrate with foam block — push top right with one hand, bottom left with other.
  • "Scissors cutting paper — top blade goes down, bottom blade goes up. The paper is being sheared."
Cue
★ Write: "Shear = [sliding] internal force. Layers slide past each other in [opposite] directions."

Beat 5 — Torsion (with demonstration)

Show
Board: Cylinder with curved arrows — clockwise at top, counterclockwise at bottom. Label: TORSION — twisting force.
Say
  • "Torsion — the material is being twisted."
  • Demonstrate with foam — twist both ends in opposite directions.
  • "Wringing water from a cloth. A wrench turning a bolt. A car's drive shaft."
Cue
★ Write: "Torsion = [twisting] internal force. Particles rotate in opposite directions around a central axis."
Flag — Pacing
Students often confuse shear and torsion. Clearest distinction: shear is linear (layers sliding in a straight direction), torsion is rotational (layers rotating around an axis). Draw both side by side. Ask: "Which one would a wet cloth experience when you wring it? Why is it not shear?" 30 seconds partner discussion.
Section 3 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. External = acts from outside the object. Internal = acts inside the material.
  2. Tension
  3. Compression
  4. Torsion
  5. (Stretch) Shear. External forces: one hand pushes top face right, other pushes bottom face left. Internal force: shear — layers slide past each other in opposite directions.

Section 4: Plasticity and Elasticity

Learning target: I can explain the difference between elastic and plastic deformation, identify examples of each, and explain what the elastic limit means.
Timing: ~15–20 minutes direct instruction
Bring: 2 paper clips. Optional: helmet photo + bridge cable photo.

Beat 1 — Deformation intro

Show
Board: DEFORMATION = change in shape due to a force
Say
  • "When a force acts on a material, the material may change shape."
  • "That change is called deformation. De-form — losing its form."
  • "Two types — they're completely different in what they mean for the material."
Cue
★ Write: "When a force acts on a material, the material may change shape — this is called [deformation]."

Beat 2 — Elastic deformation

Show
Board: ELASTIC DEFORMATION → temporary change → returns to original shape. Before/during/after sequence with spring returning.
Say
  • "Elastic deformation — the material changes shape during the force, then springs back."
  • "This is a temporary change."
  • "The key: remove the force → original shape returns."
Cue
★ Write: "Elastic deformation = material [returns] to its original shape when the force is removed. This is a [temporary] change."

Beat 3 — Plastic deformation

Show
Board: PLASTIC DEFORMATION → permanent change → stays deformed. Before/during/after sequence where 'after' shape differs from 'before'.
Say
  • "Plastic deformation — the material changes shape and stays there."
  • "This is a permanent change."
  • "Squish playdough — it stays squished. Bend a metal wire into an L — it stays as an L."
Cue
★ Write: "Plastic deformation = material [keeps] its new shape even after the force is removed. This is a [permanent] change."
Flag — Vocabulary
The word "plastic" here does NOT mean the material plastic. It means "able to be permanently shaped" — from Greek plastikos. Walk through: "Is playdough plastic? Is silly putty plastic?" to distinguish plastic-deformation from plastic-the-material.

Beat 4 — Elastic limit

Show
Board: ELASTIC LIMIT = the point where elastic deformation becomes plastic deformation. Number line: "below elastic limit → elastic" / "above elastic limit → plastic".
Say
  • "Every material has an elastic limit — a maximum force before it stops bouncing back."
  • Bend paper clip slightly: "Below the elastic limit — springs back."
  • Bend sharply: "Above the elastic limit — stays bent. Permanently deformed."
Cue
★ Write: "Below the elastic limit: springs [back] (elastic). Above the elastic limit: stays [deformed] (plastic)."

Beat 5 — Engineering applications

Show
Board: Two columns — HELMET (plastic foam) | BRIDGE CABLE (steel).
Say
  • "Helmet foam deforms plastically in a collision. It absorbs the force by staying crushed. If it sprang back elastically, it would bounce the force into your skull."
  • "Bridge cables must stay elastic — they stretch under load and spring back to original length."
  • "Same physics. Opposite design requirement."
Cue
★ Write: "Helmets use plastic foam — deforms [plastically] to absorb force. Bridge cables must stay [elastic] — return to original length under load."
Section 4 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Elastic = temporary change, material returns. Plastic = permanent change, material stays deformed.
  2. Elastic deformation — the spring returns to its original length.
  3. Plastic deformation — stays at 90°, permanent change.
  4. The force exceeds the elastic limit — the material no longer returns to its original shape.
  5. (Stretch) Plastically deforming material — the bumper should absorb force by crushing (staying deformed), not bounce force back to the passenger compartment.

Section 5: Newton's Third Law

Learning target: I can state Newton's Third Law, identify action-reaction pairs, and explain why these forces do not cancel each other.
Timing: ~20–25 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1 — The law statement

Show
Board: Newton's Third Law: For every ACTION, there is an equal and opposite REACTION.
Say
  • "Newton's Third Law. This one sounds simple and it will confuse students for years."
  • "Let me say it precisely: when object A exerts a force on object B, object B exerts a force on object A that is equal in size and opposite in direction."
  • Pause 4–5 seconds.
Cue
★ Write: "When one object exerts a force on a second object, the second object exerts a force on the first that is [equal] in size and [opposite] in direction."

Beat 2 — The three properties

Show
Board: Three bullet points — (1) Equal in magnitude / (2) Opposite in direction / (3) Acting on DIFFERENT objects
Say
  • "Three properties — all three must be true."
  • "Acting on different objects: THIS is the one students miss."
  • "The action force acts on object B. The reaction force acts on object A. Two different objects."
  • "If both forces act on the same object, they are NOT an action-reaction pair."
Cue
★ Write: "Action-reaction pairs: equal in [magnitude] / opposite in [direction] / acting on [different] objects."
Flag — This is the heart of Section 5
Property 3 — different objects — is what makes the law coherent and prevents the "they cancel out" misconception. Spend 60 extra seconds here. Ask: "Why does it matter that they act on different objects?" Don't answer — wait for a student response. Then move to the misconception beat.

Beat 3 — Examples table

Show
Board: Three-scenario table with the reaction force column blank. Fill in live.
SituationAction forceReaction force
You jump off the groundYou push down on the floorFloor pushes up on you
You row a boatPaddle pushes back on the waterWater pushes forward on the paddle
You lean on a wallYou push toward the wallWall pushes back on you
Say
  • Scenario 1: "You push down on the floor when you jump. What does the floor push?" Pause. Get responses.
  • Scenario 2: "Paddle pushes backward on water. Water pushes..." Pause.
  • Scenario 3: "You push toward the wall. Wall pushes..." Pause.
Cue
★ Write: "Complete your examples table — three scenarios, both forces."

Beat 4 — Misconception 1: Heavier object always pushes harder

Show
Board: Large truck and small bicycle. Label: collision.
Say
  • "A truck hits a bicycle. Which object exerts a larger force on the other?"
  • Pause for student responses. Most will say the truck.
  • "Newton's Third Law says: the force the truck exerts on the bicycle is exactly equal to the force the bicycle exerts on the truck. Equal."
  • "Then why does the bicycle get destroyed and not the truck?"
  • Pause 4–5 seconds.
  • "Mass. The same force on a small mass produces a large acceleration. On a large mass, a small acceleration. The forces are equal. The effects are not."
Flag — Do not rush through this
This is the most counterintuitive moment in the unit. Students who "understand" it in class will revert to "heavier = more force" on the test. If time allows: two students pull a shared spring scale from opposite ends — they will both read the same force, regardless of who is stronger.
Cue
★ Write: "Complete the truck/bicycle misconception box in your notes — why are the forces equal even though the effects are different?"

Beat 5 — Misconception 2: Action and reaction forces cancel out

Show
Board: Two separate figures — a person and the ground. Label force on person (ground pushes up on person). Label force on ground (person pushes down on ground). Circle each figure separately.
Say
  • "Second misconception: if every force has an equal and opposite reaction, why does anything ever move?"
  • "Look at the diagram. The force on you comes from the ground — it acts ON YOU. The force on the ground comes from you — it acts ON THE GROUND."
  • "Forces only cancel when they act on the same object."
  • "These two forces are on different objects. They have nothing to cancel with."
Cue
★ Write: "Action-reaction forces cannot cancel because they act on [different] objects. Forces only cancel when they act on the [same] object."
Flag — The diagram is doing the heavy lifting
The physical separation of the two circles makes the argument visual. Students who are still confused: have them name each force and name the object it acts ON. If they can do that, they understand why the forces don't cancel.
Section 5 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. When one object exerts a force on another, the second exerts a force on the first that is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. (Key: equal, opposite, two objects.)
  2. Action: you push forward on the cart. Reaction: the cart pushes backward on you. Two different objects.
  3. Water pushes forward on the fish (reaction to fish's tail pushing backward on water).
  4. The error: they act on different objects, so they cannot cancel. Forces cancel only when acting on the same object.
  5. (Stretch) Pair 1: Earth's gravity pulls the book down → book's gravity pulls Earth up. Pair 2: Book pushes down on table → table pushes up on book. Credit any two correctly identified pairs with both forces and both objects named.

Cue

Say
  • "Read what you wrote before the unit started."
  • "What did you get right? What were you wrong about? What surprised you?"
