Section 1 — Weather and Climate

Weather is what is happening outside right now.

Is it raining today? Is it sunny? That is weather. Weather can change every hour.

Climate is the pattern of weather over many years.

Alberta winters are usually cold and snowy. That is climate. It is what you expect over time.

What shapes climate?

  • Distance from the equator — farther north = colder
  • Closeness to water — oceans keep temperatures moderate
  • Elevation — higher up = colder
  • Greenhouse gases — trap heat near Earth's surface
🔑 Word to know
Weather — what the atmosphere is doing right now. Rain, sun, snow, wind.
Changes from hour to hour.
🔑 Word to know
Climate — the usual weather pattern of a place over many years.
Alberta's climate is cold and dry.
⚠️ Watch out
Weather and climate are NOT the same thing.
One cold day does not mean climate change is wrong. Climate is about long-term patterns, not single days.
💡 Remember
Weather = right now. Climate = what to expect over many years.

Section 2 — The Greenhouse Effect

The Sun sends energy to Earth. Earth's surface warms up. Then it sends some of that heat back out.

But some gases in the air trap that heat. They act like a blanket around Earth.

This is called the greenhouse effect. Without it, Earth would be too cold for life.

But when there are too many greenhouse gases, too much heat is trapped. Earth gets warmer than it should.

SUN ↓ [Earth's surface warms up] ↓ Heat tries to escape into space ↓ [Greenhouse gases trap some heat] ↓ Earth stays warm — but too many gases = too much heat trapped

Main greenhouse gases

  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂) — from burning coal, oil, and gas
  • Methane (CH₄) — from livestock, landfills, and natural gas
  • Water vapour — natural, but increases as Earth warms

⚠️ Watch out
The greenhouse effect is NOT the same as the ozone hole.
The ozone hole is about UV protection. The greenhouse effect is about heat. These are two different problems.
💡 Remember
Some greenhouse effect is natural and needed. Too much causes Earth to get too warm. That is the problem.

Section 3 — Why Is Climate Changing?

Climate has always changed slowly over thousands of years. These are natural causes.

But over the last 150 years, climate is changing much faster. This is mainly because of human activity.

Natural causes

  • Volcanoes — send ash and gases into the air
  • Changes in Earth's orbit around the Sun
  • Changes in how active the Sun is

Human causes

  • Burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) — adds CO₂
  • Cutting down forests — fewer trees to absorb CO₂
  • Farming — livestock produce methane

🔑 Word to know
Fossil fuel — coal, oil, and gas. Formed over millions of years. Burning them releases CO₂.
Cars, heating, electricity — all use fossil fuels.
⚠️ Watch out
Climate change is not a future problem. It is happening right now.
Glaciers in Alberta are already melting. Wildfire seasons are already longer.
💡 Remember
Natural causes exist. But the rapid change we see now is mainly from human activity — especially burning fossil fuels.

Section 4 — What Is Already Changing

Effects in Alberta

  • Glaciers in the Rocky Mountains are melting
  • Wildfire seasons are longer and more intense
  • Seasons are shifting — spring comes earlier
  • Indigenous communities are seeing changes to the land they depend on

How we measure climate change

  • Scientists use thermometers, satellites, ice cores, and computer models
  • Indigenous Knowledge Keepers use observations passed down through generations — longer records than most instruments

Indigenous connection

Elders and Knowledge Keepers have observed the land for hundreds of years. They notice when animals migrate at different times, when berries ripen earlier, when river levels change. This knowledge comes from watching the land carefully over many generations. It gives us a longer picture of change than any scientific instrument can match. Both types of knowledge matter.

🔑 Word to know
Ice core — a tube of ice drilled from a glacier. Air bubbles inside show what the atmosphere was like thousands of years ago.
🔑 Word to know
Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) — knowledge about the environment passed down through Indigenous generations.
A valid source of scientific evidence.
💡 Remember
Scientists and Indigenous Knowledge Keepers are both tracking climate change. Both sources of evidence are important.
