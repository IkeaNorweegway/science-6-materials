Core Questions — Q1 to Q30

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q1 ★ Particle model — four key ideas · fill-in spaces (between particles) · moving (always) · speed (of movement changes with temperature). Accept: gaps/space for first blank; never stop/never still for "moving"; rate/pace for "speed."
Q2 ★ States of matter · matching Solid → packed tightly, vibrating in place → Ice cube. Liquid → close but flowing → Orange juice in a cup. Gas → far apart, fast-moving, fill container → Steam from a kettle.
Q3 ★ Thermometer mechanics · fill-in + explain Blanks: faster · farther/further apart · space · rises.
Ice water: liquid level would fall/drop — particles slow and come closer together, liquid contracts and takes up less space in the tube.
Q4 ★★ Particle model · error analysis Error: Particles are not tiny pieces of the same material — they cannot be seen or collected as "shavings." Particles are far too small to be visible with the naked eye or any standard microscope.
Correct: Particles are the fundamental building blocks of matter — not miniature versions of it. They are unimaginably small and invisible.
Space: The space between particles is real — it explains why gases can be compressed and why matter can change state.
Q5 ★ Expansion / contraction · word bank fill-in Heated: faster · farther apart · expands. Cooled: slower · closer · contracts.
Q6 ★ Phase changes · label diagram Solid→Liquid: melting. Liquid→Solid: freezing. Liquid→Gas: evaporation. Gas→Liquid: condensation. Solid→Gas: sublimation. Gas→Solid: deposition.
Circle (heating): melting, evaporation, sublimation. Box (cooling): freezing, condensation, deposition.
Q7 ★★ Expansion · application + compare Why gaps: In summer heat, metal particles spread further apart → deck sections expand → without gaps, expanding metal would push against adjacent sections with great force and buckle or crack.
Alberta vs. tropical: Student is right. Alberta's temperature swings (e.g., −35°C winter to +35°C summer) are far greater than in a stable tropical climate → metal expands and contracts much more → larger gaps required.
Q8 ★ Conservation of mass · phase change Mass = 75 g. Circle: stayed the same.
Explanation: Melting only rearranges how particles are organized — from a fixed arrangement (solid) to a flowing one (liquid). No particles are created or destroyed, so mass is conserved.
Q9 ★ Temperature + particle speed · table 20°C → 80°C: Faster (more heat energy → particles speed up). 20°C → −10°C: Slower (cooling removes energy → particles slow). −5°C → 0°C: Faster (temperature is rising, even though close to 0°C → particles still speed up).
Q10 ★ Water anomaly · diagram + explain More space: the ice (solid).
Why ice floats: When water freezes, particles lock into a crystal structure that pushes them further apart than in liquid form. Ice is therefore less dense than liquid water. Less dense objects float — so ice sits at the surface.
Q11 ★ Celsius scale · matching + explain 0°C = Water freezes. 37°C = Normal human body temperature. 100°C = Water boils. −20°C = Cold Alberta winter day.
Calibrated: set and adjusted so the measurement scale reliably produces the same reading under the same conditions.
Why water: water is universally available; its freezing and boiling points are easily reproduced in any lab anywhere in the world.
Q12 ★★ Conservation of mass · error analysis + experimental design Error 1: Mass did not disappear — the water became water vapor (gas) and escaped into the surrounding air. Mass was conserved; it just moved locations.
Error 2: There is not "less matter now." The water vapor is still matter — it has mass and takes up space.
Experiment: Boil water in a sealed, airtight container on a scale. Measure mass before and after heating. The total mass will be unchanged.
Q13 ★ CS abstraction + particle model · explain Keeps: particle existence, the spaces between particles, the fact that particles move, and that speed relates to temperature.
Leaves out: exact particle shape/colour, forces between particles, quantum-level behavior, chemical bonding details.
Like the weather app: both use simplified stand-in values (temperature, pressure, humidity / particle speed, spacing) to represent extremely complex systems — keeping only what is needed to make useful predictions.
Q14 ★ States of matter · sketch from memory Solid: tightly packed, regular arrangement, particles touching or nearly touching. Fixed shape: Y · Fixed volume: Y. Liquid: close together but irregular/disordered, some space. Fixed shape: N · Fixed volume: Y. Gas: widely spaced, spread across entire box, scattered. Fixed shape: N · Fixed volume: N.
Q15 ★★ Water anomaly · two-explanation analysis Correct: Explanation B.
How I know: When water freezes, its molecules arrange into a hexagonal crystal lattice — a crystal structure — that has more space between particles than liquid water. This makes ice less dense, not more. Less dense = floats.
Real-world evidence: Ice cubes float in water. Rivers freeze at the surface, not the bottom. Both observations prove ice is less dense than liquid water.
Q16 ★ Thermometer safety · error analysis table Reading from above: parallax error — the liquid appears to line up with a higher mark than it actually does → false high reading. Fix: read at eye level, liquid level straight across.
Stirring: could break the glass thermometer; could also contaminate the liquid; heat distribution affected. Fix: use a separate stirring rod.
Recording immediately: thermometer hasn't equalized with the liquid yet → reading is not stable and may be wrong. Fix: wait until the liquid level stops moving before reading.
Q17 ★ FNMI seasonal ice · explain value of long-term observation Part A: Any one: when ice is thick/safe to travel on; how ice behaves near river currents (thinner, breaks earlier); what timing of freeze signals about coming season.
Part B: 40 years of observation provides a much longer baseline than 3 years of sensor data; can detect slow long-term trends; includes memory of unusual years and outliers that give context to current observations. Sensor data is precise but not yet deep enough in time to distinguish trend from variation.
Q18 ★ Particle speed comparison · three-part Faster: Container B (75°C). More space between particles: Container B.
Cooling from 75°C to 15°C: Volume will decrease — particles slow down, come closer together, material contracts.
Q19 ★★ Phase change misconception · separate two ideas Idea 1 — what changes: Volume (arrangement) — water particles lock into a crystal structure further apart → ice takes up more space than liquid water of the same mass.
Idea 2 — what stays the same: Mass — the number of particles does not change.
Correction: When water freezes, its volume increases because particles spread into a crystal structure — but mass stays exactly the same because no particles are added or removed.
Q20 ★ Expansion · two real-world applications Power lines: In summer heat, wire particles spread further apart → wire expands and gets longer → hangs lower/more slack. In winter cold, particles come closer → wire contracts → pulled tight.
Rail track gaps: Gaps allow room for metal sections to expand in summer heat without pushing against adjacent sections. Without gaps on a hot day: sections expand, push against each other, creating force large enough to warp/buckle the track out of alignment.
Q21 ★★ Particle model evidence + limitations · explain Evidence (any two): (1) Diffusion — smells spread through a room without being pushed, which shows particles move and have spaces between them. (2) Gases can be compressed — possible only if there is space between particles. (3) Phase changes — the particle model accurately predicts and explains all six phase transitions. (4) Expansion when heated — particles spreading apart explains why matter takes up more volume at higher temperatures.
Limitation: The particle model does not explain what holds particles together, does not describe the forces between particles, and does not predict how different substances behave differently.
Q22 ★ Celsius scale · label diagram 100°C = Water boiling point. 37°C = Normal human body temperature. 20°C = Room temperature (comfortable indoor temperature). 0°C = Water freezing point. −20°C = Cold winter day (typical Alberta January).
Reference points: 0°C and 100°C (circled). Chosen because they are easily reproduced anywhere using common water; the scale between them is divided into 100 equal degrees.
Q23 ★★ Thermometer · digital vs. liquid + particle model Part A: The Celsius scale is defined by water's particle behavior — 0°C is when water particles slow enough to form a solid; 100°C is when particles move fast enough to escape as gas. The particle model defines what temperature means, regardless of which device is reading it.
Part B: Same: both measure temperature in Celsius; both respond to the same physical reality (particle speed/energy); both rely on the same reference points. Different: liquid thermometer works by liquid expansion (particles spreading apart); digital uses a sensor whose electrical resistance changes with particle energy — no liquid involved.
Q24 ★ States of matter · comparison table Liquid particle spacing: close but not fixed / some space. Gas particle movement: fast, random, in all directions. Liquid fixed shape: No. Gas fixed volume: No. Gas compressible: Yes (easily). Examples: any reasonable daily-life examples accepted for each state.
Q25 ★ Water anomaly · aquatic life + three-part Why top, not bottom: Ice is less dense than liquid water (crystal structure = more space between particles) so it floats to the surface rather than sinking.
Temperature under ice: approximately 0–4°C (liquid water, not frozen).
Why it matters: Fish and other aquatic organisms can survive in the liquid water under the ice all winter. If ice sank, the lake would freeze solid from the bottom up and there would be no liquid habitat left for them.
Q26 ★★ Experimental design · wax melting point Variable changed: temperature / amount of heat applied.
Control variables (any two): amount of wax; size/shape of container; rate of heating; type of heat source.
What to measure: temperature at which the wax visibly changes from solid to liquid (using a thermometer).
Prediction: If the wax is a pure substance, then it will melt at a single consistent temperature because pure substances have a definite melting point rather than a range.
Q27 ★ Matter definition · identify + explain Wooden chair: YES — has mass and volume. Smell of candle: YES — scent molecules are particles with mass that travel through air. Beam of light: NO — light is energy (photons), has no mass. Steam from soup: YES — water vapor is a gas; has mass and takes up space. Heat from radiator: NO — heat is energy transfer, not matter. Air in balloon: YES — gas with mass and volume.
Common hard ones: smell (yes — particles) and light/heat (no — energy, not matter).
Q28 ★★ Water anomaly + freeze-thaw weathering · three-part Part A: When water freezes in the crack, particles lock into a crystal structure further apart than in liquid form → water expands → expanding ice pushes outward against rock walls → crack widens over repeated freeze-thaw cycles.
Part B: Student is wrong. If ice were denser, water would contract when freezing → take up less space → crack would not widen (or might even narrow slightly). No freeze-thaw weathering would occur.
Part C: Water trapped in road cracks will freeze in Alberta winters, expand, and progressively widen the cracks, eventually breaking up the road surface. Drainage removes water before it can freeze in cracks.
Q29 ★ Thermometer / parallax error · visual Correct reading: 34°C. Error type: parallax error.
How to avoid: Always position eyes at the same level as the top of the liquid column — read it straight across, not from an angle above or below.
Q30 ★★ FNMI knowledge + water anomaly + particle model · three-part Part A: Later freezing + earlier melting suggests average temperatures are rising — water particles have more kinetic energy for more of the year, so fewer days dip below the freezing threshold.
Part B — Reasons 1 and 2 (any two): (1) 20 years > 11 years — longer baseline detects slow trends and can distinguish genuine change from year-to-year variation. (2) Knowledge Keeper's observations include qualitative, location-specific detail (specific river bends, current speed effects, storm patterns) that fixed sensors cannot capture. (3) Generational memory includes outlier years that provide context satellite records don't yet span.
Part C: If ice were denser and sank, lakes would freeze solid from the bottom up. Even a "shorter ice season" would still mean complete freezing of the water column — no liquid habitat for fish. The water anomaly is what makes "a shorter ice season" still survivable for aquatic life, rather than catastrophic.

Stretch Questions — S1 to S5

Stretch questions are open-ended synthesis tasks. Use these as model-answer guides for discussion, not single-answer marking.
Q#Concept ClusterModel Answer Guide
S1 ★★ Particle model + phase changes + conservation of mass (a) States and phase changes: Solid (ice) → melting → Liquid (water) → evaporation → Gas (steam/water vapor) → condensation → Liquid (water).
(b) Mass: 200 g at start; 200 g at end. The tin was sealed — all water vapor stayed inside and condensed back. Mass is conserved throughout all phase changes.
(c) Particle model at each stage: Solid — particles vibrating in fixed positions, tightly packed. Liquid — particles close, sliding past each other freely. Gas — particles far apart, moving fast and randomly in all directions. All phase changes rearrange particles; number never changes.
S2 ★★ Experimental design + thermometer + expansion Key design requirements: Control same liquid volume, same container size, same heat source, same starting temperature — vary only the colour (add food colouring). Measure the height the liquid rises in the tube for a fixed temperature increase.
Predicted result: Colour would NOT affect expansion. Particle model: colour comes from dye molecules, not from a change in the liquid's particle properties. Rate of expansion depends on intermolecular forces and particle spacing, not colour. Award marks for correct reasoning process even if student is uncertain of the conclusion.
S3 ★★ FNMI knowledge + water anomaly + scientific methods (a) Long-term systematic observation IS a valid scientific method. Key arguments: (1) Observations span generations — far longer time series than most instrument records. (2) Built-in accountability — accuracy was essential for survival (wrong ice = death), so errors were corrected. (3) Covers variation across seasons, decades, and unusual years that short-term sensor data cannot match.
(b) If ice sank, rivers and lakes would freeze solid from the bottom up — no liquid water would remain. Ice travel wouldn't exist (no ice surface would form above liquid water). The entire premise of ice-road knowledge would be irrelevant.
(c) Any well-constructed question that needs both: e.g., "Has the timing of peak ice thickness changed over the past 50 years, and what does this mean for safe travel windows?" — Knowledge Keeper provides 50-year baseline + location-specific detail; sensors provide precise measurement and real-time data.
S4 ★★ CS abstraction + particle model + phase changes (a) Must include (any three): particles exist and have spacing; particles are always moving; temperature controls particle speed; at 0°C liquid water particles slow and arrange into a crystal structure with wider spacing (the anomaly).
(b) Leave out (any two): exact chemical bonding forces (hydrogen bonds); quantum-level particle behavior; individual particle positions; variations in pressure; dissolved minerals.
(c) The particle model shows that water is anomalous — unlike most substances, its solid state (crystal lattice) has more space between particles than its liquid state. The simulation correctly models this: at below 0°C, particles spread apart rather than coming closer. This is a documented, observable property of water, not an error in the model.
S5 ★★ Synthesis — all four unit concepts What is incomplete: Temperature doesn't just change how matter "looks" — it changes the arrangement, spacing, and movement speed of particles, which produces real physical changes (expansion, phase changes, anomalous behavior of water).
Must reference at least 3 of: particle model (particles always moving, speed = temperature); phase changes (rearrangement is real and changes state, but conserves mass); thermometer (measures particle speed as temperature); expansion joints (particle spreading = physical stress on materials); water anomaly (crystal structure = less dense = floats); FNMI freeze-thaw knowledge (long-term observation of real particle-level phenomena in nature).
Closing sentence must convey: Temperature is a measure of the average speed/kinetic energy of particles — not just how something looks.
