Forces — Workbook Answer Key

Grade 6 Science · Model answers and marking guidance for all 25 core questions and 4 stretch questions

Core Questions — Q1 to Q25

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q1 ★ Force definition + examples Definition: A force is a push or a pull acting on an object. Accept any wording that captures both ideas (push/pull, acting on something).
Examples: Any two reasonable forces from the student's morning — pushing a door open, pulling a backpack strap, gravity on their feet while walking, friction while walking, opening a locker, etc.
Q2 ★ External forces on box · identify + direction (a) Applied force · Direction: forward / in the direction of the push (same as motion).
(b) Friction · Direction: backward / opposite to the direction of motion.
Note: Also accept gravity (downward) and normal force (upward) as additional correct answers if students name them.
Q3 ★ Internal force — rope diagram Internal force type: Tension.
Particles inside the rope are being pulled apart — forces from both ends stretch the rope, pulling its particles away from each other in opposite directions.
Q4 ★ Elastic vs. plastic deformation · clay Plastic deformation. The clay kept its new (flat) shape after the force was removed — it did not return to its original shape. Elastic deformation would have meant the clay returned to its original round form. Since it stayed flat, the deformation was permanent = plastic.
Q5 ★ Newton's Third Law · state + properties Completion: "…the second object exerts an equal and opposite force back on the first object." Accept any accurate restatement.
Three properties: 1) Equal in magnitude (same size). 2) Opposite in direction. 3) Act on different objects. All three must be present.
Q6 ★ Force diagram · label + analyze Labels: Up arrow = Normal force. Down arrow = Gravity / Weight. Right arrow (longer) = Applied force. Left arrow (shorter) = Friction.
(a) Vertical forces are balanced — the box doesn't move up or down; normal force equals gravity, net vertical force = 0.
(b) Applied force is greater than friction → net force is to the right → box accelerates right. Unequal horizontal forces = acceleration in direction of larger force.
Q7 ★★ Friction misconception · error analysis (a) Error 1: Objects do not contain or "run out of" force — force is an interaction between two objects, applied from the outside, not stored inside. Error 2: The puck slowed because friction (an external force from the ice) acted on it — not because of something inside the puck decreasing.
(b) Correct: The hockey puck slowed because friction — a force from the ice surface — acted in the direction opposite to the puck's motion. Friction has a specific direction (opposite to motion) and a measurable size in Newtons. It is an external force from the ice, not something the puck possesses.
Q8 ★ Internal forces · three scenarios (a) Suspension bridge cable: Tension (particles pulled apart by the weight of the deck pulling down).
(b) Concrete column: Compression (floor weight pushes down, ground pushes up — particles squeezed together).
(c) Scissors cutting cardboard: Shear (two blades push layers of cardboard in opposite directions past each other).
Q9 ★ Elastic limit · paper clip (a) Elastic deformation (springs back).
(b) Plastic deformation (stays permanently bent).
(c) The first bend kept the force below the paper clip's elastic limit — it returned to its original shape. The sharp bend exceeded the elastic limit — the particles permanently rearranged and the clip stayed in its new shape (plastic deformation).
Q10 ★ Newton's Third Law · fish + water (a) Reaction force: Water pushes forward on the fish.
(b) Object: The fish.
(c) Action and reaction forces act on different objects — the action (fish pushing water) acts on the water; the reaction (water pushing fish) acts on the fish. Forces only cancel when they act on the same object. Since these act on different objects, they cannot cancel.
Q11 ★ Net force · calculate + predict (a) Net force = 15 N − 6 N = 9 N · Direction: right.
(b) Box accelerates to the right.
(c) If 15 N reduced to 6 N: net force = 6 − 6 = 0 N. Box would remain at rest (or continue at constant velocity if already moving) — balanced forces produce no acceleration.
Q12 ★ Friction direction · car braking (a) Friction acts south (opposite to direction of travel).
(b) Friction always acts opposite to the direction of motion — the car is moving north, so friction acts south.
(c) Before braking, net force was zero. Steady speed = zero acceleration = balanced forces. Engine force forward was balanced by friction and air resistance backward.
Q13 ★ Internal forces · three diagrams (a) Forces pushing inward from both sides: Compression.
(b) Two layers sliding in opposite directions: Shear.
(c) Forces pulling outward from both sides: Tension.
Q14 ★★ Engineering design · helmet vs. bridge cable (a) Plastically.
(b) Plastic deformation absorbs impact energy permanently — the foam crushes and stays crushed, meaning it dissipated the collision force rather than bouncing it back. Elastic deformation would return all energy as a rebound, transmitting the full impact force to the rider's head.
(c) Bridge cables must deform elastically — opposite because the cables must return to original length under every load cycle (traffic, wind, temperature changes). Permanent stretching (plastic deformation) would change bridge geometry and risk structural failure.
Q15 ★ Action-reaction pairs · valid or not (a) Not valid — gravity and normal force both act on the book (same object). An action-reaction pair must act on different objects.
(b) Valid — you push the wall (acts on wall); wall pushes you (acts on you). Different objects, equal, opposite. ✓
(c) Not valid — applied force and friction both act on the box (same object).
(d) Valid — Earth pulls book down (acts on book); book pulls Earth up (acts on Earth). Different objects, equal, opposite. ✓
Q16 ★★ Force diagram error · friction direction (a) Error: Friction must act opposite to the direction of motion. The box is sliding right and slowing — friction must point left. If the arrow is drawn pointing in the wrong direction (same as motion), that violates the definition of friction.
(b) Corrected diagram: Applied force → right; Friction → left (shorter or equal length to applied, with friction clearly labeled pointing opposite to motion).
(c) If friction genuinely pointed left (correct direction), then net force on the decelerating box = friction (left) > applied (right) → box slows and stops. This is consistent with the scenario.
Q17 ★ Shear and torsion · four scenarios (a) Wrench turning bolt: Torsion.
(b) River current cracking bank: Shear.
(c) Wringing towel: Torsion.
(d) Torsion involves twisting/rotating around a central axis (particles rotate in opposite directions on either end); shear involves layers sliding past each other in parallel but opposite directions — no rotation around a central axis.
Q18 ★ Friction on bike · four-part (a) Friction (from brake pads on wheel rim).
(b) Opposite to direction of motion (uphill / backward).
(c) Helpful: brake pads on rim (slowing); tires gripping road (propulsion). Against rider: chain/bearing friction (reduces pedaling efficiency).
(d) No — without friction, tires would skid on the road (no grip for steering or braking), brake pads would not grip the rim, and the rider could not even push off the ground. Friction is essential for the bike to function safely.
Q19 ★★ Newton's Third Law · truck/car misconception (a) No.
(b) Newton's Third Law: the force the truck exerts on the car is exactly equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the force the car exerts on the truck. Mass does not change this — the forces are always equal during the collision.
(c) The forces are equal, but the masses are very different. The same force causes much greater acceleration in the small-mass car than in the large-mass truck. Greater acceleration = greater speed change = greater structural damage to the lighter vehicle.
Q20 ★ Deformation sequence · spring diagram "After" diagram: spring returns to original length (same as BEFORE).
(a) Elastic deformation.
(b) The spring returned to its original shape after the force was removed — the defining feature of elastic deformation is return to original form.
(c) After being pushed past the elastic limit: the spring would stay permanently compressed/shorter — it would not fully return to its original length. The "After" diagram would show a shorter spring.
Q21 ★★ External → internal → deformation chain · chair (a) Applied force (downward).
(b) Compression (particles in the legs are squeezed together by the downward external force).
(c) Plastic deformation (leg cracked and stayed bent — permanent change).
(d) Full chain: the applied force (pushing down on the seat) created a compression internal force in the chair legs as particles were forced toward each other. When the force exceeded the elastic limit of the wood, the particles rearranged permanently — plastic deformation — and the leg cracked and remained bent.
Q22 ★ Draw force diagram · book on table Diagram: two equal-length arrows — 12 N pointing up (normal force) and 12 N pointing down (gravity). Arrows must be the same length.
(a) Net force = zero. The two 12 N forces are equal and opposite — they balance exactly.
(b) NOT an action-reaction pair. Both forces act on the same object (the book). An action-reaction pair must act on different objects. The actual Newton's Third Law pairs are: Earth pulls book down / book pulls Earth up; AND table pushes book up / book pushes table down.
Q23 ★★ Newton's Third Law error analysis · shopping cart (a) The student identified two forces both acting on the same object (the cart). Applied force AND friction both act on the cart. An action-reaction pair must act on different objects — you cannot have both forces in the pair acting on the same object.
(b) Correct reaction to "my push on the cart": the cart pushes me backward (equal, opposite, acts on me — a different object).
(c) Any valid pair: e.g., my feet push the floor backward (on floor) / floor pushes me forward (on me). OR: cart pushes me backward / I push cart forward. Both forces on different objects.
Q24 ★ Elastic force + deformation · archery (a) Force type: Elastic (spring) force.
(b) Direction: forward (in the direction the arrow is shot — the string restores toward its original position, pushing the arrow).
(c) Internal force in bowstring: Tension (string is being pulled/stretched from both ends).
(d) Plastic deformation (permanently changed — does not return to original shape).
Q25 ★★ Full force analysis · rock climber (a) External forces: Gravity (downward) and Tension from rope (upward).
(b) Internal force in rope: Tension.
(c) Net force = zero. The climber is motionless = not accelerating. If net force were non-zero, there would be acceleration. Balanced forces: tension up = gravity down.
(d) Valid pair: Climber pulls down on rope (acts on rope) / rope pulls climber up (acts on climber). Equal magnitude, opposite direction, different objects. ✓

Stretch Questions — S1 to S4

Stretch questions require multi-concept synthesis. Use as discussion guides or for extended assessment marking.
Q#Concept ClusterModel Answer Guide
S1 ★★ Force diagram + N3L · skateboarder (a) Force diagram must include: gravity (down), normal force (up from ground), friction from ground (forward — reaction to foot pushing back). All labeled, arrows proportional.
(b) Action-reaction pair: Foot pushes ground backward (on ground) / Ground pushes foot (skater) forward (on skater). Equal, opposite, different objects.
(c) Net horizontal force is forward (ground reaction > any resistive forces) → forward acceleration. If net force were zero, skater would move at constant speed, not accelerate.
S2 ★★ External → internal → deformation · foam pad (a) External force: Applied force (from the concrete block pressing down).
(b) Internal force: Compression (particles squeezed together).
(c) Deformation type: Plastic (stays crushed — permanent).
(d) Yes — same type. Helmet foam SHOULD deform plastically in a crash: plastic deformation absorbs the impact energy by permanently crushing, rather than bouncing the force back elastically toward the rider's head. The crushed foam is evidence the energy was absorbed. A used/crashed helmet must be replaced because the foam has already plastically deformed.
S3 ★★ Collision · N3L + deformation + why damage differs (a) Forces are equal during the collision. Newton's Third Law: the force the heavier car exerts on the lighter is exactly equal (and opposite) to the force the lighter car exerts on the heavier.
(b) Plastic deformation (permanently crushed).
(c) Plastic deformation dissipates impact energy over time and distance by permanently changing shape — "crumple zones." If the frame were elastic, it would rebound and transmit the full collision force instantly to the passenger compartment. Plastic deformation slows the force, reducing its effect on occupants.
(d) Correction: By Newton's Third Law, the forces are exactly equal — the heavier car did NOT exert more force. The lighter car is more damaged because the same force produces much greater acceleration in a smaller mass (a = F/m). Greater acceleration = greater deformation — not a greater force.
S4 ★★ Shock absorber design · all five concepts (a) Elastically — must return to original length after each bump to absorb the next one. Plastic deformation would leave it permanently compressed and non-functional.
(b) Internal force: Compression.
(c) External force: Applied force / impact from road (transmitted through the wheel).
(d) Small bump: force below elastic limit → elastic deformation → spring fully returns. Huge pothole: force exceeded elastic limit → partial plastic deformation → spring permanently shortened by 5 cm and stays compressed.
(e) Action-reaction pair: Damaged spring pushes up on bike frame (on frame) / bike frame pushes down on spring (on spring). Equal magnitude, opposite direction, different objects.
