Section 1 — What Is in Our Solar System?

Our solar system is our Sun and everything that orbits it.

Space is mostly empty. The distances between things are enormous.

Emit vs. reflect

Some things emit light — they make their own. Stars emit light.

Some things reflect light — they bounce light from something else. Planets and moons reflect the Sun's light. That is why we can see them at night.

Scale — how big is the solar system?

If the Sun were a basketball, Earth would be a tiny speck about 30 metres away.

Neptune would be about 1 kilometre away.

The solar system is almost entirely empty space.

Indigenous connection

Cree and Blackfoot peoples have observed the sky for generations. They used stars to navigate, to mark seasons, and to time ceremonies. This knowledge has been passed down for hundreds of years — far longer than any scientific telescope has existed. Indigenous sky knowledge and modern astronomy describe the same sky.

Natural and artificial satellites

A natural satellite formed on its own. Earth's Moon is a natural satellite.

An artificial satellite was built and put into orbit by humans. GPS satellites are artificial satellites.

Both stay in orbit because of gravity. They do not need engines to stay up.

Ex-Alta 1 — Alberta's satellite

Ex-Alta 1 is a small satellite called a CubeSat. It was built by students at the University of Alberta. It was launched into orbit in 2017 from the International Space Station.

It collects data about space weather — charged particles that affect Earth's magnetic field. It sends the data back to Earth using a radio signal.

Section 3 — Living in Space and Pluto

Challenges of living in space

Astronauts on the International Space Station (ISS) face big problems.

ChallengeWhy it is a problem
No breathable airMust bring oxygen or make it on board
MicrogravityMuscles and bones weaken without gravity. Must exercise 2 hours a day.
RadiationEarth's atmosphere normally blocks radiation. In space, there is much less protection.
Extreme temperaturesThe ISS goes from very hot to very cold as it moves in and out of sunlight.

Pluto — from planet to dwarf planet

Pluto was called a planet for many years. In 2006, scientists changed that. They reclassified Pluto as a dwarf planet.

Why? Astronomers discovered many other similar objects in the same region. Pluto does not clear its path the way full planets do.

Changing the classification was the right thing to do. It is an example of science working well — updating ideas when new evidence arrives.
