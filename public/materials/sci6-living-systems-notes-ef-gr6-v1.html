Section 1 — Living and Non-Living Things

An ecosystem is a place where living things and non-living things work together.

A pond. A forest. A field. These are all ecosystems.

Everything in an ecosystem is connected. If one part changes, all the other parts feel it.

Biotic — the living parts

Biotic means living. Biotic things are alive — or were once alive.

  • Plants, trees, grass
  • Animals, insects, birds
  • Bacteria and fungi that break things down

Abiotic — the non-living parts

Abiotic means non-living. Abiotic things were never alive.

  • Sunlight, water, air
  • Soil, rocks, temperature

Section 2 — How Plants Make Their Food

Plants do not eat food. They make their own food.

The process is called photosynthesis.

What goes in

  • Sunlight — captured by a green substance in leaves called chlorophyll
  • Water — taken in through the roots
  • Carbon dioxide — taken in through tiny holes in the leaves

What comes out

  • Glucose — the food the plant uses for energy. Stored as starch.
  • Oxygen — released into the air. The oxygen you breathe.
Sunlight + Water + Carbon dioxide ↓ PHOTOSYNTHESIS (in the leaves) ↓ Glucose + Oxygen

Section 3 — Food Webs and How Things Depend on Each Other

Living things depend on each other. This is called interdependence.

Animals need plants for food, oxygen, and shelter. Plants need animals to spread seeds and return nutrients to the soil.

Food chains and food webs

A food chain shows one path of energy. Grass → rabbit → fox.

A food web shows all the paths. Most animals eat more than one thing. Real ecosystems are food webs, not simple chains.

Sun ↓ Grass → Rabbit → Fox ↓ Insects → Frog → Hawk

When one part of a food web changes, the whole web feels it. This is called a trophic cascade.

Example: Remove wolves from a forest. Deer eat more plants. Plants disappear. Rivers change. This is a trophic cascade.

Sacred plants and Indigenous knowledge

Indigenous connection

Many Indigenous peoples — including Cree and Blackfoot — have cared for plants for generations. Sacred plants like sweetgrass, sage, cedar, and tobacco are important to ceremonies. They are also important to ecosystems. Indigenous knowledge holders understand how these plants affect the health of the whole ecosystem. This knowledge is a valid and important source of scientific understanding.

🔑 Word to know

🔑 Word to know

Remove one animal — the whole web shifts.
⚠️ Watch out
Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) are part of the ecosystem too.
They break things down and return nutrients to the soil. Without them, nothing would grow.
💡 Remember
Everything is connected. Change one thing and the whole ecosystem feels it.
