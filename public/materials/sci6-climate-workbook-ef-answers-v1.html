Climate — Easy Read Workbook Answer Key

Grade 6 Science · Easy Read version · Model answers for all 30 questions and 5 stretch questions

EF workbooks use word banks. For fill-in questions, accept any answer from the word bank that correctly fills the blank. For open response, accept any response that includes the key idea noted.

Section 1 — Weather, Climate, and the Greenhouse Effect (Q1–Q10)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q1 Weather vs Climate sort · table fill-in Raining today in Calgary = Weather · Alberta gets cold winters on average = Climate · Snowstorm last Tuesday = Weather · Amazon hot and humid year-round = Climate · Temperature today is 22°C = Weather
Q2 Greenhouse effect steps · fill-in list 1. sun's energy enters the atmosphere. 2. It warms Earth's surface. 3. greenhouse gases trap this heat. 4. Heat is re-radiated as heat back toward Earth. 5. This warms Earth.
Q3 Cold March misconception · word bank + explain Error: the student is using one event (a single cold month = weather) to argue against a long-term trend (climate). Climate is measured over decades of data — one cold month is not evidence for or against climate change.
Q4 Fossil fuels and greenhouse gases · fill-in millions of years · burning fossil fuels releases carbon dioxide · CO₂ is a greenhouse gas · too much CO₂ in the atmosphere traps more heat (in order)
Q5 Latitude and climate · fill-in Near the equator, sunlight hits at a direct angle → climate is warmer. Near the poles, sunlight is spread out over a large area → climate is colder.
Q6 Three greenhouse gases + sources · table fill-in carbon dioxide (CO₂) → burning fossil fuels · methane (CH₄) → livestock farming · nitrous oxide (N₂O) → fertilizers. Accept in any order as long as each gas is paired with its correct source.
Q7 Carbon sinks · word bank + explain A carbon sink absorbs CO₂ from the atmosphere. This helps reduce the amount of greenhouse gas in the air.
Q8 Three local climate factors · table fill-in Any three from: latitude · elevation · distance from ocean · mountains · ocean currents · prevailing winds. Accept any contextually correct example (e.g., latitude → tropical regions are warmer; mountains → rain shadow effect).
Q9 Natural climate change — evaluate · word bank + fix Nadia = Correct. Marcus = Incorrect. Fix Marcus: Earth's climate has changed before humans existed due to natural variation — including Milankovitch cycles (orbital changes) and volcanic eruptions. Humans have accelerated climate change since industrialization, but they did not cause all past climate change.
Q10 Natural vs enhanced greenhouse effect · fill-in Natural greenhouse effect: needed / makes Earth liveable. Without it, Earth would be −18°C. Enhanced greenhouse effect: caused by human activity → traps too much heat.

Section 2 — Evidence and Feedback Loops (Q11–Q20)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q11 Arctic albedo feedback loop · chain fill-in Arctic temperatures rise → ice melts → more dark ocean is exposed → ocean absorbs more heat → temperatures rise even more → this is a feedback loop (the effect makes itself worse).
Q12 Wildfires cascade · chain fill-in Temperatures hotter and conditions drier → leads to more wildfires → CO₂ released → less carbon stored → this warms climate further.
Q13 Greenhouse effect vs ozone hole · word bank + explain Greenhouse effect: heat trapping — greenhouse gases trap heat near Earth's surface, warming the planet. Ozone hole: thinning of the ozone layer allows more UV radiation to reach Earth's surface. They are different problems with different causes and effects.
Q14 Ice cores as evidence · word bank + fill-in Ice cores contain ancient air trapped in bubbles. Scientists can measure past CO₂ levels. Ice cores give data from hundreds of thousands of years ago.
Q15 Three types of climate evidence · word bank (choose 3) Any three from: ice cores · tree rings · coral records · ocean sediment · pollen records · historical documents.
Q16 Temperature trend table · word bank + describe The trend: temperatures are rising / warming trend / increasing — values go from 0.0 to +1.1°C over the century. Warming is accelerating (the jump from the 1990s to 2020s is larger than earlier jumps).
Q17 Climate change started in 2000? · misconception fill-in The student is not correct. Human-caused climate change started around the Industrial Revolution (the 1800s) when burning coal became widespread. Since then, CO₂ emissions have accelerated.
Q18 Climate models as computational thinking · word bank + explain A climate model uses inputs (CO₂, temperature data) and mathematical rules. It simulates future climate as outputs (predicts temperatures). This is an example of abstraction in computer science — the model simplifies the real world to make it computable.
Q19 Climate change is a future problem? · misconception + word bank The student is not correct. Three effects happening now (any three): Arctic ice melting · sea level rising · more wildfires · extreme weather · species shifting ranges · glaciers shrinking.
Q20 Tree rings in drought · word bank + describe During a drought, tree rings would be narrow rings. Because: dry conditions mean less growth — trees cannot grow as much when water is scarce.

Section 3 — Applying and Evaluating (Q21–Q30)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q21 Three personal actions · word bank (choose 3) Any three from: use public transit · eat less meat · reduce flying · use renewable energy · reduce waste · buy local food.
Q22 Two government/societal actions · word bank (choose 2) Any two from: carbon tax · renewable energy laws · public transit investment · building codes · international agreements.
Q23 Signal vs noise — graph interpretation · word bank + explain Student is wrong because: the dips are short-term variation (noise) within a clear long-term trend (signal). Year-to-year changes happen for many reasons. The correct interpretation: look at the overall pattern — temperatures have risen steadily from 1880 to 2020 despite individual dips.
Q24 Mitigation vs adaptation · word bank + examples Mitigation means: reduce emissions (slow climate change). Example: solar panels OR switching to electric cars. Adaptation means: adjust to changes (prepare for effects already happening). Example: building sea walls.
Q25 Albedo feedback loop — Arctic ice · chain fill-in Ice melts → less ice → more ocean exposed → darker surface absorbs more heat → temperatures rise even more → this is called a positive feedback loop (warming accelerates itself).
Q26 Canada's north warms faster — two effects · word bank (choose 2) Any two from: permafrost thawing · methane released · infrastructure damaged · Indigenous communities affected · wildlife habitat changed · sea ice lost.
Q27 Tree rings cross-validated · word bank + explain Tree rings are reliable as climate evidence. Because: the tree ring data matches / confirms the recorded weather data from the 1930s drought — this is called cross-validation. When two independent types of evidence agree, the evidence is stronger.
Q28 Climate justice — open response · word bank scaffolding Accept any reasoned position. Award marks for using at least two WB terms. Key ideas to look for: wealthier developed countries have produced more historical emissions; developing countries contributed less but face significant impacts; equal percentage cuts may be unfair; climate justice asks who bears the burden of action.
Q29 TEK and Bow River · word bank + explain TEK adds to scientific records because: it provides long-term observations spanning generations — far longer than instrument records. It carries local knowledge of specific places. TEK can fill in gaps in scientific data from the pre-instrument era — it confirms or extends what instruments later recorded.
Q30 School carbon footprint action plan · table fill-in Accept any two actions from the word bank with a plausible benefit and one realistic challenge. Examples: reduce electricity use → benefit: lower emissions / challenge: requires behaviour change. plant trees → benefit: absorbs CO₂ / challenge: trees take years to grow. No single right answer — award marks for two valid WB actions each paired with a stated benefit and challenge.

Stretch Zone (S1–S5)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
S1 97% consensus vs misleading headline · open response The headline implies equal disagreement, but 97% of climate scientists agree humans are causing warming — that is a strong scientific consensus, not a debate. Misleading because: a minority view is presented as if it balances a majority. Questions to ask: Who published this? How many scientists? Is this peer-reviewed? What does the majority of evidence show?
S2 Permafrost methane feedback loop · draw + explain Loop: warming → permafrost thaws → bacteria decompose frozen organic matter → methane (CH₄) released → CH₄ is 80× more powerful than CO₂ → much more warming → more permafrost thaws → loop continues. Concerning because: this is a runaway positive feedback — once it starts, it is self-reinforcing and very difficult to stop.
S3 Technology alone to fix climate change · open response Advantage: technology solutions (like carbon capture) can be scaled without requiring widespread behaviour change; they may work faster once developed. Disadvantage: carbon capture technology is not yet proven at scale; relying on it alone risks delay — it may allow continued emissions while waiting for a technology that may not arrive in time; it does not address the root cause.
S4 Multiple data sources · open response Important because: each data source has limitations — satellite records are recent, ice cores are deep but not everywhere, weather stations have coverage gaps. Using multiple sources allows cross-validation — when sources agree, confidence is higher; when they disagree, it flags an issue to investigate. Single source problem: could have instrument error, geographic bias, or gaps in coverage.
S5 One climate effect — three impacts · open response Example answer (wildfire): Ecosystem — forest habitat destroyed, species lose homes, soil erodes. Human community — air quality drops, infrastructure damaged, evacuation costs. Indigenous community — loss of traditional territory, medicinal plants destroyed, traditional hunting/gathering disrupted. Accept any climate effect with a reasoned impact across all three categories.
