Section 2 — Temperature, Expansion, and Changes (Q11–Q20)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q11 Cooling — particle behavior · fill-in energy · slower · closer together · contract
Q12 Contraction — bridge in winter · word bank + explain contracts (gets smaller). Explanation: Cold removes energy from particles → they slow down and move closer together → the metal takes up less space. Accept: particles slow/come closer → bridge shrinks.
Q13 Dissolving — conservation of mass · word bank + explain No, the student is wrong. Explanation: Dissolving is a physical change. The salt particles are still there — they are just spread out among the water particles. Mass is conserved.
Q14 Physical vs. chemical changes · sort table Ice melting = Physical · Wood burning = Chemical · Sugar dissolving = Physical · Iron rusting = Chemical · Glass breaking = Physical
Q15 Ice floats — evaluate explanations · word bank + fix Amara = Correct. Deshi = Incorrect. Fix Deshi: Ice does not lose mass when it freezes. The particles spread farther apart (different arrangement), making ice less dense than liquid water — that is why it floats. Accept any response that (1) corrects the mass claim and (2) uses density/particle spacing to explain floating.
Q16 Thermometer — liquid expansion · word bank + explain rises. Why: Particles gain energy → move faster → spread farther apart → liquid expands → takes up more space → level in tube rises.
Q17 Dissolving rate — temperature effect · word bank + explain hot water. Because: Hot water particles move faster → collide more with sugar particles → break sugar apart faster → sugar dissolves more quickly.
Q18 Separation — muddy water · word bank + explain filtration. How: A filter has tiny pores. Water particles are small enough to pass through. Mud particles are too big → they stay on the filter.
Q19 Separation — salt water · word bank + explain evaporation. How: Water particles gain enough energy to escape as gas (evaporate). Salt particles do not evaporate → they are left behind in solid form.
Q20 Particle motion in solids · True/False + explain False. Explain: Particles in a solid always vibrate (move back and forth) in fixed positions. They never stop moving completely — they just cannot move from place to place.

Section 3 — Applying and Evaluating (Q21–Q30)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
Q21 TEK + particle model · open response Accept any response connecting: thick ice = particles tightly packed = safer; thin/porous/blue-grey ice = particles more spread out or mixed with water = unsafe. Indigenous knowledge about ice texture and colour connects to the same particle-level ideas as the particle model.
Q22 Models and abstraction · open response A model leaves out: exact particle shape, colour, quantum behavior, forces between particles, chemical bonding. This is okay because: models show only the key features needed to explain observations — they are useful even if not perfect.
Q23 Limits of particle model · open response Accept any one: forces between particles / why particles stick together / how particles attract or repel each other / why different particles have different properties. The particle model describes behavior but does not explain the causes of those forces.
Q24 Dissolving — misconception · word bank + explain wrong. What actually happens: Salt particles are still there — they are mixed in among the water particles. Dissolving is a physical change. The particles do not become water particles.
Q25 Ice on lakes — apply particle model · open response Step 1: Ice is less dense than liquid water → floats to surface → forms layer on top. Step 2: Ice layer insulates the water below → water below stays liquid → fish can breathe (oxygen dissolved in liquid water). Accept any response capturing both steps.
Q26 Boiling — particle model · open response Boiling means: particles at and below the surface gain enough energy to escape as gas (evaporation happening rapidly throughout the liquid, not just at the surface). Accept: fast evaporation / particles gain enough energy to become gas / bubbles of gas form inside the liquid.
Q27 Material properties — frying pan handle · word bank + explain low heat conductivity. Because: The handle must not transfer heat energy from the hot pan to the hand. A material with low heat conductivity does not pass energy through it easily.
Q28 Freeze-thaw weathering · open response Water enters crack → water freezes → expands (particles spread farther apart) → pushes against rock walls → crack widens → repeat over many cycles. This is called freeze-thaw weathering.
Q29 Measurement error · word bank + fix parallax error. Fix: Look at the ruler straight on — eye level with the measurement mark, not from an angle.
Q30 Apply particle model — own example · open response Accept any valid everyday example (e.g., steam rising from tea, road gaps, ice in a drink, cooking pasta). Award marks for: naming the example, identifying which particle model idea applies (moving, spacing, energy, phase change), and giving a particle-level explanation.

Stretch Zone (S1–S5)

Q#Concept / TypeAnswer / Marking Guide
S1 Investigation design · open response Change: temperature of water (e.g., cold / room temp / hot). Measure: time for a set amount of sugar to dissolve. Keep same: amount of sugar, amount of water, type of sugar, stirring (or no stirring). Accept any two controlled variables.
S2 Water anomaly — life on Earth · open response Ice floats → insulates water below → lakes and rivers do not freeze solid → aquatic life survives through winter. If ice sank, lakes would freeze solid from the bottom up → most aquatic life would die. Accept any response capturing: ice floats → insulation → life survives.
S3 Road gaps — thermal expansion · open response In summer, road material heats up → particles spread farther apart → material expands. Without gaps, sections would push against each other and buckle or crack. Gaps give the material room to expand safely.
S4 Scientific claims — counter-example · open response Not always true. Water between 0°C and 4°C actually contracts as it warms — this is the opposite of normal expansion behavior. This counter-example disproves the claim that ALL substances always expand when heated. Accept any clear explanation of water's unusual behavior near 0°C.
S5 Dry ice — sublimation · word bank + explain Phase change name: sublimation. Useful because: Dry ice stays very cold without creating liquid water (no mess, no soaking). It keeps food frozen while evaporating cleanly as gas.
