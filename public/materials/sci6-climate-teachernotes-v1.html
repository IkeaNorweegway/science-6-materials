Pre-Knowledge Check — Facilitation Guide

Open-ended: what do students already know about climate and climate change? Collect baseline thinking — especially the greenhouse effect vs. ozone hole confusion, and "climate change is a future problem."

Show

Board: Pre-Knowledge Check — Climate
Open-ended prompt visible (or distributed on paper). Five lines for writing.

Say

"Write everything you think you know about climate and climate change. What is climate? How is it different from weather? What causes it to change? What effects have you noticed or heard about?"

"No marks. No correction today. I want your honest first thoughts — we'll come back to this page at the end of the unit. The goal is to see how your thinking changes."

Cue — ★ Write

Students write for 4–5 minutes. Collect mental notes on: who distinguishes weather vs. climate? Who mentions the ozone hole? Who thinks effects are future? This is your baseline.

Misconception Flag — Pre-K Baseline: Watch for These
  • "Climate change is making the ozone hole bigger" — greenhouse effect ≠ ozone hole. Flag for Section 2.
  • "We'll deal with it in the future" — climate change is present-tense in Alberta. Flag for Section 4.
  • "Weather and climate are basically the same thing" — different timescales. Core focus of Section 1.
  • "One cold winter proves climate change is fake" — signal vs. noise. Core focus of Section 1.

Do not correct in the pre-K check. You want the starting point. The unit corrections will land harder if students remember being wrong here.

Section 1 — Weather vs. Climate

Lesson 1 · Student notes: Section 1

Beat 1.1 — The Core Distinction

Show

Board: Two-column heading: WEATHER | CLIMATE
Under weather: "specific place + specific time — hours to days"
Under climate: "average pattern — decades"

Say

"Weather is the state of the atmosphere at a specific place and specific time — hour to hour, day to day. 'It's raining in Edmonton right now.' That's weather."

"Climate is the average pattern of weather in a region, measured over long periods — usually at least 30 years. 'Edmonton gets about 60 days of below -20°C temperatures in a typical winter.' That's climate."

"Weather can change from one day to the next. Climate describes what is typical over many decades."

"Fill the blanks: weather is at a [specific] place and [specific] time. Climate is the [average] pattern measured over [decades / at least 30 years]."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill: specific · specific · average · decades. Vocabulary table: Weather, Climate, Atmospheric conditions, Climate trend.

Beat 1.2 — The Timescale Evidence Problem

Show

Board: Draw the timescale comparison from the notes:
Weather: |—day—|—day—|—day—| → "Should I wear a coat?"
Climate: |——10 years——|——10 years——|——10 years——| → "Is Alberta getting warmer?"
Then draw the signal vs. noise graph: scattered data points around a rising trend line.

Say

"A single cold week in January tells us nothing about climate. A single hot summer does not confirm climate change. These are individual data points — noise in the data."

"Climate claims require long-term data — measurements collected consistently over decades, across many locations. The blank is 'long-term' in that sentence."

"Look at this graph. Each dot is one year's temperature. Some dots are high, some are low. But the trend line moves upward over 50 years. The individual dots are noise. The trend line is the climate signal."

"When scientists make claims about climate change, they're talking about the trend line — not any individual dot."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill: individual data points = [single/individual]. Climate claims require [long-term] data. Connection prompt: write before reading ahead. Vocabulary: Long-term data.

Beat 1.3 — Alberta Examples + Why Timescale Matters for Claims

Show

Board: Three-row table — Observation | What it tells us
Row 1: "Edmonton had its coldest recorded February in 2024" → Weather
Row 2: "Average winter temperatures in Alberta have risen 2°C over 50 years" → Climate trend
Row 3: "July 2023 was the hottest month globally since records began" → Possible signal (needs context)

Say

"The timescale of your evidence must match the timescale of your claim. That's the key rule."

"A claim like 'climate change isn't real because it snowed in May' confuses weather — a short event — with climate — a long-term pattern. These are different questions requiring different evidence."

"Three blanks in that paragraph: [weather] · [climate] · [timescale] of your claim."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: confuses [weather] with [climate] · timescale of your [claim].

Misconception Flag — "One cold year proves climate change is wrong"

This is the most common misconception students bring in. Address it directly using the signal vs. noise diagram — one data point does not define a trend. The trend is defined by the full dataset, not the exceptions inside it.

Key line: "A single cold year inside a long-term warming trend is still a warming trend."

Point students to the misconception callout in their notes — have them read it, then check understanding with retrieval check Q3.

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Weather vs. climate: Weather = state of atmosphere at a specific place and time (hours to days). Climate = average pattern of weather in a region over decades (at least 30 years).
  2. Minimum years for climate trend: At least 30 years of data.
  3. Cold March reasoning error: Confuses weather (a short-term event) with climate (a long-term trend). One cold month is noise inside a trend — it doesn't define the trend. Climate claims require decades of data, not individual months.
  4. Alberta examples: Weather = any short-term observation (snowfall last Tuesday, yesterday's high temperature). Climate trend = any long-term pattern (average temperatures rising over 50 years, wildfire seasons getting longer).
  5. Two problems: Timescale: 2 years is too short — need at least 30 years to identify a climate trend. Scale: one station is too limited — global climate claims require data from many locations worldwide.

Section 2 — Earth's Climate Systems

Lessons 2–3 · Student notes: Section 2

Misconception Flag — Greenhouse Effect ≠ Ozone Hole (CRITICAL — Highest-Stakes Error in Unit)

Students routinely conflate these. It is not a minor error — confusing them leads to wrong conclusions about both cause and solution. Address it explicitly in Lesson 2 when introducing the greenhouse effect, and again in Lesson 5 when covering human causes.

The dedicated comparison table is at the bottom of the student notes package. Use it in class — do not just leave it for students to find.

The one-line distinction: Greenhouse effect = CO₂ in the lower atmosphere trapping heat. Ozone hole = CFCs destroying the upper atmosphere's UV shield. Different gases. Different layers. Different effects. Different solutions.

Beat 2.1 — The Sun and What Happens to Incoming Energy

Show

Board: Draw Earth with the Sun. Three arrows for sunlight: one bouncing back (reflected), one hitting land/ocean (absorbed by surface), one hitting clouds (absorbed by atmosphere).
Label with "reflected" / "absorbed by surface" / "absorbed by atmosphere."

Say

"The Sun is the engine of Earth's climate. It sends energy as solar radiation — light and heat. When that energy reaches Earth, three things can happen."

"Some is reflected back into space — especially by clouds and ice. Some is absorbed by the land and ocean. Some is absorbed by the atmosphere."

"The energy absorbed by Earth's surface warms it. That warmth then rises back toward space as infrared radiation — heat."

"Blanks: solar [radiation] · reflected · absorbed by [land and ocean] / [the atmosphere] · infrared [radiation]."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: [solar] radiation · [reflected] · absorbed by the [land and ocean] · absorbed by the [atmosphere] · rises as [infrared] radiation. Label the greenhouse effect diagram arrows.

Beat 2.2 — The Natural Greenhouse Effect

Show

Board: Draw the greenhouse effect diagram from the notes — atmosphere layer with arrows: sunlight in (↓), heat rising from surface (↑), atmosphere re-emitting heat back down (↓).
Label: "Greenhouse gases absorb outgoing heat and re-emit it back."

Say

"The atmosphere contains gases that act like the glass in a greenhouse. They let sunlight in — but slow heat from leaving."

"When heat rises from Earth's surface, greenhouse gases absorb it and re-emit it back toward Earth. This is the natural greenhouse effect."

"Here's the key word: natural. The greenhouse effect is essential for life. Without it, Earth's average temperature would be approximately -18°C — too cold for liquid water and most life."

"The blanks: greenhouse gases let [sunlight] in, slow [heat] from leaving. They [absorb] and [re-emit] heat back. Without the greenhouse effect: [-18]°C."

"Now — here is what I want you to know before we go any further. The greenhouse effect is NOT the same as the ozone hole. They involve different gases, different layers of the atmosphere, and different effects. I'll come back to this with a table — but hold that distinction in your head."

Cue — ★ Write + ✎ Draw

Blanks: let [sunlight] in · slow [heat] from leaving · gases [absorb] and [re-emit] · -18°C. Students label the three arrows on the greenhouse diagram in their notes.

Beat 2.3 — Greenhouse Gases and the Problem of Amplification

Show

Board: Three-row table — Gas | Natural source: Water vapour | CO₂ | Methane.
Add: "Natural GHGs keep Earth at ~15°C average. Problem begins when human activities add MORE."

Say

"Three main greenhouse gases and their natural sources. Water vapour — evaporation from oceans and lakes. Carbon dioxide — respiration and volcanic eruptions. Methane — wetlands, decomposition."

"These are a normal part of Earth's atmosphere. They keep Earth at about 15°C — warm enough for life."

"The problem begins when human activities add extra amounts of these gases. More gas → more heat trapped. The blanks: greenhouse gases are a [normal] part of Earth's atmosphere. Average temperature ~[15]°C. Problem begins when humans add [extra/more] amounts, trapping [more] heat."

Cue — ★ Write

Greenhouse gases table completed with natural sources. Blanks: [normal] part · [15]°C · [extra / more] amounts · trapping [more] heat. Vocabulary: Greenhouse effect, Greenhouse gas.

Beat 2.4 — Geographic Factors: Latitude, Water, Elevation, Winds

Show

Board: Four-section layout — one for each geographic factor:
1. Latitude diagram (equator = direct rays, poles = angled rays)
2. Water proximity (coastal = mild, inland = extreme)
3. Elevation (-6.5°C per 1000m)
4. Prevailing winds (off ocean = mild/moist; off land/north = cold/dry)

Say

"Earth doesn't have one climate — it has many. Four factors explain most of the variation."

"One: Latitude. Closer to the equator, sunlight hits directly — more energy per square metre. Near the poles, sunlight arrives at an angle, spread over a larger area — less energy. This is why the tropics are warm and polar regions are cold."

"Two: Proximity to large water bodies. Water heats up and cools down slower than land. Coastal regions have moderate temperatures — winters not as cold, summers not as hot. Inland Alberta has extreme ranges — very cold winters, hot summers."

"Three: Elevation. Higher = colder. About 6.5°C cooler per 1000 metres gained. Banff is colder than Calgary even in the same region."

"Four: Prevailing winds. Winds carry temperature and moisture. Off the ocean — mild and moist. Off land or from the north — cold and dry."

Cue — ★ Write

Latitude blanks: [equator] → warm · [poles] → cold · tropics are [warm] / poles are [cold].
Water blanks: heats/cools [slower] · coastal = [moderate] · inland = [extreme] temperature ranges.
Elevation: higher → [colder] temperature · mountain towns are [colder].
Winds: carry [temperature] and [moisture] · ocean winds = [mild/moist] · land/north winds = [cold/dry]. Vocabulary: Latitude, Prevailing winds, Elevation.

Beat 2.5 — Albedo and the Ice-Albedo Feedback Loop

Show

Board: Two-column albedo comparison: fresh snow (~90% reflected) vs. dark ocean (~6% reflected).
Then draw the ice-albedo feedback loop: Ice melts → darker surface exposed → absorbs MORE heat → more ice melts → (cycle).

Say

"Albedo is a measure of how much sunlight a surface reflects. High albedo = reflects a lot = lighter surface. Low albedo = absorbs a lot = darker surface."

"Fresh snow: about 90% of sunlight reflected. Dark ocean: about 6%. Ice sheets: very high albedo. Forests: low to medium."

"Here's where it gets important. When ice and snow melt, darker land or ocean is exposed. That darker surface absorbs more heat. More heat melts more ice. More melted ice exposes more dark surface. The cycle reinforces itself — this is called an ice-albedo feedback loop."

"This is why warming in the Arctic is accelerating faster than the global average — the feedback loop is active there."

Cue — ★ Write

Albedo blanks: how much [sunlight / light] a surface reflects · snow ~[90]% · ocean ~[6]%.
Feedback blanks: when ice/snow [melt] → darker surface absorbs [more] heat → melts [more] ice. Vocabulary: Albedo.

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Three things that happen to sunlight reaching Earth: (a) Reflected back into space by clouds and ice. (b) Absorbed by the land and ocean. (c) Absorbed by the atmosphere.
  2. Natural greenhouse effect: Greenhouse gases in the atmosphere let sunlight in but slow outgoing heat (infrared radiation) from escaping. When heat rises from Earth's surface, GHGs absorb it and re-emit it back toward Earth, keeping the planet warmer than it would be otherwise (~15°C instead of ~-18°C).
  3. Two greenhouse gases + natural sources: Any two from: CO₂ (respiration, volcanic eruptions) · methane / CH₄ (wetlands, decomposition) · water vapour / H₂O (evaporation from oceans/lakes).
  4. Coastal BC vs. Edmonton: BC is coastal — proximity to the Pacific Ocean moderates temperatures (water heats and cools more slowly than land). Edmonton is inland — extreme temperature range with very cold winters. Same latitude, different proximity to large water body.
  5. Albedo feedback loop: Sea ice has high albedo (~90% reflected). When it melts, dark ocean water is exposed (~6% reflected). Ocean absorbs far more heat → warms faster → melts more sea ice → exposes more dark ocean. Each step amplifies the warming — a self-reinforcing feedback loop.

Section 3 — Causes of Climate Change

Lessons 4–5 · Student notes: Section 3

Beat 3.1 — Natural Causes

Show

Board: Four-item list — Natural causes:
1. Volcanic eruptions
2. Milankovitch cycles (orbital changes)
3. Solar output variation
4. Large meteor impacts

Say

"Earth's climate has always changed — long before humans existed. Understanding what caused those changes is what lets us know what's causing the changes happening now."

"Four natural causes. Volcanic eruptions: release CO₂ and aerosol particles that temporarily block sunlight, causing short-term cooling — not warming."

"Milankovitch cycles: over tens of thousands of years, Earth's orbit changes shape — circular to elliptical. This changes how much solar energy we receive at different times of year. These cycles explain the timing of past ice ages."

"Solar output variation: the Sun doesn't put out constant energy. It varies by about 0.1% over an 11-year cycle."

"Meteor impacts: a very large impact can throw massive dust into the atmosphere, blocking sunlight for months or years."

"Blanks: volcanic eruptions release [CO₂] and particles that [block] sunlight causing [cooling] · orbit [changes] · ice [ages] · [0.1]% · massive [dust]."

Cue — ★ Write

Natural causes blanks: [CO₂] and particles · temporarily [block] sunlight · short-term [cooling] · orbit [changes / shape] · [ice] ages · [0.1]% solar variation · [massive / enormous] dust.

Beat 3.2 — Human Causes (Fossil Fuels + Deforestation)

Show

Board: Carbon stored vs. carbon released diagram — underground storage (millions of years) vs. combustion (decades).
Then: deforestation two-step — CO₂ released when burned + future CO₂ absorption lost.

Say

"Since the Industrial Revolution — roughly 1850 — human activities have added greenhouse gases to the atmosphere far faster than natural processes alone."

"Burning fossil fuels: coal, oil, and natural gas are made of carbon stored underground for millions of years. Burning releases that stored carbon as CO₂ into the atmosphere very rapidly. This is the largest human contributor."

"Deforestation: trees absorb CO₂ through photosynthesis. Cut and burn a forest — two things happen. The CO₂ stored in the tree is released. And the trees that would have absorbed future CO₂ are gone."

"Connect to Living Systems: where did the carbon in coal originally come from? Plants that absorbed CO₂ through photosynthesis millions of years ago. Fossil fuels are compressed ancient organic matter — photosynthesis that ran millions of years ago."

"Blanks: far [faster/more] than natural processes · stored [carbon] as CO₂ · [largest] human contributor · [photosynthesis] · CO₂ [released] · trees [gone/removed]."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: far [faster / more] than natural · stored [carbon] as CO₂ · [largest] human contributor · through [photosynthesis] · CO₂ [released] · trees that would have absorbed future CO₂ are [gone]. Connection prompt completed. Vocabulary: Fossil fuels, Deforestation, Carbon cycle.

Beat 3.3 — Human Causes (Methane + Industrial Pollution)

Show

Board: Methane sources — cattle (enteric fermentation) · flooded rice paddies.
Add: Methane traps ~80× more heat per molecule than CO₂ over 20 years.

Say

"Methane — CH₄ — is a greenhouse gas that is far more powerful than CO₂ over a 20-year period. Two main human sources: cattle and other ruminant livestock produce methane during digestion. Flooded rice paddies produce methane through bacterial decomposition."

"Industrial agriculture is a significant methane source — this is why diet is sometimes mentioned in climate discussions."

"Industrial pollution: factories and agriculture also release nitrous oxide — N₂O — from fertilisers, and various industrial gases with very high warming potential."

"Blanks: produce [methane] during digestion · methane traps [80 / eighty] times more heat per molecule than CO₂ · [nitrous] oxide."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: produce [methane] · traps [80] times more heat · [nitrous] oxide (N₂O). Vocabulary: Amplified greenhouse effect.

Beat 3.4 — Why Human Causes Now Dominate

Show

Board: Three natural drivers status:
Solar output → stable / not increased enough
Volcanic activity → would cause cooling, not warming
Orbital cycles → operate over 10,000s of years
Then: the only variable that matches timing and rate = [human CO₂]

Say

"Natural climate drivers have not changed significantly in the last 150 years. Solar output: stable — not increased enough to explain observed warming. Volcanic activity: if anything, recent major eruptions cause temporary cooling. Orbital cycles: these operate over tens of thousands of years — they can't explain 150-year warming."

"The only major change that matches the timing and rate of observed warming is the rapid increase in atmospheric CO₂ and other greenhouse gases from human activity."

"Blanks: solar [stable] · volcanic [cooling] · orbital [tens of thousands of years] · [human] activity."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: [stable / not increased] · causes [cooling] · [tens of thousands of years] · [human] activity.

Misconception Flag — "Climate has always changed, so humans aren't causing it"

Address this directly after Beat 3.4. The misconception conflates "climate has changed naturally in the past" with "current change is natural."

Key evidence to cite:

  • Ice core data shows CO₂ today (~420 ppm) is higher than at any point in 800,000 years of natural cycles.
  • Rate of change: natural climate shifts happen over thousands of years. Current warming happened in ~150 years.
  • The pattern and speed match human fossil fuel use, not any natural cycle scientists can identify.

Point students to the misconception callout in their notes — have them read it and then write the connection prompt.

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Two natural causes: Any two from: volcanic eruptions (particles block sunlight → temporary cooling) · Milankovitch orbital cycles (change amount of solar energy received → ice ages over tens of thousands of years) · solar output variation (11-year cycle, ~0.1% variation) · large meteor impacts (dust blocks sunlight → cooling).
  2. Forest burned — stored carbon: Two things: the CO₂ stored in tree tissue is released into the atmosphere; and the trees that would have continued absorbing CO₂ through photosynthesis are gone. Double CO₂ hit.
  3. Fossil fuels as largest contributor: Carbon that was stored underground for millions of years is released as CO₂ in decades. The volume of CO₂ released far exceeds what any natural process adds on human timescales. And it matches the timing of observed warming from ~1850 onward.
  4. Challenge "natural variation" claim: Rate of change — natural cycles take thousands to tens of thousands of years; current warming happened in 150 years. CO₂ levels — ice cores show today's CO₂ (~420 ppm) is higher than any point in 800,000 years of natural cycles. Natural drivers (solar, volcanic, orbital) haven't changed enough to explain observed warming.
  5. Methane reduction: Sources: cattle/ruminant digestion (enteric fermentation) · flooded rice paddies. Why faster benefit: methane is ~80× more potent than CO₂ per molecule over 20 years, but it also breaks down faster in the atmosphere. Reducing methane produces faster climate benefits than CO₂ alone because its short-term warming impact is higher and its atmospheric lifetime is shorter.

Section 4 — Effects of Climate Change in Alberta

Lessons 6–7 · Student notes: Section 4

Misconception Flag — "Climate Change Is a Future Problem"

Address this immediately at the start of Section 4. All effects in this section are present-tense observations, not forecasts. Alberta's average temperature has already risen ~1.5°C since 1950. Wildfire seasons are already measurably longer. Glaciers are already smaller. These changes have already happened.

Opening line for this section: "Everything in this section has already happened. These are not predictions."

Beat 4.1 — Changes in Weather Patterns

Show

Board: Alberta's climate is already shifting:
• Average temperature: +1.5°C since 1950
• Spring arrives earlier
• Winters shorter
• Summer heat events more frequent

Say

"Everything I'm about to say has already happened. These are measurements, not forecasts."

"Alberta's average annual temperature has risen approximately 1.5°C since 1950. That sounds small — but 1.5°C is the difference between a river staying frozen and beginning to thaw. It shifts entire ecological systems."

"Spring arrives earlier — ice breaks up on rivers earlier, birds migrate earlier, insects emerge earlier. Winter periods are shorter — fewer consecutive days of extreme cold. Summer heat events are more frequent and more intense."

"Blanks: [1.5]°C · spring arrives [earlier] · winters [shorter] · heat events [more] frequent."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: [1.5]°C since 1950 · spring arrives [earlier] · winters [shorter] · heat events [more] frequent.

Beat 4.2 — Extreme Weather Events

Show

Board: Warmer atmosphere = holds more moisture
→ More intense rainfall when it rains
→ More severe hailstorms
→ More intense drought between rain events (ground dries faster)

Say

"A warmer atmosphere holds more moisture. More moisture means more intense events — not necessarily more frequent rain, but heavier rain when it does rain."

"Alberta has already seen increases in extreme weather events: the 2013 Calgary floods — billions of dollars in damage from intense rainfall. More severe hailstorms."

"Paradox: a warmer atmosphere also produces more intense drought between rain events — the ground dries out faster in higher heat. So more moisture in the air but drier soil. Both happen."

"Blanks: holds [more] moisture · [more] intense rainfall · [more] severe hailstorms · [extreme] weather events."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: holds [more] moisture · [more] intense rainfall · [more] severe hailstorms · [extreme] weather events.

Beat 4.3 — Wildfire Season

Show

Board: Alberta wildfire season = measurably longer
Contributing factors: warmer/drier summers · less snowpack · earlier spring snowmelt.
Examples: Fort McMurray 2016 · Donnie Creek 2023.

Say

"Alberta's wildfire season has grown measurably longer — begins earlier in spring, ends later in fall. Contributing factors: warmer, drier summers that dry out vegetation faster. Less snowpack entering spring, so forests are already drier. Earlier spring snowmelt means more months with flammable conditions."

"The 2016 Fort McMurray wildfire and 2023 Donnie Creek fire are examples of the kind of severe fire events associated with climate-driven drying."

"Blanks: wildfire season [longer] · warmer [drier] summers · less [snowpack] · earlier spring [snowmelt]."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: wildfire season [longer] · warmer [drier] summers · less [snowpack] · earlier spring [snowmelt].

Beat 4.4 — Glaciers and Water Resources

Show

Board: Athabasca Glacier before/after images (or sketch a timeline of retreat).
Label: "Source → Rivers → Farms and cities." Show early melt increase → future long-term decline.

Say

"Alberta's mountain glaciers — in the Rockies — are the summer water source for major rivers: the Bow, Athabasca, North Saskatchewan. Through summer, these glaciers release meltwater, keeping rivers flowing even when rain is scarce."

"Alberta's glaciers have been shrinking since the late 1800s, and the rate is accelerating."

"Shorter term: river flow may actually increase in early summer as glaciers melt faster. Longer term: as glaciers become too small to provide sustained meltwater, river flow will decrease later in the century."

"Communities and farms that depend on glacier-fed irrigation will face water shortages."

"Blanks: glaciers are the [source] for rivers · glaciers have been [shrinking] · river flow [may increase] then [decrease] · [glacier-fed] irrigation."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: [source / water supply] for rivers · have been [shrinking] · river flow [may increase] early · [will decrease] later · [glacier-fed] irrigation. Vocabulary: Glacier.

Beat 4.5 — Agricultural Shifts

Show

Board: Agricultural change — four bullet points:
• New crops possible (warmer = longer season)
• Existing crops face more heat/drought stress
• Pest and disease ranges shifting northward
• Farmers must adapt planting, water use, crop choices

Say

"Agriculture is shifting. As temperatures rise and growing seasons change — some crops that once only grew farther south can now be grown in Alberta. That's the partial truth in 'climate change might be good for farmers.'"

"But: crops that once grew well in southern Alberta now face greater heat and drought stress. Pest and disease ranges are shifting northward as winters become less severe — new pests reach crops that didn't face them before. Farmers must adapt."

"Blanks: crops once grew farther [south] · greater [heat/drought] stress · pest ranges shifting [northward]."

Cue — ★ Write

Blanks: farther [south] · greater [heat/drought] stress · shifting [northward].

Beat 4.6 — Impacts on FNMI Communities

Pacing Flag — Lesson 7 Needs Space

FNMI community impacts should occupy most of Lesson 7. This is not a checkbox — it is a core content section. If possible, use a primary source: a recorded Elder, a news report from an affected community, or a case study. The goal is for students to understand that climate change is not affecting everyone equally, and that disrupting traditional practices has consequences for food security, cultural continuity, and community health — not just inconvenience.

Show

Board: Three-section structure:
1. Ice roads — formation, purpose, what happens when they fail
2. Traditional harvesting seasons — shifting timing, misalignment with traditional knowledge
3. Medicinal and sacred plants — moving, declining, knowledge becoming less reliable

Say

"Climate change is not affecting everyone equally. For First Nations, Métis, and Inuit communities in Alberta and across Canada, climate change is disrupting systems that communities have relied on for generations."

"Ice roads. Many remote northern communities — including First Nations communities in northern Alberta — depend on roads built on frozen lakes and rivers to access supplies, medical care, and family in winter. As winters warm, the ice freezes later and thaws earlier. Ice roads are open for fewer weeks each year. Some no longer form at all in some years. Communities face increased costs and supply challenges."

"Traditional harvesting seasons. Indigenous communities have practised sustainable harvesting of plants, fish, animals, and medicines according to seasonal cycles stable for thousands of years. Climate change is shifting those cycles. Fish spawning times are shifting. Migratory animals arrive at different times than traditional knowledge predicts. Traditional hunting and trapping seasons are no longer aligned with actual animal movements."

"Medicinal and sacred plants. Many plants used in traditional medicine and spiritual practice are sensitive to specific temperature and moisture. Some species are shifting northward, out of traditional territories. The knowledge of where and when to harvest — passed down through generations — is becoming less reliable."

Cue — ★ Write + ⊡ Think

Section 4 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Alberta temperature rise since 1950: Approximately 1.5°C.
  2. Glaciers and rivers: Mountain glaciers release meltwater through summer, keeping rivers flowing even when rainfall is scarce. Glaciers are shrinking → initially may increase summer flow as they melt faster, but long-term river flow will decrease as glaciers become too small. Communities and farms dependent on glacier-fed rivers will face water shortages.
  3. Intense rainfall AND more drought: Warmer atmosphere holds more moisture → when it rains, rainfall is more intense. But between rain events, higher temperatures dry the ground out faster. Both effects happen — more extreme rain events AND drier soil between them.
  4. Specific FNMI impact + mechanism: Accept any one from ice roads (warm winters = ice forms later and melts earlier → fewer weeks of access → increased costs, supply and health access challenges), harvesting seasons (species shifting → traditional knowledge of timing no longer accurate → disruption to food security and practice), or sacred/medicinal plants (shifting northward out of traditional territory → less accessible → traditional knowledge of location/timing less reliable → cultural and health impacts).
  5. Longer seasons for farmers — evaluate: Partly true: longer growing season does allow some new crops; some areas that were marginal become viable. What it leaves out: heat and drought stress on existing crops; pest and disease ranges expanding northward; water supply from glaciers declining; adaptation costs; not all farmers benefit equally; southern Alberta may face more challenges than gains.

Beat 5.2 — Climate Models as Computational Artifacts

CS Connection — Climate Models as Abstractions

Climate models are computational artifacts and abstractions — the same principle as the particle model (Matter) and force diagrams (Forces). The model keeps the essential relationships (CO₂, ocean temperature, ice cover, solar input) and strips out complexity (individual trees, waves, clouds). Connect explicitly to prior units if students have covered them.

Key CS thread: scientists write code representing physical laws, run the model forward in time, compare output to observed data. When model output matches real-world measurements, confidence in the model increases. Then use the model to project "what if" scenarios.

Show

Board: Climate model = program that simulates Earth's climate system
List the variables the model includes: CO₂ levels · ocean temperature/circulation · ice/snow cover · solar output · atmospheric circulation.
Then: "The model simplifies this detail to be computationally workable — not every tree, wave, or cloud is simulated."

Say

"Climate scientists build computer models — programs that simulate how Earth's climate works. These are computational artifacts: tools created through programming to solve a specific problem."

"A climate model is an abstraction. It represents the key variables: CO₂ levels, ocean temperature and circulation, ice and snow cover — albedo — solar output, atmospheric circulation. And it simplifies — not every tree, every wave, or every cloud is simulated."

"This is the same principle as a force diagram in physics: keep the essential relationships, remove the unnecessary detail. The simplification is what makes it useful."

"Scientists run the model, compare output to real data, adjust, repeat. When a model's predictions match real-world measurements, confidence in the model increases. Then they use it to project: what happens if emissions stay high? What if they drop sharply by 2030?"

"Blanks: CO₂ [levels] · ocean [temperature] · solar [output] · model [simplifies] this detail."

Beat 5.4 — Traditional Ecological Knowledge as Scientific Data

Pacing Flag — TEK Deserves Its Own Lesson Beat

TEK is not a cultural add-on to this section — it is a core content concept about what counts as scientific evidence and why multiple lines of evidence strengthen a conclusion. The Bow River ice break-up example is specific enough to use as a teaching case. The goal: students understand that TEK is a valid, long-term, empirically grounded data source — not separate from science but part of it.

Show

Board: TEK = systematic observations accumulated over generations — often the longest record of local ecological conditions available.
Bow River case study: Elder oral records vs. instrument records — ice break-up now ~2 weeks earlier than 40 years ago.

Say

"Traditional Ecological Knowledge — TEK — refers to detailed observations of local environments accumulated by Indigenous peoples over many generations. In some cases, hundreds to thousands of years."

"TEK is not folklore. It is a systematic body of knowledge built through careful, long-term observation of the same environments, passed down and tested across generations."

"Why is it scientifically valuable? It is often the longest long-term record of local ecological conditions — longer than any instrument record. It captures baseline variation — what 'normal' looked like before instruments were deployed. It records species relationships and seasonal patterns at a fine local detail that satellites can't match. And it was accumulated by people who depended on its accuracy for food and survival — observation quality was maintained by necessity."

"The Bow River example: Elders from communities along the Bow River have documented spring ice break-up through oral tradition going back generations. Comparing Elder records with instrument records shows ice break-up now occurs approximately 2 weeks earlier than 40 years ago. This is a valid data set — consistent, long-term, locally verified observations of the same annual event."

"And the collaborative model: scientists bring instruments and analytical tools. Knowledge keepers bring observational records and deep local ecological understanding. Together they can detect changes neither could access alone."

Section 5 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Three measuring tools: Any three from: weather stations (temperature, precipitation, wind over decades) · satellites (ice extent, sea level, temperature from orbit, remote areas) · ice cores (past CO₂ levels and temperatures via trapped air bubbles — proxy data) · tree rings (wide/narrow rings record past growing conditions — proxy data) · ocean buoys (ocean temperature, acidity, heat absorption).
  2. Proxy data: Data that reconstructs past conditions from physical evidence rather than directly measuring them. Example: ice core air bubbles reconstruct past CO₂ levels; tree ring widths reconstruct past growing conditions.
  3. Climate model as abstraction: A climate model is a computer program that simulates Earth's climate system. It is an abstraction because it keeps the essential variables (CO₂, temperature, ice cover, solar input) while simplifying or leaving out complexity that can't be computationally modelled (individual trees, waves, clouds). Like all abstractions, it is useful precisely because it is simplified.
  4. TEK as valid scientific data: TEK is accumulated through systematic, long-term observation of the same environments over generations — often centuries or millennia. It is tested and refined across generations by people who depended on its accuracy for survival. It provides the longest locally specific data record available — before any instrument was deployed. It is empirically grounded, not anecdote.
  5. Two sources together = stronger conclusion: 25 years of satellite data captures recent trend but may not have enough length to separate signal from noise confidently. 80 years of Elder records provides a longer baseline and local specificity. Together: longer time series (signal vs. noise — more data = more confident trend identification), two independent lines of evidence both pointing the same direction (multiple lines of evidence strengthen the conclusion beyond what one source can do alone).
