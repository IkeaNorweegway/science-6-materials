## Unit Overview for Teachers

**Tier 1 unit — 5–6 weeks, highest instructional priority.**

Forces is the most diagram-intensive unit in Grade 6 Science. Every concept must be visualised — force arrows, internal force diagrams, deformation sequences. Students who cannot draw force diagrams will struggle on assessment. Prioritise visual practice and whiteboard time throughout.

**The central misconception to watch all unit:** action-reaction forces cancel each other out. This error is persistent even after direct instruction. The diagram with forces on *separate* objects is the critical intervention — return to it as often as needed.

## CS Integration

Force diagrams are abstractions: they keep only the forces acting on an object and strip away all other detail. This is the same principle as abstraction in CS. The connection appears explicitly in Section 1 (Beat 6) and should be revisited when students encounter the workbook CS questions.

## Misconception Map

| Misconception | When it appears | How to address |
|---|---|---|
| "Net force = add all the numbers" | Lesson 1 | Opposite directions cancel — worked example with 20 N and 8 N |
| "Friction isn't a force — it just slows things" | Lesson 2 | Spring scale demo — friction has magnitude and direction |
| "Plastic" means the material plastic | Lesson 4 | Etymology: plastikos = able to be shaped |
| "Heavier object pushes harder in a collision" | Lesson 5 | Spring scale between two students — same reading regardless of strength |
| "Action-reaction cancel, so nothing moves" | Lesson 5 | Two separate circles — forces on different objects cannot cancel |
