## Unit Overview for Teachers

**Tier 1 unit — 5–6 weeks, highest instructional priority.**

The photosynthesis misconception — plants get food from soil — is the most persistent and corrosive error in this unit. Students who leave with it intact will misunderstand food webs, ecosystems, and the relationship between producers and consumers. Address it explicitly in Lesson 1, return to it in Lesson 3, and check it on every assessment.

**The second major misconception:** decomposers are not "really" part of the ecosystem. This is less dramatic but leads students to think of ecosystems as collections rather than systems. Address it early (Lesson 1) and return to it in the trophic cascade case study.

## CS Integration

Lesson 6–7: students write if/then conditional logic to model ecosystem relationships, mirroring how ecosystems cascade when one variable changes. The Scratch simulation is an abstraction — it keeps essential logic and removes biological complexity. Connect to the particle model abstraction from Matter unit if students have done it.

## Misconception Map

| Misconception | When it appears | How to address |
|---|---|---|
| "Plants get food from the soil" | Lessons 1–2, returns throughout | Van Helmont tree experiment; where did the 1000 kg come from? |
| "Decomposers are just garbage collectors — not part of the ecosystem" | Lesson 1 | Remove decomposers: what happens to nutrient cycle? |
| "Photosynthesis and respiration cancel out" | Lesson 2 | Earth's 21% oxygen would not exist if they did |
| "Food chains show how ecosystems actually work" | Lesson 3 | Food webs show real complexity; chains are simplifications |
